## 1. Developmental sleep arc — "REM builds, deep sleep prunes"

**My framing.** The maintenance system isn't identical at every age, and where it changes tells you what it's for. Before birth the brain runs almost entirely on REM-like active sleep; that proportion falls across the lifespan. REM builds/wires; slow-wave sleep prunes.

**Numbers (verified).** Active (REM) sleep peaks ~80% around 30 weeks' gestation → ~58% by ~38 weeks → ~50% at term → ~25% by age 3, then roughly stable.

**Mechanistic links (both are leading hypotheses, not settled fact).**
- REM → synaptogenesis: the **ontogenetic hypothesis** (Roffwarg, Muzio & Dement, 1966) — infant REM supplies endogenous neural stimulation that drives circuit development when there's little waking experience to do it.
- Deep/slow-wave sleep → pruning/downscaling: the **Synaptic Homeostasis Hypothesis** (Tononi & Cirelli, 2003/2006). Developmental-pruning tie is strongest in adolescence — SWA falls ~40% in parallel with synaptic pruning (Feinberg, 1982; Feinberg & Campbell).
- *Nuance:* SHY downscaling is a nightly, all-ages renormalization; the clean "deep sleep = developmental pruning" mapping is really the adolescence story.

**Fetal "kicking" = active-sleep motor output.** My idea: what people read as "baby awake and kicking" is largely active-sleep movement — the pons not suppressing the outgoing motor signal (RBD-like).
- *Refinement (Blumberg, 2015):* infant active sleep DOES have background atonia; the movements are discrete **myoclonic twitches** — actively generated brainstem events that break through and produce clean reafferent feedback that helps wire the sensorimotor map. So it's a *feature* (purposeful "motor babble"), not a failure of suppression — a better story than the RBD analogy, and it ties directly to the ontogenetic/endogenous-stimulation idea.

**Status / use.** Strong, teachable framework; label the two mechanistic links as hypotheses. → Feeds **Ch6 Sleep** (architecture arc, compact) and **Ch10 Lifespan** (developmental cognition, full depth).

## 2. Cognitive aging — Gf→Gc, novelty, and "use it or lose it"

**My position (developed across discussion).**
- It's **fluid** intelligence (Gf) that declines; **crystallized** (Gc) rises. We *shift*, we don't simply lose.
- The *first* encounter with anything demands Gf; every encounter after starts from Gc. Truly novel tasks are rarely met after ~25 — the **reminiscence bump (~15–25)** marks where novelty density actually is; adult "expertise" (even mathematicians) is mostly repackaging known machinery, not from-scratch reasoning.
- **Integrated model:** senescence (shaped by life-history evolution) sets the decline *rate*; the metabolic expense of neural tissue means unneeded/unused functions aren't maintained, so use-it-or-lose-it *compounds* it. Compensatory activation in older brains reflects accumulated strategy/scaffolding.

**Honest evidence assessment.**
- Solid: Gf declines from the ~20s; Gc maintained into the 70s–80s (Salthouse; Cattell–Horn). This is essentially Ackerman's **PPIK** (process→knowledge shift).
- Supports me: reminiscence-bump *novelty/stability* account locates peak novelty at ~15–25 (a leading explanation; the cultural *life-script* account competes). "No adult is a heavy raw-Gf user" is substantially right — most demanding work is Gc retrieval.
- Pushback that holds: (a) **processing speed** — the pacemaker of much Gf decline — falls from the 20s despite being impossible *not* to use; (b) cognitively demanding careers slow the decline *rate* but don't preserve *peak capacity* (reserve, not prevention); (c) far transfer of training to Gf is weak.
- Direction caveat on compensatory activation: expertise → neural *efficiency* (LESS activation); compensatory over-recruitment (HAROLD/CRUNCH) is a *strain/deficit* signature, usually read as downstream of decline, not caused by strategy accumulation. STAC "scaffolding" is the thread that partly supports the strategy angle.
- **Where it landed:** the integrated both/and (senescence rate + metabolic pruning) is legitimate and mainstream-compatible. Disagreement narrows to *proportion*. Open empirical lever: do individual differences in continued novel-reasoning engagement predict Gf-decline *rate* strongly (use-model) or modestly (reserve-modulator)? Current evidence: modest → use is a real *modulator*; biology sets the dominant *rate*.

**Status / use.** Live scholarly position — NOT settled textbook fact. → Could inform **Ch9 (Thinking, Language & Intelligence** — Gf/Gc, cognitive aging) and **Ch10 (Lifespan)**. Must be presented as a hypothesis alongside the biological-pacemaker account.

## 3. Chessmaster autopsy anecdote

**Heard.** A chess master with a neurodegenerative illness could see fewer moves ahead; on autopsy the least-damaged regions were the chess-related ones, the rest "Swiss cheese." Read as use-protects-tissue.

**Status.** Specific case *unverified* — couldn't locate a primary source; likely secondhand/embellished. Real adjacent phenomena: expert skills often preserved late into dementia; **cognitive reserve** (documented chess players functioning despite heavy Alzheimer's pathology). Inference caveats: (1) disease-topography confound — degenerative diseases spare regions for reasons intrinsic to their spatial pattern; (2) "spared tissue → retained skill" is more parsimonious than "use → spared tissue." → If a primary source turns up, the real test is region-by-region preservation vs. that disease's *expected* topography.

## 4. Sensation & Perception — should we drop the "sensation" definition?

**My proposal.** Stop teaching "sensation" as a separate stage — after first exposure there's no truly novel sensation; the cortex always brings predictions (predictive coding).

**Assessment.** Real insight: the *clean* sensation→perception dichotomy is false; it's one predictive process (matches the book's Seth / Rao & Ballard spine).

**Counter.** Predictive coding is Bayesian = prior × likelihood; **sensation is the likelihood/evidence term.** Delete it and the framework breaks — no evidence term → no prediction error → nothing to correct. Also transduction and psychophysics (phototransduction, cochlear mechanics, Weber's law, adaptation) are lawful and largely prediction-independent at the receptor. And the naive dichotomy is the *foil* the misconception-then-correction structure needs.

**Resolution.** Don't delete — *define* sensation, then immediately show it's inseparably fused with prediction (one inferential process, two terms). → Feeds **Ch4 (S&P framing)** and **Ch5**.

## 5. Human sleep is evolutionarily unusual (supports the Ch6 voice line)

Human sleep is the **shortest and most REM-dense of any primate** (~25% REM vs <5% in several monkeys/lemurs). The **sleep intensity hypothesis** (Samson & Nunn, 2015) ties this to coming down from the trees to sleep on the ground, in groups, near fire. → Evidence behind the Ch6 `[VOICE]` line that natural selection tuned sleep differently across species — "everybody needs exactly 8 hours" is the wrong frame.
