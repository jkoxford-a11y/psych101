## Where This Fits

You've now covered how we sense the world (Ch. 4), how we learn (Ch. 6), how we think (Ch. 8), how our personalities form (Ch. 11), and what motivates us (Ch. 12). This chapter examines one of the most direct connections between psychology and physical health: stress. The stress response is fundamentally psychological — it depends not on what happens to you but on how you appraise it. That appraisal process, and what it does to the body over time, is what health psychology studies.

## Misconception Opener: "The Stress Response Is a Design Flaw"

Students often come to this chapter convinced that stress is simply bad — a malfunction, a sign that something has gone wrong. The goal, by this view, would be to eliminate stress entirely.

Robert Sapolsky (2004), a neuroendocrinologist who has spent decades studying stress in baboons and humans, offers a different diagnosis. When a zebra is being chased across the savanna by a lion, its stress response is magnificent: heart rate and blood pressure surge, blood glucose floods the muscles, digestion and reproduction shut down, immune surveillance shifts, and the animal burns through every available resource to outrun the threat. Thirty minutes later, either the zebra is dead or it's grazing again, physiologically returned to baseline.

The design works flawlessly — for short-term physical emergencies. The problem, Sapolsky argues, is that humans activate this same response for threats that aren't over in thirty minutes: a difficult boss, a strained relationship, financial insecurity, an unresolved conflict. The stress response becomes pathological not because it is broken but because the system was never designed to run continuously. The lion never goes away.

That reframe — from "stress is harmful" to "chronic, unresolvable activation of an otherwise adaptive system is harmful" — is the organizing principle of this chapter.

## Section 1: What Is Stress? Stressors, Appraisal, and the Biopsychosocial Model

### Health Psychology and the Biopsychosocial Model

For most of the twentieth century, medicine operated on a **biomedical model**: illness is caused by biological factors such as pathogens, genetic mutations, or structural abnormalities, and treatment means correcting those biological causes. This model succeeded brilliantly at reducing infectious disease. But it struggled with the chronic conditions that now dominate public health — heart disease, diabetes, cancer — because those conditions are not explained by biology alone.

**Health psychology** is the field devoted to understanding how psychological and social factors influence physical health. It rests on a different framework: the **biopsychosocial model**, which holds that biology, psychology, and social context together determine who gets sick, how severely, and how quickly they recover (Hooker & Pressman, 2026; Taylor, 2012). By this model, psychological factors like stress are not merely correlates of illness — they are causal mechanisms that "get under the skin" and alter physiology.

One field within health psychology, **psychoneuroimmunology (PNI)**, studies how the nervous system, endocrine system, and immune system communicate with each other — and how psychological states disrupt or support those connections. PNI is the mechanistic bridge between "I'm stressed" and "I got sick."

### What Is a Stressor?

The word *stress* entered psychology from structural engineering, where it described force applied to a material. Hans Selye adapted it for biology after a series of serendipitous experiments in the 1930s. He was investigating the effects of an ovarian hormone extract on rats and noticed that almost any injected substance — including saline solution — produced the same cluster of symptoms: stomach ulcers, enlarged adrenal glands, shrunken immune tissue. Selye realized the animal's body was responding not to the hormone but to the aversive experience of being handled and injected. He coined the term **stressor** to describe any stimulus that triggers the body's stress response (Selye, 1946).

Stressors vary widely in magnitude and duration:

- **Catastrophes** are sudden, overwhelming events affecting many people — natural disasters, terrorist attacks, pandemics. These can produce lasting psychological and physiological effects even in people who are not directly harmed.
- **Major life events** include significant changes and losses: a divorce, a serious illness, job loss, or the death of a close family member. Holmes and Rahe (1967) developed one of the earliest instruments to quantify cumulative life-change stress; their research showed that people with high life-change scores in a given year were more likely to fall ill in the year that followed.
- **Daily hassles** are minor but chronic irritants — traffic, interpersonal friction, running late, small conflicts. These may seem trivial, but research shows that daily hassles raise blood pressure, alter stress hormones, and suppress immune function (DeLongis, Folkman, & Lazarus, 1988). The cumulative effect of small, repeated activations is at least as important to health as major life events.

### Cognitive Appraisal: Stress Is in the Eye of the Beholder

The most important insight of Richard Lazarus and Susan Folkman's **transactional model of stress** is that stress is not a property of the event but of the relationship between the event and the person's perceived resources (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984). The same circumstance — an important deadline, a public presentation, a competitive exam — will produce physiological stress in one person and motivated excitement in another. What varies is the appraisal.

The model proposes two sequential appraisal processes:

**Primary appraisal** asks: *Is this relevant to my well-being, and if so, is it a threat, a harm already done, or a challenge I might be able to master?* Events appraised as irrelevant produce no stress response. Events appraised as threatening activate the physiological alarm.

**Secondary appraisal** asks: *Do I have the resources — physical, psychological, social, financial — to cope with this?* An event appraised as a threat but also as manageable produces moderate stress and mobilizes problem-solving. An event appraised as a threat with no available coping resources produces the most damaging stress.

This framework has an important practical implication: changing how a person appraises a stressor — reframing a threat as a challenge, or identifying previously unrecognized resources — can reduce physiological stress activation even when the external situation doesn't change. This is one mechanism through which cognitive-behavioral interventions reduce stress-related health problems.

> **Think About It:** Think of a stressor in your own life. What was your primary appraisal (threat, harm, or challenge)? What resources did you bring to it in secondary appraisal? Did your assessment change how your body felt?

## Section 2: Stress in the Body — Physiology, Wear, and Immunity

### Two Stress Axes: Fast and Slow

When you perceive a threat, two biological pathways activate in parallel, operating at different speeds and with different consequences.

The **SAM axis** (sympathetic-adrenal-medullary system) is the fast pathway. The sympathetic nervous system (covered in Ch. 3) activates within seconds, triggering the adrenal medulla to release **epinephrine** (adrenaline) and **norepinephrine** into the bloodstream. Heart rate and blood pressure surge, blood glucose rises to fuel muscles, digestion slows, and pupils dilate. This is the classic **fight-or-flight response** — Walter Cannon's term for the mobilization of the body's emergency resources. It is fast, powerful, and designed for situations requiring immediate physical action.

The **HPA axis** (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal system) is the slower, more sustained pathway. The hypothalamus signals the pituitary gland to release ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone), which travels through the bloodstream to the adrenal cortex and stimulates the release of **cortisol**. Cortisol peaks roughly 20–30 minutes after stress onset. It prolongs the mobilization of energy reserves, modulates the immune system, and prepares the body for sustained effort. But it also suppresses processes that are metabolically expensive and nonessential during an emergency: growth, reproduction, digestion, and, crucially, immune surveillance.

Under normal circumstances, once the stressor resolves, cortisol levels fall and baseline physiology is restored. The problem arises when the stressor doesn't resolve.

### The General Adaptation Syndrome

Selye (1946) proposed that the body's response to prolonged stress follows a predictable three-stage pattern he called the **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)**:

1. **Alarm stage:** Initial mobilization. The SAM and HPA axes activate; the organism is fully aroused and energetically primed to address the threat. Resistance to illness may temporarily dip as resources are redirected.
2. **Resistance stage:** Sustained mobilization. The organism appears to cope successfully, but the physiological cost is ongoing. HPA axis activity remains elevated; cortisol continues to suppress immune function even as the organism functions normally from the outside.
3. **Exhaustion stage:** Resources depleted. If the stressor continues long enough, the organism's ability to maintain resistance collapses. Vulnerability to illness, breakdown, and organ damage increases dramatically.

The GAS captures something important: chronic stress doesn't kill you dramatically; it exhausts you incrementally, eroding the systems that would otherwise protect you.

### Allostatic Load: The Price of Adaptation

Building on Selye, Bruce McEwen introduced the concept of **allostatic load** — the cumulative physiological cost of repeated or chronic stress activation (McEwen, 1998). Allostasis refers to the body's ability to maintain stability through change; allostatic load is what accumulates when those adaptive changes are required too frequently, for too long, or without adequate recovery.

High allostatic load is associated with accelerated cardiovascular disease, impaired immune function, hippocampal damage (which partially explains stress-related memory problems), metabolic dysregulation, and elevated risk for a broad range of chronic conditions. Allostatic load is highest in populations experiencing chronic psychosocial stressors — poverty, discrimination, caregiving demands, neighborhood violence — making stress physiology directly relevant to health disparities.

### Classic Study: The Cold Virus Experiment

The most vivid direct evidence that psychological stress suppresses immune function comes from a series of studies by Sheldon Cohen and colleagues. In one landmark experiment, participants were interviewed about their stress levels and then given nasal drops containing live cold virus (Cohen, Tyrrell, & Smith, 1991). Researchers then monitored participants over several days for objective signs of illness — mucus production, viral cultures, self-reported symptoms.

Not everyone exposed to the virus developed a cold. Critically, participants who reported higher psychological stress before exposure were significantly more likely to develop an objective infection, even after controlling for health habits and baseline immunity. Higher stress ratings predicted higher infection rates in a clear dose-response relationship. A subsequent study found that positive affect was protective — people with more positive emotional tone at baseline were less likely to develop colds even after viral exposure (Cohen, Alper, Doyle, Treanor, & Turner, 2006).

The mechanism is HPA-axis mediated immune suppression: sustained cortisol elevation reduces the production and efficacy of immune cells, leaving the body less able to respond to pathogens. Stress doesn't make you more likely to encounter the cold virus; it makes you more likely to succumb when you do.

**→ Classic Study Walkthrough: Cohen, Tyrrell, & Smith (1991)**

*Question:* Does psychological stress causally increase susceptibility to infectious illness?

*Method:* 394 healthy adults completed questionnaires measuring current psychological stress. All participants then received nasal drops containing one of five cold viruses (or a saline control). They were quarantined in a hotel for a week and monitored daily for objective signs of infection (viral culture, mucus weight) and subjective symptoms.

*Finding:* Higher stress scores predicted higher rates of both clinical colds and objective viral infection in a dose-response pattern. The relationship held even after statistically controlling for age, sex, education, body mass, season, allergies, and baseline antibody levels.

*Significance:* This study is one of the strongest demonstrations of psychological stress as a causal factor in immune vulnerability in otherwise healthy adults. It is not that stressed people engage in more illness-promoting behaviors (though that is also true). Stress itself suppresses immune defense.

### Do Not Confuse: Acute Stress and Chronic Stress

| | Acute stress | Chronic stress |
|---|---|---|
| **Duration** | Minutes to hours; clearly resolves | Days, months, years; no clear resolution |
| **Adaptive value** | High — energizes response to immediate threat | Low — sustains costly activation without benefit |
| **SAM/HPA activation** | Brief, followed by full recovery | Sustained; recovery incomplete |
| **Immune effect** | Mild short-term enhancement possible; rapidly normalizes | Prolonged suppression; PNI research consistently shows impaired function |
| **Health outcome** | Minimal to none; may briefly strengthen immune priming | Associated with cardiovascular disease, metabolic disorders, accelerated aging |
| **Sapolsky's zebra analogy** | Zebra running from lion | Human worrying about job security for five years |

Acute stress is not the problem; chronic, unresolvable stress is. Interventions that convert a perceived chronic stressor into a manageable, bounded challenge (through coping resources, social support, or appraisal retraining) reduce allostatic load even when the stressor itself persists.

## Section 3: Who Handles Stress Better? Individual Differences in Reactivity

### Hardiness

Suzanne Kobasa studied a population with objectively high stress loads — executives in a telecommunications company undergoing restructuring — and asked why some showed stress-related illness while others, facing the same circumstances, remained healthy (Kobasa, 1979). The people who stayed well despite high stress differed on three personality traits, which she grouped under the label **hardiness**:

- **Commitment:** Engaging deeply with work, relationships, and activities rather than withdrawing — a sense that what they were doing mattered.
- **Control:** Believing that they could influence outcomes through their own choices and actions. (Notably, this is *perceived* control, not actual control — a distinction with important implications explored in Section 4.)
- **Challenge:** Viewing change and difficulty as opportunities for growth rather than as threats to security.

Kobasa's hardiness research has held up reasonably well as a predictor of stress resistance, though subsequent research has found that the control component accounts for most of the protective effect, and that hardiness may overlap substantially with the Big Five trait of neuroticism (low hardiness = high neuroticism). Hardiness is not a fixed personality trait that you either have or don't — longitudinal evidence suggests it can be cultivated through deliberate exposure to challenging experiences in which one's competence is successfully applied.

### Type A Behavior: What Actually Matters

In the late 1950s, two cardiologists noticed that their heart disease patients showed a distinctive behavioral pattern that their other patients did not: chronic time urgency, competitiveness, hostility, and an inability to disengage from work. Meyer Friedman and Ray Rosenman (1959) formalized this as **Type A behavior pattern** and found it was associated with approximately double the risk of coronary heart disease compared with the more relaxed **Type B** pattern.

Subsequent research refined the picture substantially. The components of Type A are not equally harmful. Time urgency and competitiveness appear to carry relatively little cardiovascular risk on their own. It is **hostility** — quick anger, cynicism about others' motives, and the physiological arousal that accompanies those states — that drives the elevated heart disease risk (Iribarren et al., 2000; Miller, Smith, Turner, Guijarro, & Hallet, 1996). Hostile individuals also tend to have smaller and less supportive social networks, depriving them of one of the strongest health-protective factors known (see Section 4).

The practical implication: "working harder and faster" does not inherently harm health. Habitually responding to frustration with anger and cynicism does.

### Orchid and Dandelion: Biological Sensitivity to Context

Not everyone has the same baseline reactivity to stressors, and the differences are not randomly distributed. Thomas Boyce and Bruce Ellis (2005) proposed what they call **biological sensitivity to context**: individuals differ in how strongly their stress-response systems react to environmental inputs, and these differences are partly heritable.

The **dandelion** child has a relatively robust, less reactive stress-response system. This child does reasonably well across a wide range of environments — supportive or not, enriched or deprived. The **orchid** child has a highly reactive stress-response system (higher HPA-axis responsivity, elevated cortisol in novel or challenging situations, heightened sympathetic arousal). In adversive or chronically stressful environments, orchid children show higher rates of illness, emotional difficulties, and behavioral problems. In highly supportive, enriching environments, the same heightened reactivity produces the best outcomes of any group — greater benefit from good instruction, deeper social bonds, and in some cases stronger immune priming.

This is a departure from the traditional view that stress reactivity is simply a vulnerability. High biological sensitivity is not a flaw but a conditional adaptation — the most responsive organisms to social input, for better and for worse. The orchid brain/cortisol framing connects back to the autonomic nervous system content in Ch. 3: the same HPA axis whose chronic activation harms health also mediates learning from experience, bonding, and the benefits of supportive social environments.

## Section 4: Coping, Social Support, and Health Promotion

### Coping: Problem-Focused and Emotion-Focused

Lazarus and Folkman (1984) distinguished two broad families of coping strategies, defined by their target:

**Problem-focused coping** directly addresses the stressor. You identify what needs to change, develop a plan, and take action. If you're failing a class, problem-focused coping involves going to office hours, forming a study group, reorganizing your schedule, and requesting an extension. Problem-focused coping is generally more effective than emotion-focused coping when the stressor is controllable — when action can actually change the situation.

**Emotion-focused coping** regulates the emotional response to the stressor without directly addressing it. This includes distraction (watching a film to take your mind off anxiety), emotional expression (talking to a friend about how overwhelmed you feel), reappraisal (reminding yourself that the situation is temporary and manageable), and acceptance (acknowledging what you cannot control). Emotion-focused coping is often dismissed as avoidance, but research shows it is the more effective strategy for uncontrollable stressors — situations where action would accomplish nothing or make things worse. Grief is the canonical example: the loss of a loved one cannot be fixed, but the emotional response must be managed.

The key is match between stressor and strategy. Applying problem-focused coping to an uncontrollable stressor produces exhausting, futile effort. Applying emotion-focused coping to a controllable stressor avoids necessary action. Skilled copers — and this can be learned — accurately assess whether a situation is changeable and select their strategy accordingly.

### The Health Protection of Social Relationships

One of the most robust findings in health psychology is that social isolation is dangerous. Julianne Holt-Lunstad and colleagues conducted a meta-analysis of 148 studies covering more than 300,000 participants across diverse populations and found that people with adequate social relationships had a 50% greater likelihood of survival over the follow-up period compared with people who were socially isolated or lonely (Holt-Lunstad, Smith, & Layton, 2010). The effect size is comparable to not smoking, and larger than physical activity, obesity, or heavy alcohol consumption as mortality risk factors. Social isolation kills at a rate similar to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day.

Why? Social relationships operate through multiple mechanisms. The **buffering hypothesis** proposes that social support does not necessarily improve well-being in low-stress conditions — it specifically protects against the health costs of high stress. A network of people who can provide emotional support (someone who listens), informational support (advice on how to handle the situation), tangible support (practical help), and appraisal support (feedback about whether your reading of a situation is accurate) all reduce the physiological burden of stress.

Shelley Taylor and colleagues (Taylor et al., 2000) proposed a complementary evolutionary account: that under threat, especially for female mammals, the dominant response pattern is not fight-or-flight but **tend-and-befriend** — affiliating with others and seeking group protection. They argue that this pattern is partly mediated by oxytocin (introduced in Ch. 3), which promotes social bonding and reduces HPA axis reactivity. The fight-or-flight framing, they note, was derived primarily from research on male subjects; the tend-and-befriend pattern may better characterize stress responses in female mammals across species.

### Positive Emotions and the Broaden-and-Build Theory

Barbara Fredrickson's **broaden-and-build theory** proposes that positive emotions serve a different evolutionary function than negative emotions (Fredrickson, 2001). Where negative emotions narrow attention and behavior to address an immediate threat — fear produces avoidance, anger produces confrontation — positive emotions broaden the repertoire of thoughts and actions available, and over time that broadened state builds durable psychological, social, cognitive, and physical resources.

The health implications are substantial. Positive affect is associated with lower basal cortisol, stronger immune response, reduced cardiovascular reactivity, faster wound healing, and — most strikingly — significantly longer lifespan across multiple prospective studies (Pressman & Cohen, 2005). This is not simply a confound of baseline health: studies controlling for initial health status still find positive emotions prospectively predict longevity.

Positive emotions also function as stress buffers. In the aftermath of challenging events, the ability to find moments of genuine positive emotion — humor, gratitude, curiosity, connection — predicts faster physiological recovery from stress activation. Fredrickson calls this the **undoing effect**: positive emotions appear to accelerate the return of the cardiovascular and HPA systems to baseline after stress arousal.

This is not an argument for toxic positivity — suppressing or ignoring negative emotion. It is an argument that positive and negative affect co-occur and that the capacity to access genuine positive emotional states during difficulty is a health-protective resource.

### Control, Self-Efficacy, and What They Do to Stress Physiology

A recurring theme in health psychology research is the disproportionate harm of *uncontrollable* stressors relative to stressors of equivalent intensity that the person can act on. In classic studies, rats exposed to shocks they could not avoid showed massive immune suppression; rats exposed to shocks of the same intensity that they could terminate by pressing a lever did not (Laudenslager et al., 1983, as reviewed in Sapolsky, 2004). The helplessness, not the shock, was making them sick.

In humans, **perceived control** — the belief that you can influence outcomes — predicts lower cortisol reactivity, better immune function, and better performance under stress, even when actual control is limited or absent. Langer and Rodin's (1976) study of nursing home residents — where some residents were given control over small choices (which movie to watch, when volunteers visited) and others were not — found that the control group showed measurably better health and lived significantly longer than matched residents whose choices were made for them.

Closely related is **self-efficacy** — Albert Bandura's term for confidence in one's ability to execute specific behaviors required to produce a desired outcome (introduced in Ch. 6 in the context of Bandura's observational learning research). High self-efficacy predicts better coping with health-relevant stressors, greater adherence to medical recommendations, and better outcomes following illness. The mechanism appears to be through appraisal: people with high self-efficacy are more likely to appraise stressors as challenges rather than threats, engaging the HPA axis in a lower-cost way.

### AI Connection: Stress Detection and the Appraisal Gap

Psychological stress has become an active target for AI-assisted health monitoring. Wearable devices can track heart rate variability, skin conductance, and sleep disruption — all physiological correlates of SAM and HPA axis activation. Trained algorithms can classify these signals with reasonable accuracy as stress-elevated or stress-baseline states.

What these systems detect is the body's alarm signal — activation of the fight-or-flight pathway. This is the biological side of Lazarus and Folkman's (1984) two-stage appraisal model. What they cannot detect is the psychological side: whether this activation reflects a manageable challenge or an overwhelming threat, whether the person has coping resources available, or whether the stress will resolve or become chronic.

Secondary appraisal — the assessment of your own resources relative to a perceived threat — is a deeply personal act of meaning-making. It requires knowing your goals (what matters to you), your history (what you've coped with before), your social network (who you can call), and your embodied sense of your own capacity in this domain. An algorithm reading your heart rate has access to none of this. It can flag the alarm but cannot tell you whether to take shelter or press forward.

This is not a trivial limitation. The distinction between challenge and threat appraisal produces different cortisol profiles even when the physiological arousal level is similar. Athletes and performers often show elevated heart rates and strong HPA activation before competition — and they're healthier for it. The stress response is the same; the appraisal differs. A stress-detection AI that cannot distinguish a challenge response from a threat response cannot tell you whether to intervene or trust the process.

The breakdown point here is the same as in earlier chapters: AI systems can approximate the *surface* of psychological constructs — the measurable physiological or behavioral signatures — but not the meaning those constructs have for a situated, embodied agent with a life history and stakes in the outcome. Allostatic load accumulates because the body is repeatedly mobilized for threats it cannot resolve. An AI system faces no such stakes. It can model stress physiology but it cannot be stressed.

## Chapter Summary

Stress is not a design flaw but a feature of a system designed for short-term emergencies. The zebra-to-human contrast (Sapolsky, 2004) captures the central problem: the same physiological stress response that brilliantly mobilizes an organism against a predator becomes pathological when chronically activated by unresolvable psychological threats.

Whether a stimulus becomes a stressor depends on cognitive appraisal (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984). Primary appraisal assesses threat; secondary appraisal assesses coping resources. Interventions that target appraisal can reduce physiological stress even without changing external circumstances.

Stress activates two parallel biological pathways: the fast SAM axis (epinephrine, fight-or-flight) and the slower HPA axis (cortisol, sustained mobilization). Selye's (1946) General Adaptation Syndrome — alarm, resistance, exhaustion — describes the trajectory of prolonged stress. McEwen's (1998) allostatic load framework captures the cumulative physiological cost of repeated activation.

Cohen et al. (1991) demonstrated that psychological stress directly suppresses immune function, increasing susceptibility to infection. Chronic stress is associated with cardiovascular disease, metabolic dysregulation, and accelerated aging through allostatic load.

People differ in stress reactivity. Kobasa's (1979) hardiness research identified commitment, control, and challenge as protective factors. Friedman and Rosenman's (1959) Type A research showed hostility — not time urgency — as the cardiovascular risk component. Boyce and Ellis's (2005) orchid/dandelion model reframes high reactivity as differential susceptibility to context rather than pure vulnerability.

Coping strategies divide into problem-focused (acting on the stressor) and emotion-focused (managing the emotional response), with effectiveness depending on whether the stressor is controllable. Social relationships are among the strongest predictors of long-term health and survival (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2010). Positive emotions buffer stress and accelerate physiological recovery (Fredrickson, 2001). Perceived control reduces HPA reactivity even when actual control is limited.

## Key Terms

**Allostatic load** — The cumulative physiological wear produced by repeated or chronic stress activation (McEwen, 1998).

**Biopsychosocial model** — The framework holding that biological, psychological, and social factors together determine health outcomes, replacing the purely biological biomedical model.

**Broaden-and-build theory** — Fredrickson's (2001) proposal that positive emotions expand attention and behavioral repertoires, building durable psychological, social, and physical resources over time.

**Cortisol** — A glucocorticoid hormone released by the adrenal cortex via HPA axis activation during stress; mobilizes energy reserves but suppresses immune function with sustained exposure.

**Daily hassles** — Minor but chronic irritants that contribute substantially to allostatic load through repeated, frequent stress-response activation.

**Emotion-focused coping** — Coping strategies that regulate the emotional response to a stressor without directly addressing its source; most effective for uncontrollable stressors.

**Fight-or-flight response** — Cannon's term for sympathetic nervous system activation that mobilizes the body's emergency resources through the SAM axis.

**General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)** — Selye's (1946) three-stage model of the stress response: alarm, resistance, and exhaustion.

**Hardiness** — Kobasa's (1979) cluster of stress-protective personality traits: commitment, control, and challenge.

**HPA axis** — The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal pathway mediating the slower, cortisol-based stress response.

**Perceived control** — The belief that one can influence outcomes through one's actions; reduces cortisol reactivity and protects health even when actual control is limited.

**Primary appraisal** — The initial evaluation of whether a stimulus is a threat, a harm, or a manageable challenge.

**Problem-focused coping** — Coping strategies that directly address the source of the stressor; most effective when the stressor is controllable.

**Psychoneuroimmunology (PNI)** — The field studying communication between the nervous, endocrine, and immune systems, and how psychological states influence immune function.

**SAM axis** — The sympathetic-adrenal-medullary pathway mediating the fast, epinephrine-based fight-or-flight response.

**Secondary appraisal** — The assessment of one's available coping resources relative to a perceived threat.

**Self-efficacy** — Confidence in one's ability to execute the behaviors needed to achieve a desired outcome (Bandura); predicts challenge rather than threat appraisal.

**Social support** — Emotional, informational, tangible, and appraisal support provided by social relationships; buffers against stress-related health costs.

**Stressor** — Any stimulus that activates the body's stress response (Selye, 1946); can be catastrophes, major life events, or daily hassles.

**Tend-and-befriend** — Taylor et al.'s (2000) proposal that under threat, especially in females, the dominant response is affiliating with others and seeking group protection rather than fighting or fleeing.

**Type A behavior** — Friedman and Rosenman's (1959) pattern of competitiveness, time urgency, and hostility; the hostility component specifically predicts elevated cardiovascular risk.

The biopsychosocial model adds that psychological factors (appraisal, coping, mood) and social factors (support networks, socioeconomic position, relationships) are equally important determinants of who gets sick and how severely. A good example: coronary heart disease risk is elevated by biological factors (cholesterol, hypertension) but *also* by psychological factors (hostility, chronic stress) and social factors (social isolation, occupational demand with low control) — none of which a purely biomedical model can accommodate.

2. Describe Lazarus and Folkman's (1984) two appraisal stages. Why does the same event — say, an upcoming exam — produce stress in one student and excitement in another?

<details><summary>Check your answer</summary>

In primary appraisal, the person evaluates whether the situation is a threat, a harm, or a manageable challenge. In secondary appraisal, they assess whether they have the resources to cope with it. Two students facing the same exam may differ in primary appraisal (one sees it as a serious threat to their grade; the other sees it as a manageable test of preparation) or in secondary appraisal (one feels prepared and confident; the other feels they haven't studied enough and lack time to catch up). The resulting physiological stress activation follows from the appraisal, not from the exam itself.

</details>

3. What is the distinction between the SAM axis and the HPA axis in the stress response? Which produces cortisol, and why does sustained cortisol elevation harm health?

<details><summary>Check your answer</summary>

The SAM axis is fast: sympathetic nervous system activation triggers the adrenal medulla to release epinephrine and norepinephrine within seconds, producing the fight-or-flight response. The HPA axis is slower: the hypothalamus signals the pituitary to release ACTH, which then stimulates the adrenal cortex to release cortisol over 20–30 minutes. Sustained cortisol elevation is harmful because cortisol suppresses immune function (reducing production and efficacy of immune cells), impairs hippocampal neurons (contributing to stress-related memory problems), promotes visceral fat accumulation, and accelerates vascular damage — all effects that are adaptive for a brief emergency but damaging when sustained.

4. Describe Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome. What happens during the exhaustion stage?

<details><summary>Check your answer</summary>

The GAS has three stages. In the alarm stage, the stress response is fully activated and the organism mobilizes its resources; resistance may briefly dip as energy is redirected. In the resistance stage, the organism sustains its response and appears to cope, but the physiological cost of maintaining arousal continues — immune suppression and hormonal dysregulation are ongoing even when the organism seems fine. In the exhaustion stage, sustained activation depletes coping resources beyond recovery: vulnerability to illness, organ damage, and breakdown increase sharply. The model explains why chronic stress harms health gradually rather than catastrophically.

<details><summary>Check your answer</summary>

Allostatic load is the cumulative physiological cost of repeated or chronic stress activation — the wear-and-tear of having the stress-response system engaged too frequently, for too long, or without adequate recovery (McEwen, 1998). Allostatic load is highest in people experiencing chronic psychosocial stressors with limited resources and control: people living in poverty, those facing persistent discrimination, primary caregivers for seriously ill family members, and people in high-demand, low-control occupations. These populations also tend to have the fewest social-support resources to buffer the effect — a double disadvantage.

7. Describe the orchid/dandelion model of biological sensitivity to context. Why is high stress reactivity not simply a vulnerability?

<details><summary>Check your answer</summary>

Boyce and Ellis (2005) proposed that individuals differ in how strongly their stress-response systems react to environmental inputs. "Dandelion" individuals have robust, lower-reactivity systems and do reasonably well across a wide range of environments. "Orchid" individuals have highly reactive systems. In adverse or chronically stressful environments, orchid individuals show worse outcomes — more illness, more emotional difficulty. But in supportive, enriching environments, orchid individuals show *better* outcomes than dandelions — greater benefit from good instruction, deeper social bonding, stronger immune priming. High reactivity is therefore conditional: it amplifies both negative and positive environmental inputs rather than producing uniformly bad outcomes.

</details>

8. How does social support protect health? What did Holt-Lunstad et al. (2010) find about social isolation as a health risk factor?

<details><summary>Check your answer</summary>

Social support buffers the physiological stress response through multiple pathways: emotional support reduces subjective distress; informational support aids coping; tangible support reduces the practical burden of stressors; and merely feeling part of a network reduces HPA axis reactivity. Holt-Lunstad et al. (2010) conducted a meta-analysis of 148 studies with over 300,000 participants and found that adequate social relationships increased the odds of survival by 50% across the follow-up period — an effect size comparable to quitting smoking and larger than obesity, physical inactivity, or heavy alcohol use. Social isolation is a major, modifiable risk factor for premature mortality.

</details>

9. What is problem-focused coping, and when is it most effective? When might emotion-focused coping be the better choice?

<details><summary>Check your answer</summary>

Problem-focused coping directly addresses the stressor through action: planning, seeking information, problem-solving. It is most effective when the stressor is controllable — when action can actually change the situation. Emotion-focused coping regulates the emotional response without directly addressing the source: distraction, emotional expression, reappraisal, acceptance. It is more effective when the stressor is uncontrollable — when nothing the person does can change the situation. The key is matching strategy to controllability: problem-focused coping applied to an uncontrollable situation produces fruitless, exhausting effort; emotion-focused coping applied to a controllable situation avoids necessary action and leaves the problem unaddressed.

</details>

10. According to Fredrickson's (2001) broaden-and-build theory, how do positive emotions protect health differently from how they simply feel pleasant?

<details><summary>Check your answer</summary>

Positive emotions broaden the momentary repertoire of thoughts and behaviors available (attention expands, problem-solving becomes more flexible, social connection is easier) and, over time, that broadened state builds durable psychological, social, and physical resources that outlast the positive moment itself. Specifically relevant to stress and health: positive emotions accelerate physiological recovery from stress arousal — the "undoing effect," in which brief positive emotion speeds the return of cardiovascular and HPA systems to baseline after stress activation. This is distinct from simply feeling good: even brief, genuine positive emotional states during or after a stressor reduce the allostatic cost of that stressor by shortening the duration of physiological mobilization.

</details>
