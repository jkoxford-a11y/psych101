## 0. Purpose

This file proposes how to integrate the current theoretical spine into the remaining chapter drafts without turning the book into generic publisher prose.

The goal is not to rewrite every chapter around a slogan. The goal is to make each chapter answer one student-facing question that advances the book's larger argument:

> Organisms receive partial, delayed, filtered input; select what matters; compress experience into usable models; predict and act before full certainty is available; and update or defend those models when errors matter.

The revision strategy should preserve Jon's instructor voice: direct, conceptually serious, example-driven, and willing to correct misconceptions without overloading first-year students.

---

## 1. Governing revision rules

### 1.1 What to add across chapters

Each target chapter should receive four spine-facing moves:

1. **A student-facing spine question near the opener.**  
   This is not a learning objective. It is the question that makes the chapter matter.

2. **A sharper Where This Fits paragraph.**  
   State which part of the master loop the chapter explains: input, attention, compression, prediction, action, updating, defense, development, social calibration, or model repair.

3. **One reusable diagram or table that students can reason from.**  
   Prefer mechanism diagrams, comparison tables, flow diagrams, and compression/update loops over decorative images.

4. **One AI-after-effort touchpoint where useful.**  
   AI should clarify the psychology concept, not decorate the chapter. Students should first predict, classify, retrieve, or explain; only then should AI be used as a study partner or contrast case.

### 1.2 What not to add

Do not make every chapter sound like a predictive-processing manifesto. Avoid these overstatements:

- Do not say the brain *is* a prediction machine as if that resolves the field.
- Do not present active inference or free-energy minimization as consensus intro-psych content.
- Do not collapse prediction error, dopamine, emotion, and stress into one mechanism.
- Do not say all errors are adaptive.
- Do not make evolutionary framing dominate chapters where the main teaching job is methodological, cognitive, social, or clinical.
- Do not use AI analogies where embodiment, motivation, development, emotion, or social ecology are the real issue.

### 1.3 Figure policy for this proposal

Figures should follow the existing figure style guide:

- one hard idea per figure;
- clean, 16:9-friendly layout;
- large labels, few labels;
- no visible figure numbers embedded in the image;
- caption carries nuance and caveats;
- alt text and attribution recorded with each final asset;
- public-sharing-safe originals preferred over decorative stock images.

Suggested filenames below use descriptive slugs, not embedded figure numbers.

### 1.4 Video policy for Gemini-generated clips

Use Gemini videos sparingly. The best clips should be short, silent or lightly captioned, and concept-first.

Default constraints:

- **Length:** 8-20 seconds.
- **Format:** 16:9, 720p or compressed 1080p.
- **Text:** avoid generated text inside the video; put labels/captions in HTML or slides instead.
- **Use:** brief loop, classroom opener, or concept transition.
- **Storage:** only keep highest-impact videos in repo; avoid large decorative clips.
- **Public sharing:** use original generated scenes only; do not ask Gemini to reproduce copyrighted textbook art, branded screenshots, or identifiable real study footage.

## Current role

**Why does the same behavior look so different depending on who is explaining it?**

## Core integration thesis

Psychological perspectives are not competing trivia categories. They are different model-building strategies. Each lens reveals something and hides something.

# Chapter 2 — Research Methods & Statistics

## Current role

Chapter 2 already has a strong research-methods spine: correlation is not causation, operational definitions matter, random sampling and random assignment solve different problems, and p-values are often misread. The theoretical-spine revision should make explicit that science is collective model calibration.

## Spine question

**How do we know when our model of reality is wrong?**

## Core integration thesis

Humans detect patterns quickly, but private pattern detection is biased by local experience, salience, motivation, and sampling. Research methods are tools for forcing private models to answer to shared evidence. Statistics do not replace judgment; they formalize uncertainty.

## High-priority prose revisions

1. **Add a spine sentence to the opener.**  
   Correlation headlines are persuasive because the mind automatically turns co-occurrence into causal structure. Methods exist to slow that compression down.

2. **Revise the Evidence Ladder framing.**  
   Make the ladder a model-calibration sequence: describe -> relate -> test -> estimate uncertainty -> replicate.

3. **Add a short bridge from sampling bias to training-data bias.**  
   The chapter already includes an AI connection. Keep it lean: biased samples and biased training data are the same problem at different scales.

4. **Strengthen the p-value section around uncertainty calibration.**  
   Emphasize that a p-value is not a truth meter; effect sizes and confidence intervals help calibrate how much a model should move.

5. **Add one explicit 'what would change your mind?' prompt.**  
   This fits the spine better than generic critical-thinking language.

## Specific section-level suggestions

- **Misconception opener:** add one sentence that the third-variable problem is not a technicality; it is the mind over-compressing association into cause.
- **Operational definitions:** connect directly to AI prompts. A vague construct and a vague prompt fail for the same reason: neither defines what would count as success.
- **Experiments:** random assignment is not just a procedural step; it is a tool for preventing the researcher's preferred model from sneaking into group differences.
- **Statistics:** add a short boxed distinction: `Significance asks: should I doubt the null model? Effect size asks: how much should I care? Confidence interval asks: how precise is this estimate? Replication asks: does the model survive a second test?`

## Media revision issues

1. **Resolve duplicate Ch2 figure sets before HTML conversion.**  
   The visuals inventory notes older files for sampling/assignment and reliability/validity plus newer numbered files. Decide which are canonical, then clean captions and references.

2. **Keep Jon's own images on hold until placement is known.**  
   Do not convert Ch2 HTML until the new image set is reconciled.

3. **Consider adding one spine-specific figure not currently in the inventory.**  
   Existing figures cover methods well. The missing conceptual figure is science as model calibration.

## Learning Lab opportunities

- **Existing/recommended:** correlation-causation classifier.
- **Additional reusable lab:** `docs/labs/ch02/model-calibration.html`  
  Students inspect a claim, identify the current model, choose what evidence would change it, and classify what method could provide that evidence.
- **AI-after-effort prompt:** students first diagnose a flawed headline; then ask AI for alternative causal explanations and evaluate whether AI invents unsupported causes.

# Chapter 7 — Memory

## Current role

Chapter 7 is concept-heavy and currently over target. It already opens with the video-camera misconception and includes strong material on reconstructive memory, H.M., working memory, memory systems, forgetting, and AI hallucination. The spine revision should not add much length. It should reorganize emphasis.

## Spine question

**Why does memory preserve meaning better than detail?**

## Core integration thesis

Memory is not an archive. It is an ecological reconstruction system that keeps information useful for current and future action. Repeated episodes compress into semantic knowledge, schemas, skills, and expectations. This compression makes memory powerful and efficient, but it also explains forgetting, gist errors, source confusion, and false memories.

## High-priority prose revisions

1. **Move the life-history argument into the opener or immediately after it.**  
   The current opener correctly attacks the video-camera myth. Add the positive replacement: memory preserves what remains useful, not everything that happened.

2. **Make episodic -> semantic compression the chapter spine.**  
   Use it to connect encoding, storage, forgetting, retrieval, sleep, and reconstructive error.

3. **Reframe forgetting as updating to the current ecology.**  
   Add the junior-high morning routine example: forgetting obsolete detail is often the model staying current, not simply failing.

4. **Make priming a prediction mechanism.**  
   Priming is not just faster response after exposure; it is prior experience partially pre-activating likely interpretations.

5. **Use trauma carefully.**  
   Trauma can be introduced as a preview of Ch13: some memories resist semanticization because the nervous system treats the threat as unresolved. Keep this brief here.

6. **Trim rather than expand.**  
   Because Ch7 is already long, integrate by replacing generic memory prose with spine-framed prose, not adding new sections.

## Specific section-level suggestions

- **Misconception opener:** after "not recording, but rebuilding," add: memory's job is not faithful storage; its job is usable guidance.
- **Encoding section:** connect limited working memory to the broader constraint/bottleneck principle.
- **Storage/H.M. section:** emphasize that H.M. shows memory is not one thing; different systems support different forms of future action.
- **Sleep consolidation:** tie back to Ch5: sleep is the maintenance window where waking episodes are replayed, reorganized, and partly semanticized.
- **Forgetting section:** reorganize decay/interference/retrieval failure under the larger question: when does the model stop needing this trace?
- **Memory errors section:** frame misinformation/source confusion as provenance loss during compression. The claim survives; the source tag decays.

## Spine question

**Why is one good concept worth more than a hundred memorized facts?**

## Core integration thesis

Thinking is compressed model use. Concepts are not dictionary definitions; they are structured semantic neighborhoods built from repeated experience. Heuristics are fast uses of those models under uncertainty. Intelligence partly reflects how flexibly and precisely someone can build, access, revise, and apply those models.

## High-priority prose revisions

   Experts do not just know more facts; they have denser, better-differentiated semantic neighborhoods.

5. **Sharpen the AI bridge.**

# Chapter 9 — Lifespan Development

## Current role

Chapter 9 is currently organized around development as a construction project across the lifespan. The spine revision should strengthen the biological and social reason humans have such a long developmental period. This is where the book can make Jon's life-history framing do real explanatory work.

## Spine question

**Why does it take humans so much longer to grow up than other animals?**

## Core integration thesis

Development is model tuning across time. Genes build a range of possible nervous systems; experience tunes the actual system to the local ecology. Human childhood is long because the brain is born immature and plastic, then calibrated through attachment, language, teaching, play, peers, culture, and social learning.

## High-priority prose revisions

1. **Move human life history earlier.**  
   The opener currently emphasizes children thinking differently. Keep that, but quickly add why a long childhood exists: plasticity and social tuning.

2. **Add secondary altriciality as a short, vivid frame.**  
   Human infants are not defective adults; they are born immature because the postnatal world is part of brain construction.

3. **Connect synaptogenesis/pruning to model tuning.**  
   The draft already says `use it or lose it`. Add: pruning is the nervous system compressing developmental possibility into locally useful structure.

4. **Use Piaget as a prediction/update framework.**  
   Assimilation = current schema interprets input. Accommodation = prediction error forces schema revision.

5. **Make attachment about external regulation becoming internal regulation.**  
   Early caregivers are not just social partners; they are part of the infant's regulatory system.

# Chapter 12 — Emotion, Stress & Coping

## 8. Bottom-line recommendation

Do not do a global rewrite. Do a **spine pass**: one guiding question, one replacement thesis paragraph, one anchor figure, and one learning-lab opportunity per chapter. The strongest chapters will retain their current teaching examples but make the deeper structure more visible.

The main risk is over-integration: making every phenomenon sound like the same prediction/compression story. The defense against that risk is to keep mechanisms distinct:

- Ch2: science calibrates models.
- Ch7: memory reconstructs and compresses models.
- Ch8: thinking uses compressed semantic models.
- Ch9: development tunes models.
- Ch10: social life collides and transmits models.
- Ch11: personality stabilizes model tendencies.
- Ch12: emotion/stress mark what matters for body regulation and action.
- Ch13: disorders are costly model persistence, rigidity, or overgeneralization.

That gives the book a spine without flattening the psychology.
