## 1. The thesis

**Psychology is biological sense-making under constraint.**

Organisms do not receive reality directly. They receive partial, delayed, filtered signals from their bodies and the world. A nervous system must select what matters, extract useful structure from experience, predict what is likely, act before all evidence is available, and revise when errors matter. Human psychology is this process expanded across development, language, culture, memory, imagination, relationships, science, and self-reflection. We build models of reality, use them to act, resist revising them when they are tied to safety, identity, or belonging, update them when experience makes the old model too inaccurate or costly to maintain, and share them imperfectly with other people.

---

## 2. The master loop

The core mechanism is a loop, not a pipeline:

> **partial input → attention/selection → compression → concepts and schemas → prediction → action and regulation → prediction error → updating or defense**

Key properties of the loop:

- **It is reciprocal.** Concepts enable prediction; prediction error refines concepts. Neither comes first.
- **It is lossy at every compression step.** What emerges from compression is useful but incomplete. This is where the framework's explanatory power lives — lossy compression accounts for both competence and systematic error.
- **Updating is not automatic.** Prediction error only produces revision when the error is judged meaningful and when revising feels safer than defending the current model.
- **The loop runs at multiple timescales simultaneously:** milliseconds (perception), seconds (attention and working memory), hours (episodic encoding and sleep consolidation), years (development and identity).

## 3. Core principles

### Biological substrate, not biological reductionism

Everything psychological has a biological substrate, but biological explanation does not exhaust the psychological. The brain is living, developing, plastic tissue growing inside a physical and social ecology. That ecology provides the patterned input the brain learns to process. Genes build a constrained range of possible nervous systems; experience tunes the actual system within that range.

Biological explanation at the level of neurons and molecules is incomplete because the same neural architecture produces qualitatively different behavior across development, culture, and context. Psychological function is defined by what the system does, not only what it is made of. The social and physical environment is constitutive of the system, not merely an input to it.

### Constraint and bottleneck

The brain cannot process every signal with equal priority, in time, at available attentional cost. The constraint is a compound of bandwidth, anatomy, working memory capacity, action demands, and time pressure. **Attention is the mechanism that allocates limited processing resources.** Consciousness is narrower still — most of perception, skilled action, emotion, and bodily regulation runs automatically. Consciousness intervenes when the automatic system fails: when prediction is violated, when action is uncertain, when competing responses conflict.

The bottleneck is also why prediction is necessary. If the system had to process everything from scratch in real time, it would be too slow to act. Compression and prediction are solutions to a hard physical constraint, not optional features.

### Construction under constraint

The brain does not passively receive reality. It constructs a usable version of reality from partial sensory input, prior experience, body state, goals, concepts, emotions, language, culture, and current context. This construction is constrained by the world — it is not arbitrary — but it is not a copy of the world either.

*The mind does not experience reality directly. It constructs a usable version of reality fast enough to act in. That construction is both the source of human intelligence and the source of systematic error.*

This connects every chapter: perception is constructed from sensory fragments (Ch. 4); memory is reconstructed at retrieval, not replayed (Ch. 8); emotions are constructed from core affect and context (Ch. 12); social judgments are constructed from fast inference under ambiguity (Ch. 11); the self is a constructed model of the organism (Ch. 5); diagnoses are constructed categories applied to continuous experience (Ch. 13); AI outputs are constructed from statistical patterns without grounding (AI bridges across chapters).

This is not relativism. The constructed model is constrained by the body, the world, other people, and evidence. Some constructions work better than others. Science matters because it checks private constructions against shared reality.

### Prediction and updating

The brain uses prior experience — compressed into concepts, schemas, heuristics, emotions, and bodily states — to predict what is probably happening and act before full evidence is available. This is fast and usually good enough.

Predictions take different forms depending on what is being predicted. Concepts are semantic structures that generate predictions about meaning: what something is, what category it belongs to, and what usually follows. Emotions are embodied appraisal processes that generate predictions about mattering: what the situation means for bodily regulation, goals, safety, status, attachment, control, and action. Neither concepts nor emotions are inherently explicit or implicit — both can run automatically below awareness and both can become conscious and reportable. The distinction is not explicit versus implicit; it is what kind of future the system is preparing for.

When prediction holds, processing stays automatic. When it fails, the system must assess whether the error matters:

- Small, low-stakes errors update silently.
- Meaningful errors — involving threat, reward, loss, uncertainty, social signals, attachment, or goal relevance — recruit attention, trigger emotional signals, and may mobilize the body for action.

> **prediction error + meaning + action relevance = attentional priority + emotional signal + possible physiological mobilization**

Prediction error is not the same as emotion. Prediction error is a computational description of mismatch. Emotion is a biological response that marks something as mattering for action. They are related but not interchangeable.

The classic phasic dopamine finding is the clearest worked example of reward prediction error. In simple reward-learning tasks, some midbrain dopamine neurons respond strongly to unexpected reward, shift their response to cues that predict reward, and dip when an expected reward fails to appear. This is not pleasure itself; it is closer to learning, anticipation, wanting, and updating. Sapolsky's "dopamine is about anticipation" framing is compatible with this. The caveat is that dopamine is not only a reward-prediction-error signal — different dopamine pathways also contribute to motivation, movement, novelty, salience, and action selection.

*Student-facing version: Dopamine is not the pleasure chemical. In classic reward-learning studies, it is closer to a "better or worse than expected" teaching signal. Once the system learns what predicts the reward, dopamine shifts toward the cue — the anticipation — not the reward itself.*

*Do not generalize the dopamine RPE mechanism to other emotional systems or to stress physiology. The logic of mismatch detection generalizes; this specific pathway does not.*

### Semanticization and lossy compression

Repeated experience does not remain as isolated episodes. It compresses into semantic structures: concepts, schemas, scripts, skills, categories, and expectations. These structures let the organism infer more than it directly observes and predict without replaying every past instance. This is **semanticization**: the conversion of lived experience into portable prediction tools.

Semanticization is adaptive because it preserves useful structure while discarding detail. But it is **lossy**. The same compression that makes perception, memory, social judgment, language, and expertise efficient also produces systematic errors when the compressed model is applied outside the conditions that built it.

Examples of lossy compression errors, organized by chapter:

| Error | Mechanism | Chapter |
|---|---|---|
| Optical illusions, the dress | Visual priors applied to unusual stimuli | Ch. 4 |
| False memory, gist errors | Meaning retained, exact details reconstructed | Ch. 8 |
| Source amnesia | Claim retained, provenance lost; familiarity becomes felt truth | Ch. 8, 11 |
| Stereotypes | Social category built from biased, salient examples | Ch. 11 |
| Fundamental attribution error | Behavior compressed into trait; situation discarded | Ch. 11 |
| Trauma triggers | Threat model retains structural similarity but loses contextual boundaries | Ch. 8, 13 |
| Habits | Learned action script runs after context has changed | Ch. 7 |
| Expert blind spots | Fluent pattern recognition causes premature closure | Ch. 9 |
| Dunning-Kruger | Novice concept too coarse to reveal missing distinctions | Ch. 9 |
| Diagnosis reification | Label mistaken for causal explanation | Ch. 13 |
| AI hallucination | Statistical pattern compressed without factual grounding | AI bridges |

The unifying teaching line: **the mind is not wrong because it is broken. It is often wrong because it is using a compressed model that is usually good enough.**

### Emotion: mattering and action-readiness

Emotions are the brain's affective predictions — they estimate what a situation means for the organism's body, goals, safety, status, attachment, control, and action. They are part of the priority system, not irrational noise added on top of cognition.

Each emotion reflects a different kind of situation for the organism, not a different severity of the same signal:

| Emotion | What it signals |
|---|---|
| Surprise | Something violated expectation |
| Fear | Something may threaten safety |
| Disgust | Something may contaminate, degrade, or violate boundaries |
| Curiosity | Uncertainty feels potentially resolvable and worth approaching |
| Anxiety | Future uncertainty feels threatening and unresolved |
| Frustration | Expected progress or reward is blocked |
| Shame | The social self is under threat: something about me may be unacceptable, exposed, or diminished |
| Pride | Social standing or self-concept has been confirmed |
| Grief | An attachment bond or valued state has been permanently lost |
| Relief | An anticipated threat did not materialize — mobilization can stand down |

Emotions are not just flags. Each carries a characteristic *action tendency* (Frijda): a prepared direction for response. Fear prepares escape or freezing. Anger prepares confrontation or defense. Curiosity prepares approach and exploration. Shame prepares withdrawal or repair. Emotions are partial action plans, initiated before deliberate thought completes.

In constructionist accounts (Barrett), emotion concepts help constitute specific emotion experiences by categorizing core affect in context — the same negatively valenced, aroused bodily state can become fear, disgust, or shame depending on which concept the brain applies. Importantly, in Barrett's account emotion concepts need not be linguistic: even nonverbal organisms can have emotion through non-linguistic categorization of core affect. This means the account does not imply that infants, animals, or nonverbal humans lack emotion. In broader appraisal accounts, emotions still involve evaluations of meaning, action relevance, control, threat, loss, and goal progress. For the spine, the safe claim is that emotions are not irrational noise: they organize mattering, bodily regulation, and action-readiness. Concepts and emotions work together in the same predictive hierarchy; they are not parallel or competing systems.

*Concepts predict meaning. Emotions predict mattering.*

### Stress and allostatic load

Stress arises when the organism predicts that demands may exceed available resources or control. The body mobilizes energy because action may be required. If the mismatch resolves, the system updates and settles. If it does not — because the threat is chronic, symbolic, social, remembered, imagined, or future-oriented — the mobilization stays on. That cumulative cost is allostatic load (McEwen & Stellar, 1993).

Humans can activate the same ancient stress-mobilization systems around imagined futures, remembered failures, symbolic threats, social judgment, possible rejection, financial uncertainty, moral conflict, and future selves. The simulation capacity that evolved for planning becomes a vulnerability when it keeps the threat-mobilization system running in the absence of any real present danger. Chronic stress is what happens when the simulation machinery runs on threatening futures that never resolve.

*Course metaphor — "cognitive light cone":* This phrase is a course-specific metaphor, not a standard research or diagnostic term. It refers to the full range of events that can matter to the organism now: remembered pasts, imagined futures, other people's perspectives, social meanings, symbolic threats, possible selves, and counterfactual outcomes. Ground it in mental time travel and foresight (Suddendorf & Corballis), the use of past memory to imagine the future (Schacter, Addis & Buckner), prospection and anticipatory affect (Gilbert & Wilson), and Sapolsky's contrast between the zebra's immediate stressor and the human's chronic symbolic one (*Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers*). *Verify Grupe & Nitschke before citing in chapter text.*

### Development: how the model gets built

Genes build a constrained range of possible nervous systems; experience tunes the actual system within that range. Infants begin broadly prepared for many possible environments, then narrow toward the local ecology. Faces, voices, accents, attachment patterns, threat cues, norms, language, and reward histories all shape what becomes easy to perceive, predict, and feel. Perceptual narrowing is one clean example, but the principle generalizes: the developing brain adapts to the patterned world it repeatedly encounters.

Each person's model of the world is therefore partly private. People do not simply disagree because one is rational and one is irrational — they often bring differently tuned biological systems to the same situation. Current state also matters: hormones, fatigue, stress, sleep, hunger, and context modulate whatever the developmental baseline established.

### Memory: ecological reconstruction

Memory is a life history adaptation, not an archival system. The organizing question is not "why did we forget that?" but "what allows the organism to be maximally effective in its current environment?" The brain retains what is relevant now, sheds what is no longer relevant, and compresses what is useful but doesn't need to remain episodic.

Selective encoding follows prediction error: events that violated expectation or carried emotional significance are preferentially encoded. Routine, fully predicted events generate small prediction errors and leave little trace. This is compression working correctly.

The normal consolidation pathway is episodic → semantic. Episodes accumulate and compress into concepts, schemas, and skills that predict without requiring retrieval of individual instances. The episodic scaffolding dissolves because only the compressed product is still needed. Sleep supports this compression by replaying, reorganizing, and stabilizing memory traces. Sleep is the maintenance window for the model — when the machinery is reorganized without the competing demands of real-time sensory processing and action.

Traumatic memories resist the episodic → semantic pathway. The episode stays primed because the nervous system treats the threat as unresolved. Without the signal that "this is understood and handled," the specific episode remains maximally accessible. The organism is doing exactly what it evolved to do — but the calibration is stuck because the ecology changed while the model did not.

### Language and science: making models public

Language partially shares private models. It never fully transfers experience, but it lets people point, compare, revise, teach, argue, and coordinate. Education, science, therapy, and collaboration all depend on this bridge. Because other people are differently tuned nervous systems, when models collide in conversation that collision is itself a prediction error — an encounter with a perspective the current model didn't generate. Whether that error drives updating or defensiveness depends on threat level.

Science is the collective attempt to calibrate individual models against shared evidence. Individual prediction systems are cheap and fast but biased toward local, recent, personally experienced patterns. Statistical reasoning pools observations across people, contexts, and time — making the collective model more accurate than any individual's could be. This is why Ch. 2 (Research Methods & Statistics) belongs at the beginning of the course: it is the epistemological foundation the rest stands on, not a detour.

The same pattern-detection machinery that makes science necessary also explains why disorder is possible. The brain finds order in random data, reads agency into noise, and connects coincidences into causal narratives. Science is the corrective — a method for checking whether the pattern the model detected is actually in the world or was imposed by the detector.

### Disorder and therapy: when models become costly

Disorders are not a separate category of human experience. They are what happens when normal processes — learning mechanisms, stress responses, threat models, social calibrations, biological vulnerabilities — interact in ways that become self-sustaining, rigid, and costly. The model is usually still working; it is miscalibrated, overgeneralized, or stuck to an ecology that no longer applies.

Therapy often works by helping the system revise, contextualize, or regulate those models — not by eliminating them, but by updating the conditions under which they activate or by building new competing associations that reduce their dominance. The same processes that drive learning (prediction error, reconsolidation, extinction) are the mechanisms therapy works through.

## 4. The trade-off structure

The whole system operates under compounding trade-offs. These are not flaws — they are consequences of building a system that must act fast under constraint. Every chapter's phenomena can be located in one or more of these:

| Trade-off | What it explains |
|---|---|
| Speed vs. accuracy | Fast prediction usually works, but produces systematic errors |
| Prediction vs. updating | Priors are cheap; incorporating new evidence costs attention and energy |
| Efficiency vs. flexibility | Automatic models save effort but become rigid — extinction, habits, trauma |
| Stability vs. change | Coherent models give identity and confidence but resist revision — therapy, education |
| Local fit vs. general truth | Development tunes to local ecology, which may fail in new environments |
| Private experience vs. shared reality | Each model is unique; language and science create enough overlap for coordination |

## 5. Personality and social psychology in the locked chapter structure

Personality and social psychology sit at the intersection of multiple spine threads and deserve a brief orienting note. In the locked chapter map, **Social Psychology is Ch. 11**. Personality is not a standalone chapter; its content is selectively folded into Ch. 11 as a subsection.

**Personality content within Ch. 11** reflects stable patterns in how a person's model is tuned: what they expect, notice, value, avoid, seek, regulate, and repeat across situations. Traits are real but probabilistic — the Big Five describes reliable variance in behavioral tendencies, not fixed responses. The person-situation debate is a spine-level issue: the model (trait) interacts with context (situation); behavior is the output of that interaction, not the trait alone.

**Social psychology** (Ch. 11) is what happens when multiple differently tuned nervous systems occupy the same situation. Social heuristics, attribution errors, conformity, persuasion, prejudice, and bystander effects are all cases of the master loop operating in a social environment — where other people are both the most important input and the most consequential output. Stereotypes are social concepts built from biased samples; they are lossy compression applied to people, with unequal consequences. Because another tuned model's output is itself a prediction rather than raw signal, and because that other model may simultaneously be predicting you, private belief and public behavior can separate in social situations in a way they do not elsewhere — see §8's Ch. 11 notes for the full mechanism and Asch's own three categories of yielding.

## 7. Drafting rules

**What the framework should do in chapter prose:**
- Name the spine connection early — what model-building or model-revision process is this chapter about?
- Show where the chapter's phenomena sit in the master loop
- Use the lossy compression lens to connect competence and error — every chapter has both
- Connect concepts to their costs: every efficient mental shortcut is also a source of systematic error
- Every chapter should have a **student-facing spine question** — one sentence naming what the chapter explains in terms a student would find surprising or worth understanding. This question lives at the top of each chapter's Layer 3 notes and should guide the chapter's opening framing. It is not a learning objective; it is the question that makes a student want to read.

**What the framework should not do:**

*On scope:*
- Do not say the brain *is* a prediction machine as if that settles the debate. Say the brain *uses* prediction as a major organizing principle.
- Do not say "everything psychological is biological" without adding that biological explanation does not exhaust the psychological.
- Do not present active inference / free energy minimization as consensus neuroscience. Use as background rationale, not as a claim to teach.
- Do not say concepts are explicit and emotions are implicit. The distinction is semantic vs. affective/appraisal prediction — what kind of future the system is preparing for.
- Do not say emotion and cognition are separate systems. Emotion concepts are constitutive of emotion, not labels added after the fact.
- Do not invoke qualia as a teaching concept. The point that each person's model has a first-person character can be made without entering philosophy of mind disputes.

*On mechanisms:*
- Do not collapse prediction error, dopamine, emotion, and stress into a single mechanism. Name the chain and its links separately.
- Do not generalize the dopamine RPE mechanism to other emotional systems or stress physiology.
- Do not say dopamine equals pleasure. The wanting/liking distinction (Berridge & Robinson) matters for teaching addiction and the hedonic treadmill accurately.
- Do not say "full sensory processing is metabolically impossible" — the bottleneck is attention, bandwidth, time, and action demands, not metabolism alone.

*On specific topics:*
- Do not call oxytocin "the love hormone." It promotes affiliation in safe/familiar contexts and can increase outgroup hostility in threat contexts.
- Do not say breastfeeding is required for mother-infant bonding.
- Do not present postpartum depression as caused primarily by breastfeeding cessation. The relationship is bidirectional and multiply determined.
- Do not present the "chemical imbalance" theory of depression as established neuroscience. Name it as a claim that has driven pharmaceutical advertising; note the evidence does not support it as a complete account.
- Do not say "serotonin = happiness" or imply SSRIs restore a simple deficit. Their efficacy is real; the mechanism is more complex.
- Do not reduce addiction to the mesolimbic pathway alone. The complete picture involves prefrontal control circuits, stress systems, and neuroplasticity.
- Do not present Seth's "controlled hallucination" as consensus terminology or settled neuroscience. Use with attribution.
- Do not resolve the hard problem of consciousness. Name it, establish it is genuine, let it remain open.
- Do not conflate NCC research with the hard problem. Neural correlates address the easy problems; the hard problem asks why those correlates are accompanied by experience at all.
- Do not say Huth et al. proves the brain stores concepts as word embeddings. The embedding is a modeling tool that predicts BOLD responses, not a description of the neural code.
- Do not say LLMs understand concepts the way humans do. Geometric similarity of semantic space does not establish embodiment, motivation, development, or lived meaning.
- Do not say stereotypes are "just categories." They are ordinary categorization machinery applied to people under biased sampling with unequal social consequences.
- Do not say Piaget's developmental sequence is one-directional. Adults use prototypes and exemplars in context-dependent ways; expertise increases exemplar density without eliminating prototype use.
- Do not use in chapter text until independently verified: Popham et al. (2021) visual/linguistic semantic boundary alignment.

### Prologue

**Student-facing spine question:** *Why doesn't studying harder always mean learning more?*

**The teaching model as episodic → semantic arc.** Students arrive with episodic traces — isolated facts, examples half-remembered, intuitions without structure. Good instruction drives compression: the episodes accumulate until a generative concept emerges that predicts rather than lists. The instructor's job is not to transfer information but to create conditions for compression. A concept is ready when a student can use it to predict something they have not been explicitly told.

**Concepts give details somewhere to live.** Before a concept is in place, facts are arbitrary and fragile. After it is in place, facts become inferable consequences of the structure. This is why building the concept first — even roughly — makes subsequent detail easier to retain.

**Students as alternate developmental histories.** When students talk to each other, differently tuned models collide. That collision is itself a prediction error — the student encounters a perspective their current model didn't generate. If the context is safe enough, that error drives updating rather than defensiveness. Structured peer discussion is not a break from learning; it is a mechanism for model revision.

**The zebra question as pedagogy.** Is a zebra black with white stripes or white with black stripes? The question looks trivial but forces students to ask: what evidence would count? Embryology, evolutionary history, developmental sequence, related species, function. The answer matters less than the process — the question creates a situation where the student's current concept is insufficient and has to be rebuilt from multiple converging sources.

### Ch. 3 — Neuroscience

**Student-facing spine question:** *What does the biology of the brain tell us about behavior — and what does it leave out?*

**The bilateral symmetry argument: why nervous systems exist at all.** The action-selection problem is the origin of nervous systems, not a byproduct of them. Unicellular organisms solve approach/avoid via chemotaxis — no nervous system required. Radially symmetric multicellular organisms can still respond locally. But a bilateral body plan — front/back, left/right — means the organism has a committed direction of movement and must decide: move toward, move away, stop, persist, explore, exploit, consume, mate, defend, rest. That is the action-selection problem. Nervous systems evolve to solve it faster, across a spatially distributed body. Brains are centralized action-selection systems with memory and learning. Human brains extend action-selection into imagined futures.

**The multicellular organism framing.** Neurons are the fast-acting communication system required for a large multicellular organism to coordinate its response to the environment. Organisms that do not respond to or act on the environment don't need brains — some sessile organisms actually lose their nervous systems after becoming fixed. The prefrontal cortex executes attention and working memory; conscious/non-conscious reflects the explicit/implicit, automatic/effortful processing split that runs through the whole spine.

**Serotonin and dopamine as action-selection signals.** Serotonin signals "current state is acceptable — maintain course." Dopamine signals "opportunity or mismatch detected — redirect, and weight that direction in memory." The dopamine RPE (Schultz, 1997) is the learned, directional component. *Note: the serotonin framing here is at a high level of abstraction; the "serotonin = happiness" equation is contested and should not be taught as such.*

**Working memory capacity as possible metabolic constraint.** Working memory capacity is remarkably limited (~4 items; Cowan, 2001). One hypothesis: the PFC is already among the most metabolically expensive brain tissue, and expanding WM capacity may be constrained by energy as much as evolutionary history. *Teach as a compelling hypothesis, not a confirmed mechanism.*

**Dopamine RPE: use as illustration, not mechanism.** Present it as such. A 2024 *Nature Neuroscience* perspective explicitly says the classical RPE interpretation is "probably too simple and fails to explain all the properties of phasic dopamine observed in behaving animals" — dopamine also tracks sensory salience, novelty, movement, and action selection. The teaching value is the principle (expected ≠ actual → update), not the claim that dopamine *is* the prediction error signal for the whole brain.

**The "chemical imbalance" theory as a misconception to correct.** The serotonin-deficiency model of depression was widely promoted in direct-to-consumer pharmaceutical advertising from the 1990s onward. A 2022 umbrella review (Moncrieff et al., *Molecular Psychiatry*) found no consistent evidence that people with depression have lower serotonin levels or activity. SSRI efficacy is real, but the mechanism is not simply "adding back what's missing." A more defensible framing: neurotransmitter systems are components of integrated circuits; their role in mood and behavior is defined by which circuits they're in, not by their absolute levels. *Students often arrive with this belief from advertising. It should be named and corrected explicitly.*

**Gut-brain axis as a more defensible bidirectional connection.** The enteric nervous system contains more neurons (~500 million) than the spinal cord and communicates bidirectionally with the brain via the vagus nerve, immune signaling, and microbiome-produced metabolites. This is a more defensible basis for "diet affects mood" reasoning than "eat this food → produce this neurotransmitter → feel different." *One paragraph; no overstatement of current knowledge about specific dietary interventions.*

**Oxytocin as the neuropeptide entry point.** Neuropeptides (oxytocin, vasopressin, CRF) should be introduced in Ch. 3 alongside classical neurotransmitters to establish that the brain uses multiple classes of chemical signal. Oxytocin is the clearest teaching case: hypothalamic synthesis, posterior pituitary release, central neuromodulatory actions, HPA axis attenuation, and roles in social bonding and breastfeeding. This introduction pays forward to Ch. 10 (biobehavioral synchrony, attachment) and Ch. 12 (stress regulation). *Do not call it "the love hormone" — see Drafting Rules.*

### Ch. 4 — Sensation & Perception

**Student-facing spine question:** *Why does perception feel direct when it is constructed?*

**Perception as bottleneck management.** The chapter's central argument is that perception is not passive reception — it is active inference from partial input. The sensory systems don't deliver reality; they deliver filtered, compressed signals that the brain must interpret. The brain's solution is to use prior knowledge, context, and expectations to fill the gaps — predicting what is probably there rather than computing it from scratch.

**Top-down prediction in early perceptual processing.** The predictive coding account (Rao & Ballard, 1999) holds that higher cortical areas send predictions down to lower areas, which send back only the error — the difference between what was predicted and what arrived. This is efficient: most of the time the prediction is close enough that little error propagates. When it isn't — when the stimulus is unexpected, ambiguous, or novel — more signal propagates upward and more updating occurs. This is why expectation shapes perception before we are aware of choosing anything.

**Anil Seth's "controlled hallucination" as the sharpest teaching formulation.** The brain is always generating predictions about the causes of incoming signals. What we experience as perception is the brain's best current prediction, constrained by sensory input. Seth (2021): "We are all hallucinating all the time; when we agree about our hallucinations, we call it reality." The diagnostic test is Charles Bonnet syndrome: when bottom-up sensory constraint is removed (macular degeneration causing visual loss), the generative machinery continues and produces vivid, detailed hallucinations. *Seth's framework is influential but contested — present as one compelling framework, not settled consensus.*

**The dress illusion as the teaching demonstration.** The 2015 blue/black vs. white/gold dress (Wallisch, 2017) is the clearest mass-scale demonstration of controlled hallucination. Identical pixel values → two stable, conflicting color percepts. Mechanism: observers are making different unconscious assumptions about the illuminant and discounting it to recover the "real" surface color. Neither group is wrong — both are applying exactly what the perceptual system evolved to do.

**Perceptual disorders as evidence for the constructive account.**
- *Visual agnosia*: intact low-level vision, inability to recognize objects.
- *Prosopagnosia*: can see faces perfectly, cannot recognize them as familiar.
- *Charles Bonnet syndrome*: visual loss → vivid, detailed visual hallucinations. The brain's generative machinery, freed from bottom-up sensory anchor, continues producing percepts.
- *Anosognosia*: patient with left-side paralysis cannot recognize their own paralysis. Not denial — a genuine deficit in self-monitoring, revealing that the "I" who knows what is happening in one's body is itself constructed.

**Cultural variation in perception — evidence that priors are learned, not universal.** The Müller-Lyer illusion is substantially reduced or absent in people raised in non-carpentered environments (Segall, Campbell & Herskovits, 1963). Same stimulus, different prior, different percept. This is the strongest evidence that top-down prediction is culturally shaped.

**Individual differences in perception — a three-source taxonomy.** (1) *Sensory differences* — colorblindness (~8% of males), supertasting, synesthesia. (2) *Experience-shaped priors* — perceptual learning, priming, expertise, and cultural upbringing. (3) *Current state differences* — goals, emotional state, fatigue, attention, and context.

**The binding problem: where Ch. 4 hands the question to Ch. 5.** Color is processed in V4, motion in V5/MT, form in inferotemporal cortex — yet we experience a unified moving red ball. How? Candidate mechanisms (temporal synchrony via 40Hz gamma oscillations; attention as feature binder; global workspace broadcast) are suggestive but not conclusively established. Ch. 4 should end with this question open — because the question of how distributed processing becomes unified experience is what Ch. 5 is about.

### Ch. 5 — Consciousness

**Student-facing spine question:** *Why don't we experience most of what our brain is doing?*

**Attention as the bottleneck control system.** Attention is not a spotlight that illuminates a passive scene; it is a control system that shapes what gets processed at all — amplifying some signals, suppressing others, biasing perception toward what is currently relevant, expected, threatening, rewarding, or goal-related.

**Consciousness as override, not supervisor.** The common intuition is that consciousness runs cognition. The more accurate account is that automatic processing runs most of cognition, and consciousness intervenes when automatic processing is insufficient. This is why skilled performers often say "I stopped thinking and it clicked" — they removed the override from a system already well-calibrated.

**"Automatic camera with manual override" as the signature consciousness analogy.** Most of the time the camera handles exposure, focus, and framing automatically — fast, efficient, parallel, outside awareness. Conscious attention is the manual override: engaged when auto mode hits its limits (novel situation, prediction failure, competing responses, uncertainty). The analogy captures why automatic processing exists (efficiency) and why override is costly (slow, serial, effortful) but necessary.

**The attention-consciousness hierarchy.** Attention can operate without consciousness — subliminal priming, attentional capture by unnoticed stimuli, and inattentional blindness all demonstrate that the attentional system selects and suppresses signals that never reach conscious awareness. Consciousness is narrower than attention.

**The hard problem and the easy problems (Chalmers, 1995).** The "easy problems" — how the brain integrates information, discriminates stimuli, focuses attention, produces verbal reports — are scientifically tractable. The "hard problem" asks why any of this is accompanied by subjective experience at all. For intro psych, the correct pedagogical move is not to resolve this — it is to establish that it exists and is genuine. *Hedge: Dennett argues the hard problem is confused and disappears on full functional analysis; Chalmers argues it is irreducible. Present both without false resolution.*

**The cognitive light cone as what makes human consciousness distinctive.** Humans represent decades of personal history, imagined futures, counterfactuals, the perspectives of absent people, fictional characters, and abstract symbols. This vastness is what makes human consciousness both extraordinary and costly — the same expansion that allows planning and moral reasoning also enables rumination, anticipatory dread, and existential anxiety.

**The octopus as consciousness foil.** Approximately two-thirds of an octopus's ~500 million neurons are in its arms, not its central brain. Each arm has its own sensory processing and can act semi-autonomously without waiting for central instruction. This raises the question: is there a unified "what it's like" to be an octopus, or is there something it's like to be each arm? One paragraph in Ch. 5 opens the "what counts as a self?" question. It also makes the bilateral action-selection point: many different architectures solve the action-coordination problem; centralization is one solution, not the only one.

**The self as construction (Seth's extension).** If external perception is a controlled hallucination constrained by sensory input, then the experiencing self is also a controlled hallucination — a generative model that predicts what kind of organism this is and what "I" am about to do. The rubber hand illusion, out-of-body experiences, and depersonalization all demonstrate that the sense of self is another prediction. *Use with attribution: Seth's argument; not consensus.*

**Neural delay and retrospective construction (Eagleman).** The brain takes approximately 80ms to integrate signals from different modalities and produce a unified experience. Consciousness is not a live feed; it is a slightly delayed, edited broadcast. This reinforces the constructive account without requiring the full philosophical argument.

### Ch. 6 — Sleep

**Sleep as model maintenance — detailed version.** Three functional layers for Ch. 6:

- *Consolidation.* Memory traces laid down during waking are replayed, tagged, and transferred during sleep — particularly during slow-wave and REM stages. Sleep supports episodic-to-semantic compression; deprivation disrupts this and is not simply recoverable with effort.
- *Glymphatic clearance.* During deep sleep, the glymphatic system flushes metabolic waste (including amyloid-beta) from brain tissue. Chronic sleep deprivation allows metabolic byproducts to accumulate.
- *Regulatory reset.* Sleep restores the medial PFC-amygdala regulatory circuit. Sleep deprivation weakens top-down PFC modulation of the amygdala, amplifying emotional reactivity and making threat responses harder to regulate. Sleep deprivation is a partial offline of the system that makes deliberate behavioral control possible.

### Ch. 7 — Learning

**Student-facing spine question:** *Why is unlearning harder than learning?*

**Extinction as prediction error, not erasure.** Extinction is not the deletion of the original association. It is new inhibitory learning layered on top — a new prediction ("CS no longer predicts US") that competes with the original. The original trace remains. This is why spontaneous recovery occurs, why renewal occurs when context changes, and why extinguished responses can return under stress. The organism's model was updated, but the prior was not erased — it was suppressed by a more recent prediction.

### Ch. 8 — Memory

**Student-facing spine question:** *Why does memory preserve meaning better than detail?*

**The life history argument.** Memory is a fitness-relevant adaptation, not an archival system. The organizing question is not "why did we forget that?" but "what allows the organism to be maximally efficacious in its current environment?" Natural selection shaped memory toward ecological efficacy: fast access to what is relevant now, shedding what is no longer relevant, compressing what is useful but doesn't need to be episodic.

**Ecological relevance, not just encoding strength.** A morning routine from junior high may have been adequately encoded at the time, but it has zero action relevance now. The social and physical ecology it was calibrated to no longer exists. Forgetting it is not a failure; it is the model staying current.

**Priming as probabilistic parsing.** Priming is the memory system's equivalent of the prediction/compression solution: rather than searching from scratch, the relevant past is already partially active. Priming is context-sensitive, which is why the same person retrieves different material in different states, contexts, and social situations.

**Episodic → semantic compression as the normal pathway.** Episodes accumulate and compress into generative concepts — schemas, skills, semantic knowledge — that predict without requiring retrieval of individual instances. The episodic scaffolding dissolves because only the compressed product is needed. This is also the arc of good teaching.

### Ch. 9 — Thinking, Intelligence & Conceptual Knowledge

**Student-facing spine question:** *Why is one good concept worth more than a hundred memorized facts?*

**The organizing formulation: a concept is a location plus a pattern of relations in semantic space.** Not a definition, not a box, not a label. "Apple" sits near fruit, food, sweetness, trees, red/green/yellow, seeds, pie, school lunches, brands, health, childhood — a compressed region of meaning built from repeated episodes. This formulation unifies the entire chapter sequence:

- *Why definitions fail:* natural concepts don't have fixed boundaries (Wittgenstein, family resemblance).
- *Why prototypes work:* typical cases sit near the center of a semantic neighborhood (Rosch, 1975).
- *Why exemplars matter:* specific encounters populate and stretch the neighborhood; expertise makes the space denser.
- *Why semantic networks are right but incomplete:* spreading activation is real but concepts are not discrete nodes.
- *Why connectionism is one important computational model:* PDP/connectionist models learn from co-occurrence statistics across experience, adjusting weights until the network settles on distributed representations. This is not the confirmed neural mechanism — it is a productive model of how distributed semantic structure could be learned from repeated patterns.
- *Why embodied cognition is partially right:* Hauk et al. (2004) found somatotopic motor activation when reading action words. *Hedge: the epiphenomenal critique is live — motor activation may accompany rather than constitute meaning.*

**Huth et al. (2016) as convergent evidence.** Seven subjects listened to 2+ hours of Moth Radio Hour stories in fMRI. A semantic embedding space predicted voxel-by-voxel BOLD responses across the entire cortex. Key findings: ~140 semantically selective areas continuously distributed across temporal, parietal, and prefrontal cortex; first and largest PCA dimension: social/emotional/violent vs. perceptual/locational/numeric; semantic maps were remarkably consistent across individuals. *Do not say this proves the brain stores concepts as word embeddings — the embedding is a modeling tool that predicts BOLD responses, not a description of the neural code.*

**The social/emotional primacy finding and its evolutionary interpretation.** The largest variance dimension separating human semantic space is social/emotional/violent vs. perceptual/physical/numeric. Evolutionarily interpretable: the most important semantic dimension for a hypersocial primate is other minds and their states. *Hedge: the social primacy may partly reflect the stimulus — Moth Radio Hour stories are narratively social. Present as a finding from naturalistic story processing, not a universal architectural fact.*

**LLM-brain alignment as the modern AI bridge.** Tang et al. (2023, *Nature Neuroscience*) showed that LLM embeddings fitted to voxel models can reconstruct the gist of what someone is hearing from fMRI alone. *Verify Tang & Huth (2025, *Current Biology*) and 2024 brain-LLM alignment claims before using in chapter text — not yet independently confirmed in our research.* The reason for alignment: both systems are shaped by statistical regularities of human language. *Hedge: alignment is correlation, not mechanism. LLMs and brains may be convergent solutions to the same statistical input, not shared implementations.*

**The Piaget-schema developmental sequence.** 1. *Sensorimotor schema* (infant): action-based, not yet conceptual. 2. *Early prototype* (toddler): coarse, overextended — "doggie" may include deer. 3. *Accommodation/differentiation* (preschool–school age): world pushes back, schema splits and sharpens. 4. *Mature concept* (school age onward): richer semantic neighborhood, supports inference and flexible generalization. Key: this is not "prototypes, then concepts." Adults still use prototypes; children also store exemplars. What changes with development and expertise is the density, differentiation, and range of the semantic neighborhood.

**Assimilation and accommodation as prediction and model revision.** In spine language: assimilation is using the current compressed model to interpret a new case (prediction). Accommodation is revising the model when the new case doesn't fit (model revision after prediction error).

**The reinforcement worked example.** A student first hears "reinforcement": crude schema — reward = reinforcement. Examples break it: negative reinforcement is not punishment; reinforcement is defined by behavior increasing, not by pleasantness. Accommodation occurs. The mature concept lets the student classify new cases correctly. Use as a callout in the chapter — it demonstrates the definition-failure, prototype-building, exemplar-accumulation, differentiation sequence in a domain students already care about.

**What Popham et al. (2021) adds** (if verified — needs checking before chapter goes to print): visual and linguistic semantic maps reportedly align near the border of visual cortex, with categories represented visually on one side and linguistically on the adjacent side. *Flag: not yet independently confirmed. Do not use in chapter text until verified.*

**Language throughline, added Session 68 (2026-07-04).** Added after a discussion with Jon (and a parallel discussion he ran with GPT) surfaced that Ch9's Language section read as standard textbook coverage rather than a piece of the chapter's own compression argument.

**The organizing formulation: language is the public compression of private models.** Each person builds a private, idiosyncratic model of meaning from their own history of experience. No one has direct access to another person's model. Language is the shared, further-compressed code used to coordinate across those private models — good enough to teach, argue, and build culture with, but lossy, the same way any compression is lossy. Two people can use the same word, agree on how to use it, and still mean something subtly different by it. *Do not use "qualia" to make this point (see Drafting Rules) — "private semantic space" or "individual model of meaning" gets the same pedagogical point across without opening the hard-problem door this book otherwise stays out of.*

**What language is *for* is a live, unresolved question — present both sides.** Fedorenko, Piantadosi, & Gibson (2024, *Nature*) argue from a wide range of neuroscience evidence (including preserved reasoning in severe aphasia) that language in modern humans is primarily a tool for communication, not the medium of thought itself. This is not consensus — it stands in direct tension with Chomsky's own long-standing view that language evolved chiefly as an instrument of thought, with communication secondary. Present both; do not resolve it for students.

**Language acquisition: architecture over history-of-debate.** The old behaviorism → Chomsky → statistical-learning sequence should be reframed around what's actually settled versus not. Settled: behaviorism alone cannot explain acquisition (children generate novel grammatical structures with no reinforcement history for the specific rule). Also fairly settled: children are not blank slates — some biological preparedness for structure is real. Not settled: a literal, fully specified Universal Grammar module. Modern accounts distribute the explanatory work across biological preparedness, statistical learning (Saffran, Aslin, & Newport, 1996 — infants segment words from continuous speech after minutes of exposure to an artificial language), and social interaction, which statistics alone don't explain — Kuhl, Tsao, & Liu (2003) found that American infants exposed to Mandarin through a live person learned Mandarin phonetic contrasts, while infants given the identical audio through a recording learned nothing.

**The LLM angle updates this debate and belongs in the chapter.** Piantadosi (2023) argues LLMs — fluent without any built-in domain-specific grammar module — undercut the strongest nativist claims. The strongest reply: Kodner, Payne, & Heinz (2023) note that humans acquire language *despite* a poverty of the stimulus, while LLMs succeed *because of* a richness of it (many orders of magnitude more text than a child ever hears) — that efficiency gap is arguably evidence for built-in human structure, not against it. Separately, and just as important for this book's AI-integration theme: Mahowald, Ivanova, Blank, Kanwisher, Tenenbaum, & Fedorenko (2024) distinguish formal linguistic competence (grammatical pattern knowledge) from functional linguistic competence (using language to reason and refer to the world) — human neuroscience shows these draw on different neural systems, and current models are far better at the first than the second. Teaching line: fluent language is a compressed pattern; a compressed pattern is not the model-building it was compressed from.

**Linguistic relativity: reframe as granularity, not determinism.** The standard strong/weak Whorf framing is accurate but inert — it doesn't connect to the chapter's own spine. The better frame: experience is continuous; language carves it into named categories; a named distinction becomes cheap, automatic, and easy to communicate, while an unnamed distinction stays available but effortful. Primary examples, in order of evidentiary strength: (1) color — Russian's *goluboy*/*siniy* split sharpens discrimination at the lexical boundary, and the effect disappears under verbal interference (Winawer et al., 2007) — the model case, since the interference manipulation shows the effect is genuinely linguistic; (2) spatial reference frames — absolute (north/south/east/west) vs. egocentric (left/right) systems track with habitually different spatial representations (Levinson et al., 2002) — frame as habitual attention, not causation of navigation ability; (3) number words — Pirahã lacks exact-quantity words beyond "few/many" and shows impaired exact matching of larger sets (Gordon, 2004); Mundurukú, similarly lacking a full counting system, can compare/add large approximate quantities but fails at exact arithmetic past four or five (Pica, Lemer, Izard, & Dehaene, 2004) — frame as "approximate quantity is built in, exact number is a cultural technology language supplies," and explicitly flag that this is not evidence of lower intelligence. **Demote to a brief, explicitly hedged mention, not core prose:** grammatical gender (Phillips & Boroditsky, 2003) and time metaphors (Boroditsky, 2001) — both are famous flagship examples with real replication failures (a registered replication failed to reproduce the gender effect; at least six independent attempts failed to reproduce the time-metaphor finding). Naming the replication problem is itself a teaching opportunity, not just a caveat to bury.

**Framing effect is the bridge from heuristics to language, not just a System 1 example.** Language doesn't merely describe a decision, it helps construct the decision representation System 1 evaluates — "200 saved" vs. "400 die" compress the same outcome around different reference points. Prospect theory's loss-aversion account explains why those framings pull differently. Use this explicitly as the transition sentence out of Section 2 into Section 3.

**Intelligence throughline, added Session 68 (2026-07-04).** Also added following the same discussion — the chapter's Intelligence section previously had no stated connection to the compression spine at all, which is why it read as a bolted-on psychometrics unit.

**The organizing formulation: intelligence is how well a mind can build, use, revise, and transfer compressed models.** Not "how much a person already knows." Fluid intelligence (*Gf*) is the speed and quality of building a new compressed model from unfamiliar material with no existing schema to lean on (Raven's-matrices-style pattern abstraction is close to a pure test of this). Crystallized intelligence (*Gc*) is the accumulated library of models already built and compressed from experience. *g* can be taught as the broad, shared variance across tasks that may partly reflect domain-general model-building resources — working memory capacity, processing speed, relational integration — but flag clearly that "compression efficiency" is this book's teaching synthesis, not a term from the psychometric literature itself; *g* is defined empirically as a factor, not defined as compression ability.

**Gardner and Sternberg are a valid critique of what IQ compresses away, not a rival measurement model.** Frame explicitly: both theories exist because something real gets left out when cognitive performance is compressed into one score. Where they run into trouble is treating that valid critique as equivalent, psychometrically, to *g*-based models — the evidence for genuine independence among Gardner's eight intelligences is weak, and both frameworks are more accepted in education than in cognitive-neuroscience measurement.

**IQ as a useful, lossy, standardized compression — say this explicitly, don't just imply it.** An IQ score is exactly what this chapter's argument would predict: useful for what standardization is good for (prediction, comparison, tracking change), blind to exactly what compression always drops.

**Flynn Effect ties to the "how the model gets built" development thread.** Richer environments supply more and better raw material for the compression process to work on — independent of genes entirely.

**Group differences: this is where a lossy compression becomes socially dangerous.** Frame explicitly as the darkest, highest-stakes version of the chapter's own argument — a compressed score mistaken for a fixed essence. Jon reviewed the existing hedged treatment of this topic (real in the data, contested in interpretation, not explained by between-group genetics, stereotype threat as one documented mechanism, environmental factors substantial) and chose to keep it essentially as-is, tied to this framing sentence, rather than shrink, swap the example, or cut it — treat that as a settled editorial decision, not open for silent revision.

### Ch. 10 — Lifespan Development

**Student-facing spine question:** *Why does it take humans so much longer to grow up than other animals?*

*Primary source: Oxford & Geary, "Life History Evolution in Hominins" (Jon's paper — use as grounding reference for this chapter, not necessarily as an in-text citation for an intro course).*

**Life history theory as the organizing framework.** Humans are at the extreme slow end of the fast/slow life history continuum: largest brains, longest developmental period, latest first reproduction, longest lifespan. This is not incidental — it is the central explanatory fact about human psychology.

**The expensive brain problem.** At approximately 4 years of age, the human brain consumes nearly double the glucose of an adult brain. Somatic growth slows to compensate. No single mother can fund this alone.

**Secondary altriciality as the mechanism for tuning.** Human infants are born with brains at ~25% of adult volume (chimpanzees: ~50%). Maximum immaturity at birth = maximum postnatal plasticity = maximum sensitivity to local social and ecological input. The extended developmental period is the window in which the brain is calibrated to its specific social world.

**Cooperative breeding as the social infrastructure of human cognition.** Grandmothers, fathers, siblings, bilateral kinship networks, and alloparents reduce the mother's energetic burden by 14–29%, allowing shorter interbirth intervals. The "gray ceiling" on primate brain size was broken not by becoming smarter first, but by restructuring the social ecology. Our intelligence co-evolved with our sociality.

**The developmental stages as evolved programs.** Infancy (0–3): rapid brain growth, language onset, attachment formation. Childhood (3–6.9 years — unique to humans): peak brain glucose uptake; theory of mind; rapid concept learning. Juvenile (7 to puberty): social skills, peer competition/cooperation, skill practice. Adolescence (largely absent in great apes): PFC maturation continues; reward system hyperresponsive; learning of adult economic, social, and reproductive ecology.

**The Neanderthal comparison as a natural experiment.** Neanderthals had comparable or larger final brain volumes but evidence of faster early brain development — less postnatal plasticity. The *pace* of brain development may matter as much as the endpoint. *Treat as an informative comparison with appropriate uncertainty, not a settled conclusion.*

**Differential susceptibility — orchid/dandelion (Boyce & Ellis, 2005).** Dandelion children develop adequately across a wide range of conditions. Orchid children are more reactive in both directions: worse outcomes in harsh environments *and* better outcomes in supportive ones. High environmental sensitivity is a design feature when the environment is informative. Upside sensitivity is the empirical signature distinguishing differential susceptibility from diathesis-stress. *Behavioral longitudinal evidence is stronger than specific candidate-gene findings, which have had replication issues — teach the framework without leaning on specific genes.*

**Teaching as an evolutionary adaptation.** Only humans (to present knowledge) actively teach — modify behavior specifically to facilitate learning in a naïve observer. The classroom is a culturally instantiated version of the provisioned, protected developmental environment that human life history built.

**Mother-infant biobehavioral synchrony as the mechanism for early attachment.** Feldman et al. document synchronized oxytocin pulses, heart rate variability, cortisol rhythms, vocal matching, and eye contact timing between mothers and infants. This synchrony predicts later attachment security and social competence. The infant's stress-regulatory capacity is partly external at birth — the mother's regulated state regulates the infant's state through physiological coupling. Over development, the infant internalizes the regulatory capacity that was initially shared. *Do not say breastfeeding is required for synchrony — synchronized visual, tactile, and vocal contact also drives oxytocin and synchrony.*

### Ch. 11 — Social Psychology

**Student-facing spine question:** *Why do other people's models change our own?*

**Social psychology as model-building organisms in contact.** Social psychology studies what happens when model-building organisms interpret, influence, coordinate with, and defend themselves against other model-building organisms. Other people are the most important input the brain processes — the most relevant, most variable, most consequential, and most ambiguous source of information an organism encounters. Social cognition is not a specialized module; it is the prediction system applied to its most important domain.

**Attachment supplies the developmental prototype for social prediction generally.** Before a nervous system predicts what other people think or intend, it first predicts something more basic: if I signal need, will care arrive? Bowlby's internal working model is this prediction, built in infancy from real caregiving history — Ch. 10's attachment and biobehavioral synchrony material is its developmental origin. The standard extension of attachment theory to adult relationships (Hazan & Shaver, 1987) proposes that this early template — is connection reliable, is signaling need safe, what happens when I reach for someone — gets carried forward and applied to friendships, romantic relationships, and group belonging generally. Social psychology's phenomena run on top of this older, more basic prediction; "will this group accept me" is a variant of the question attachment answered first. *This is a widely used extension of attachment theory, not an uncontested one — flag the scope and mechanism as debated if cited in chapter text.*

**Interacting with another tuned model is not the same kind of input as interacting with the world.** Ordinary environmental input is indifferent — a chair does not adjust itself based on what you are likely to conclude about it. Another person's behavior is different in two ways: it is itself the output of a compression system, not raw signal, so it can function as genuine evidence about a shared reality (pooling information the way any other observation would); and the person producing it may simultaneously be modeling you, anticipating how you will react to what they do. This reciprocal, non-neutral quality is what makes social input structurally different from object perception, and it is why "what I privately predict is true" and "what I do" can come apart in the social domain in a way they cannot when the input is a rock.

**Social judgment as fast inference under ambiguity.** People are the hardest things to read — intentions are invisible, faces are ambiguous, words are underdetermined, behavior is situationally shaped. The brain resolves this ambiguity the same way it resolves perceptual ambiguity: fast inference using priors. Those priors include category membership, past experience, current goals, mood, and cultural scripts. Social judgment is quick, usually good enough, and systematically wrong in predictable ways.

**Attribution is compression: behavior becomes character, situation gets discarded.** When a person acts, the brain rapidly constructs an explanation. The fundamental attribution error is the systematic over-compression of situational causes into dispositional ones — behavior is stamped as character even when the situation was doing most of the causal work. This is lossy compression applied to people: we retain the trait inference and discard the contextual detail. It is also the same statistical error the person-situation debate corrects for self-judgment (a single behavioral sample under-predicts disposition — see the Personality subsection below): the FAE is what happens when an outside observer commits that same n=1 mistake about someone else's tuning, without access to the aggregated cross-situational data that would reveal a person's actual behavioral pattern.

**Stereotypes are social compression with power.** Stereotypes are category expectations built from biased, salient, culturally supplied examples. They allow fast social prediction in the same way any concept allows fast inference — at the cost of lossy generalization. The special feature is the sampling bias: social categories are learned from media, cultural narrative, limited personal contact, and emotionally charged examples, not from representative samples. And unlike misremembering the word "apple," stereotype-based errors have asymmetric social consequences.

**Belonging is the general motivational stake beneath locally specific social rules.** Cultures supply the specific content of social norms — what is expected, rewarded, or sanctioned varies by context and is transmitted the way beliefs are (see below). But the reason violating a norm carries weight at all is older and more general than any specific rule: humans have a basic drive to form and maintain relational bonds, and threats to that bond are treated as motivationally urgent regardless of the specific content of what was violated (Baumeister & Leary's belongingness hypothesis, 1995). This is the same principle running underneath social identity theory (group membership regulates self-esteem) and reciprocal altruism (cooperation among non-relatives requires tracking reputation and standing over time) — both are applications of one underlying stake: predicting and protecting your position in other tuned models' evaluation of you, not only predicting the physical world.

**Conformity is social calibration.** When people shift their responses toward group norms, they are often not simply caving to pressure — they are updating their model using social information. Other people's responses are data about what is probably true, safe, appropriate, or expected. Asch's conformity studies are most interesting not as demonstrations of weakness but as demonstrations that social consensus is a legitimate epistemic input — one that the prediction system weights heavily and not always wrongly.

**Asch's own reported categories of yielding map onto three different points in the loop where social input can intervene.** Some participants reported that they came to see the lines the way the majority did — social input altered compression itself. Most reported that they privately still leaned toward their own answer but assumed the group must know something they didn't — a belief-level update, informational social influence treating the group's output as legitimate evidence. A further group reported knowing they were right and answering incorrectly anyway, purely to avoid standing out — here the prediction never changed at all; only the action output did, overridden by a competing cost function (anticipated social exposure). This third category is the clearest demonstration that what the model predicts and what gets executed as behavior are not the same thing, and that a second tuned agent in the situation is what allows the two to separate. *Verify Asch's original 1955/1956 reporting of these three categories, and the specific proportions attributed to each, before citing this breakdown in chapter text — secondary sources describe it consistently but the primary framing should be checked directly.*

**Obedience narrows the action model.** Milgram's findings are not primarily about aggression or sadism. They are about how authority, role, and context reshape the perceived range of available actions. Obedience works partly by shifting perceived responsibility and partly by narrowing the model of what one is free to do. This connects to the agentic state as a situation in which the action-selection system is partially outsourced.

**Beliefs as transmitted vs. feelings as conditioned.** Beliefs are vertically transmitted information — acquired through explicit instruction, narrative, family, culture, peer group. Feelings (conditioned affective responses) are built from personal experience history through associative learning. This distinction matters for attitude change: persuasion and evidence work better on belief-based attitudes; direct exposure and experiential re-learning work better on feeling-based ones. A student can update the belief "snakes are not dangerous by default" and still feel visceral fear around them — because the fear is conditioned, not believed.

**Source amnesia as the mechanism that blurs the belief/feeling boundary.** Beliefs can become felt truths through repetition. Familiarity from repeated exposure generates an implicit sense of recognition that is cognitively indistinguishable from genuine knowing. The person no longer tracks *why* they believe something; it just feels true. Propaganda, advertising, and politically motivated repetition exploit this.

**The just-world hypothesis as the social mechanism for stigma.** The belief that the world is fair generates victim-blaming when applied to mental illness, poverty, academic failure, or trauma. This is the social-psychology mechanism by which stigma is maintained and self-stigma is generated. It connects Ch. 11 directly to Ch. 13 (Disorders).

**Belonging threats recruit old threat-detection architecture rather than requiring new machinery.** Consistent with the framework's general pattern of reusing existing systems for new prediction targets (dopamine's reuse across reward domains, the amygdala's reuse across threat categories), the leading evolutionary account holds that exclusion and physical injury draw on an overlapping alarm-system logic rather than the brain building a dedicated system for social threat from scratch (MacDonald & Leary, 2005, review this argument at the behavioral and evolutionary level). *Caution: the specific neural claim most often cited for this — that Cyberball-induced social exclusion activates the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex, the same region implicated in physical pain (Eisenberger, Lieberman, & Williams, 2003) — has had significant replication trouble. Recent meta-analytic work finds social exclusion reliably engages the default mode network but not reliably the dACC, and critics argue the original dACC activation may reflect general conflict/expectancy-violation processing rather than pain specifically. Do not present this in chapter text as "the same brain region as physical pain" — present MacDonald & Leary's evolutionary reuse argument as the load-bearing claim, and flag the neural-specificity claim as contested if it is mentioned at all.* A separate, methodologically distinct fMRI finding (Klucharev et al., using a facial-attractiveness judgment paradigm, not a line-judgment task) reported that disagreeing with group opinion produced a prediction-error-like signal in reward and error-monitoring circuitry, and that conforming activated reward-related regions — a possibly relevant, literal instantiation of "the brain treats social disagreement as an error signal," but this needs a full read of the primary paper before any use in chapter text, and should not be conflated with the Cyberball findings above.

#### Personality (content folds into this chapter)

**Student-facing spine question:** *Why do the same people behave differently across situations and yet remain recognizably themselves?*

**Traits as probabilistic summaries, not inner switches.** The Big Five (and other trait frameworks) describe reliable patterns of variation across people — tendencies to interpret, regulate, seek, and avoid in characteristic ways. A trait is not a fixed response or an inner essence. It is a probabilistic summary of how a person tends to behave, on average, across many situations. High conscientiousness does not guarantee conscientiousness in every situation; it predicts it more often than low conscientiousness would.

**Personality is person × situation.** The same person can be warm in one context and guarded in another, impulsive at a party and disciplined at work. This is not inconsistency — it is the interaction of a stable tuned model with different situational contexts. Traits predict behavior most reliably across aggregated situations and least reliably in any single instance. Mischel's original critique and subsequent resolution both live here: traits are real, situations are powerful, and the interaction is the phenomenon.

**Self-concept as a compressed self-model.** Just as a concept is a compressed model of an external category, the self-concept is a compressed model of the organism itself — its goals, values, abilities, roles, relationships, and characteristic patterns. It is built from episodic memory, feedback from others, social comparison, and cultural meaning. Like any compressed model, it is incomplete, selective, and updated slowly.

**Identity gives continuity but can resist revision.** A stable identity allows prediction, planning, and commitment. It also provides a reference model that interprets new information in ways that preserve coherence. This is why identity can resist revision even in the face of contradictory evidence — the model is protecting its own internal consistency. Therapy, education, and significant life transitions all sometimes require this resistance to be worked through, not just bypassed.

**Self-model protection is one mechanism, showing up under several names.** Cognitive dissonance (revising a belief to match behavior rather than face the inconsistency), self-serving bias (attributing success internally and failure externally), and deindividuation (behavior loosening when the self-model's normal monitoring function goes quiet in a crowd) are three faces of the same underlying principle stated in the thesis: models resist revision when doing so threatens identity or safety, or come apart when the self-model that would normally regulate action is not actively engaged. The first two protect the self-model from a threatening revision; the third shows what happens to behavior when the self-model's monitoring role is temporarily suspended rather than actively defending itself.

**Personality develops from temperament plus ecology.** Temperament — the biologically rooted, early-appearing variation in reactivity, self-regulation, and approach/avoidance — sets the initial tuning. Ecology (family, peers, culture, socioeconomic context, life events) shapes how that temperament is expressed, reinforced, and elaborated. Neither temperament nor ecology is destiny. Differential susceptibility applies: high-reactive temperaments show larger effects in both directions.

**Durable personality change requires repeated new contexts, not just insight.** Understanding why a pattern exists does not automatically change it. Durable change more often follows sustained new roles, relationships, habits, behavioral experiments, and feedback loops that repeatedly engage different responses and reward them. This connects to Ch. 7 (learning through consequence) and Ch. 13 (therapy as model revision through new experience, not only understanding).

### Ch. 12 — Emotion, Stress & Coping

**Student-facing spine question:** *How does the body decide what matters?*

**The cognitive light cone as the mechanism for human-specific chronic stress.** Zebras mobilize stress physiology around immediate physical threat. Humans can activate the same ancient systems around imagined futures, remembered failures, symbolic threats, social judgment, financial uncertainty, moral conflict, and future selves. Chronic stress is what happens when the simulation machinery runs on threatening futures that never resolve.

**The amygdala / SM case study as a teaching anchor for fear circuitry.** Patient SM (Adolphs et al., 1994) has bilateral amygdala damage. She shows no conditioned fear response, approaches strangers at inappropriately close distances, and has handled venomous snakes without aversion. She can describe that a situation *should* be scary without experiencing fear. This is the clearest available evidence that the amygdala is necessary for threat appraisal — not for knowing about danger, but for generating the affective response to it. *Some caution: debate exists about whether the SM deficit is specific to fear or broader.*

**The hedonic treadmill — why external achievements don't sustain happiness.** Brickman & Campbell (1971): people adapt to positive and negative life changes and return to a baseline level of subjective well-being. The dopamine mechanism: dopamine signals anticipation of reward, not the reward state itself. Once a reward becomes expected (the lottery win becomes the new baseline), the anticipatory signal fades. What maintains the dopaminergic signal: novelty, engagement within competence, unpredictable positive social connection. *Don't overstate adaptation — longitudinal evidence shows adaptation is real but incomplete; circumstances matter, especially at the extremes.*

### Ch. 13 — Disorders & Therapy

**Student-facing spine question:** *When does a protective model become costly?*

**Trauma as stuck episodic consolidation.** Traumatic memories resist the episodic → semantic pathway. They remain primed, vivid, and intrusive because the nervous system treats the threat as unresolved. Extreme stress — high cortisol, amygdala hyperactivation — disrupts hippocampal encoding during the traumatic event itself. The hippocampus contextualizes memory in time and place, stamping events as "this happened, there, then, and is now over." Under severe threat, that contextual encoding is impaired. The memory never gets marked as past. This is why traumatic intrusions feel present-tense rather than like remembering.

**Fear generalization widens the threat net.** After trauma, the nervous system broadens the similarity radius. Stimuli that partially resemble the original threat get treated as dangerous. Immediately adaptive; maladaptive when the threat net stays wide in a changed ecology.

**The dysregulation paradox.** Recontextualization requires prefrontal function — narrative reasoning, temporal reasoning, updating. But the threat response suppresses the PFC and floods the system with cortisol and adrenaline. The moment the memory activates, the person is in the state least capable of processing it.

**Why reconsolidation-based treatments work.** Memories become labile (updateable) when retrieved, then restabilize. Treatments like EMDR and exposure therapy open that reconsolidation window — activating the memory enough to access it, then pairing it with something that signals safety before restabilization occurs. This is different from extinction, which adds new learning alongside the original trace. Reconsolidation approaches attempt to update the original trace itself.

**Sickness behavior as evolved template for depression-like states.** The behavioral profile of depression — withdrawal, anhedonia, fatigue, reduced appetite, social disengagement, slowed cognition — is also the behavioral profile of an organism fighting infection. Hart (1988) argued that sickness behavior is an evolved, centrally organized program that conserves energy for immune response. The inflammatory cytokines mediating this (IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6) also produce depression-like states when administered to humans. This reframes depression not as a broken mood regulation system but as the brain's activation of an ancient defensive shutdown program — appropriate for infection or social defeat, maladaptive when triggered chronically. *Not the complete model for depression — one mechanism among several.*

**The mesolimbic pathway as the common addiction mechanism.** All major addictive substances increase dopamine transmission in the projection from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) to the nucleus accumbens (NAcc). Each substance reaches the same pathway via different mechanisms. The NAcc encodes *wanting* (motivational salience) rather than *liking* (hedonic pleasure) — Berridge & Robinson's distinction. In late-stage addiction, the *wanting* system remains hyperresponsive while the *liking* response has adapted downward through tolerance. *Do not reduce addiction to this pathway alone — the complete picture involves prefrontal control circuits, stress systems, and distributed neuroplasticity.*

**Postpartum depression and the oxytocin/HPA axis connection.** PPD (10–15% prevalence) occurs in the context of precipitous postpartum hormonal withdrawal combined with sleep deprivation, allostatic load, and social demands. Nursing reliably triggers oxytocin release, which attenuates the HPA stress response. When breastfeeding fails or stops early, this stress-regulatory mechanism is interrupted at the time the mother's system is most taxed. Prospective studies find elevated PPD rates with early breastfeeding cessation, though the relationship is bidirectional. *Do not frame PPD as a failure of breastfeeding. Breastfeeding is not required for bonding.*
