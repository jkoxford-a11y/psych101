# Theoretical Spine
## Psychology as Biological Sense-Making Under Constraint

*Working document — not a chapter draft. Use this during chapter review and revision to check that framing is consistent and claims are appropriately bounded. Last updated: 2026-06-26.*

---

## The organizing thesis

**Psychology is biological sense-making under constraint.**

Brains receive partial information. Development tunes them to local ecologies. Attention manages the bottleneck. Prediction makes fast action possible. Emotion tags meaningful mismatch. Stress mobilizes energy when prediction implies demand or threat. Memory reconstructs to inform the present and the future; what is no longer needed as episode compresses into concept or semantic knowledge. Imagination runs on the same machinery — simulating futures the body hasn't yet committed to. Language bridges private models. Collaboration and science calibrate those models against evidence and other minds.

---

## The core claim

Everything psychological has a biological substrate, but biology is not fixed machinery — and biological explanation at the level of molecules and cells does not exhaust the psychological. The brain is living, developing, plastic tissue that grows inside a particular physical and social ecology. That ecology provides the patterned input the brain learns to process.

The brain is not in direct contact with the world. It is locked in the dark, receiving electrochemical signals from sensory systems. It does not get reality itself. It gets partial, delayed, filtered information about reality. From that, it builds a working model of what is probably out there, what matters, what is likely to happen next, and what the organism should do.

---

## The bottleneck

That model has to be compressed because the brain cannot process every sensory detail with equal priority, in time, and at available attentional cost. The constraint is a compound of bandwidth, anatomy, working memory capacity, action demands, and time pressure — not metabolism alone, though metabolic cost is real. **Attention is the control mechanism for allocating limited processing resources.**

Consciousness is narrower still. Most of perception, emotion, skilled action, and bodily regulation runs automatically. Consciousness becomes especially important when the automatic system fails: when something surprising happens, when predictions are violated, when action is uncertain.

This is why the Tesla Vision analogy works. Any vision system — biological or artificial — faces the same compression problem: raw sensory input is not yet perception. The system has to infer objects, motion, risk, and action relevance from incomplete data. But the analogy has limits. Humans are embodied, affective, developmental organisms. Our predictions are tied to action, survival, attachment, status, goals, and bodily regulation. Tesla's system has none of that.

---

## Prediction and prediction error

Prediction is one of the main solutions to the bottleneck. The brain uses priors, heuristics, concepts, priming, bodily state, emotion, social context, and current goals to decide what is probably happening. That lets the system run fast and cheap most of the time. Routine perception, skilled action, social interpretation, and habit all depend on not starting from scratch. (Rao & Ballard, 1999; Clark, 2016)

When prediction holds, processing stays automatic. When prediction fails, the system has to decide whether the error matters:

- Small, low-stakes errors can update silently without recruiting attention or emotion.
- Meaningful errors — those involving threat, reward, loss, uncertainty, social signals, attachment, or goal relevance — recruit attention more forcefully and may mobilize the body for action.

The formula is not "prediction error = emotion." The better version:

> **prediction error + meaning + action relevance = attentional priority + emotional signal + possible physiological mobilization**

**Prediction error is not the same as emotion.** Prediction error is a computational description of a mismatch. Emotion is a biological response that tags information with urgency, valence, and action relevance. They are related but not interchangeable.

---

## Emotion

Emotions are not irrational noise layered on top of cognition. They are part of the priority system — biological signals about meaning. Each emotion reflects a different situation for the organism, not a different severity of the same signal:

| Emotion | What it signals |
|---|---|
| Surprise | Something violated expectation |
| Fear | Something may threaten safety |
| Disgust | Something may contaminate, degrade, or violate boundaries |
| Curiosity | Uncertainty feels potentially resolvable and worth exploring |
| Anxiety | Future uncertainty feels threatening and unresolved |
| Frustration | Expected progress or reward is blocked |

The common structure is: *the model has encountered information with implications for action, value, threat, control, or learning.* These are not interchangeable versions of "model failed."

Emotion also does more than flag errors — it prepares action, tracks value, regulates bodily state, shapes memory consolidation, and communicates to others.

A formal account connecting emotion, allostatic regulation, and prediction exists in the active inference / free energy minimization literature (Friston et al.; Barrett's theory of constructed emotion as interoceptive inference). This framework is theoretically coherent and actively researched but is frontier-level and contested — not yet settled enough to present as consensus in an intro textbook. Use it as background rationale, not as a claim to teach.

---

## Dopamine and learning

Dopamine reward-prediction error (Schultz et al., 1997) is the clearest teaching example because it gives a concrete, well-studied mechanism: dopamine is not simply pleasure; it tracks prediction, anticipation, and the mismatch between expected and actual reward. When actual reward exceeds prediction, dopamine neurons fire; when expected reward fails to appear, firing drops below baseline.

Two cautions:

1. **The RPE model is incomplete.** A 2024 *Nature Neuroscience* perspective explicitly says the classical RPE interpretation is "probably too simple and fails to explain all the properties of phasic dopamine observed in behaving animals." Present it as the best-understood version of reward learning, not as the mechanism for prediction error in general.

2. **Don't generalize the dopamine mechanism to other emotions or stress.** The logic of mismatch detection generalizes; the dopamine mechanism does not.

## Stress and allostatic load

Stress arises when the organism predicts that demands may exceed available resources or control. The body mobilizes energy because action may be required. If the mismatch resolves, the system updates and settles. If it does not — because the threat is chronic, symbolic, social, remembered, imagined, or future-oriented — the mobilization stays on. That cumulative cost is allostatic load (McEwen & Stellar, 1993).

Prediction error is one route into stress, not the only one. The chain: *meaningful prediction error or perceived demand → emotional tagging → possible physiological mobilization → if unresolved, allostatic load.*

**Stress, emotion, prediction error, and allostatic load are related but not interchangeable.**

This is the zebra point. Zebras mostly mobilize stress physiology around immediate physical threat. Humans can activate the same ancient systems around imagined futures, remembered failures, symbolic threats, social judgment, possible rejection, financial uncertainty, moral conflict, and future selves. The human cognitive light cone — the range of events, meanings, and consequences that can matter to the organism — is vastly larger. That is our advantage and our vulnerability.

## Development: how the model gets built

Genes build a constrained range of possible nervous systems, but experience tunes the actual system within that range. Infants begin broadly prepared for many possible environments, then narrow toward the local ecology. Faces, voices, accents, attachment patterns, food, danger, norms, language, status cues, and reward histories all shape what becomes easy to perceive, predict, and feel. Perceptual narrowing is one clean example, but the principle generalizes: the developing brain adapts to the patterned world it repeatedly encounters.

This means synaptic organization — the pattern of connections the brain has built — is partly a record of developmental history. Each person's model of the world is therefore partly private. People do not simply disagree because one is rational and one is irrational. They often bring differently tuned biological systems to the same stimulus.

Current state also matters — hormones, fatigue, stress, attention, context, sleep, hunger, goals. Development sets the baseline; state modulates it.

## Concepts

A good concept is not a vocabulary word. It is a prediction machine. Once I understand "apple," I can predict seeds, taste, texture, colors, uses, memories, and category boundaries. Once students understand "reinforcement," "validity," "stress," "attachment," or "perception," the details become less arbitrary — they become inferable from the structure of the concept.

This is why teaching should not be memorization-first. Memorization without concepts is brittle. Concepts compress experience so that details become predictable. The instructor's job is to help students elaborate concepts until the details have somewhere to live.

But concepts are also partly subjective because they are built from lived experience. My "yellow," my "home," my "threat," my "fair," my "success," and my "authority" are not guaranteed to feel like yours. That private, first-person character matters — not as philosophical decoration, but as a reminder that each person's model has a subjective texture that cannot be fully accessed from outside. This is why "explaining" something to students is necessary but not sufficient.

## Language and collaboration

Language is the bridge. It lets us make private models partially public — not perfectly; words never fully transfer experience. But they let us point, compare, revise, explain, metaphorize, argue, test, and coordinate. Education, science, therapy, and collaboration all depend on this bridge.

Other people are alternate developmental histories. They are not just holders of opinions. They are differently tuned nervous systems carrying different priors, concepts, examples, and felt meanings. When students interact, their models collide. If structured well, that collision creates prediction error in a safe enough context to support updating rather than defensiveness.

That may be the deepest teaching model: create situations where students' current concepts are insufficient, but not humiliatingly so. Let them experience the limits of their model. Give them other minds to think with. Then help them build a richer concept that predicts more, explains more, and travels farther.

## The trade-off structure

The whole system is organized around compounding trade-offs:

**Speed vs. accuracy.** Fast prediction usually works, but can mislead. Deliberate processing is slower, more costly, and more accurate.

**Prediction vs. updating.** Priors are cheap. Incorporating new information costs attention and energy — which is why prediction errors have to *force* reallocation through emotion and arousal rather than happening smoothly.

**Efficiency vs. flexibility.** Automatic models save effort but can become rigid. This is why extinction is not erasure, why habits are hard to break, and why trauma can lock in threat-predictions long after the threat is gone.

**Stability vs. change.** A coherent model gives identity and confidence, but makes revision harder. Both therapy and education work against this resistance.

**Local fit vs. general truth.** Development tunes us to our ecology, but that tuning may fail elsewhere — in new cultures, novel environments, or situations the developmental history didn't prepare for.

**Private experience vs. shared reality.** Each model is unique, but language and science create enough overlap to allow comparison, coordination, and cumulative knowledge.

## Memory

Memory is a life history adaptation, not an archival system. Natural selection shaped memory toward ecological efficacy: organisms that retain what is currently relevant, shed what is no longer relevant, and can access relevant material rapidly will respond more appropriately and quickly than those carrying dead weight or searching slowly. The organizing question is not "why did we forget that?" but "what allows the organism to be maximally efficacious in its current environment?"

**Selective encoding.** The brain preferentially encodes events that violated expectation or carried emotional significance — novelty, threat, reward, social meaning. Routine events that were fully predicted generate small prediction errors and leave little trace. This is compression working correctly, not memory failing.

**Ecological relevance, not just encoding strength.** Routine events from prior ecologies are not merely weakly encoded — they become irrelevant as the social and physical context that gave them meaning dissolves. A morning routine from junior high was calibrated to an ecology that no longer exists. Even if retrievable, it would support no current prediction and no current action. What persists is what still connects to the present organism's world: transferable skills, conceptual structures, attachment patterns, threat calibrations, unresolved states.

**Priming keeps the relevant past accessible.** The things most likely to be needed in the current ecology should be most rapidly accessible. Priming is the mechanism by which current context keeps relevant material activated for fast response. The model is always current; memory contributes to it only where the past is still ecologically live.

**Episodic memory compresses into semantic memory.** This is the normal consolidation pathway. Episodes accumulate and compress into generative concepts — schemas, skills, semantic knowledge — that predict without requiring retrieval of individual instances. The episodic scaffolding then dissolves because only the compressed product is needed. This is also the arc of good teaching: students begin with episodic traces ("I read this example") and instruction drives compression into a concept that travels and predicts.

**Trauma as stuck consolidation.** Traumatic memories resist the episodic→semantic pathway. The episode stays primed and vivid because the nervous system treats the threat as unresolved. Consolidation into a managed semantic structure requires something like "this situation is understood and handled." If that signal never arrives, the specific episode remains maximally accessible for rapid activation. Current environmental cues that share structure with the original threat keep triggering the old model. The organism is doing exactly what it evolved to do — keeping a high-stakes unresolved situation primed — but the calibration is stuck. This is why reconsolidation-based treatments work on the episode itself rather than just adding new knowledge alongside it.

### Prologue

**The teaching model as episodic→semantic arc.** Students arrive with episodic traces — isolated facts, examples half-remembered, intuitions without structure. Good instruction drives compression: the episodes accumulate until a generative concept emerges that predicts rather than lists. The instructor's job is not to transfer information but to create conditions for compression. A concept is ready when a student can use it to predict something they haven't been told.

**Concepts give details somewhere to live.** Before a concept is in place, facts are arbitrary and fragile. After it is in place, facts become inferable consequences of the structure. This is why building the concept first — even roughly — makes subsequent detail easier to retain. The detail has a home.

**Students as alternate developmental histories.** When students talk to each other, differently tuned models collide. That collision is itself a prediction error — the student encounters a perspective their current model didn't generate. If the context is safe enough, that error drives updating rather than defensiveness. Structured peer discussion is not a break from learning; it is a mechanism for model revision.

**The zebra question as pedagogy.** Is a zebra black with white stripes or white with black stripes? The question looks trivial but forces students to ask: what evidence would count? Embryology, evolutionary history, developmental sequence, related species, function. The answer matters less than the process — the question creates a situation where the student's current concept is insufficient and has to be rebuilt from multiple converging sources.

### Ch. 3 — Neuroscience

**Dopamine RPE: use as illustration, not mechanism.** The Schultz (1997) reward prediction error model is the clearest mechanistic example of mismatch detection driving learning. Present it as such. A 2024 *Nature Neuroscience* perspective (Langdon et al.) explicitly says the classical RPE interpretation is "probably too simple and fails to explain all the properties of phasic dopamine observed in behaving animals" — dopamine also tracks sensory salience, novelty, movement, and action selection in ways the original model doesn't capture. The teaching value is the principle (expected ≠ actual → update), not the claim that dopamine *is* the prediction error signal for the whole brain.

### Ch. 6 — Learning

**Extinction as prediction error, not erasure.** Extinction is not the deletion of the original association. It is new inhibitory learning layered on top — a new prediction ("CS no longer predicts US") that competes with the original. The original trace remains. This is why spontaneous recovery occurs, why renewal occurs when context changes, and why extinguished responses can return under stress. The organism's model was updated, but the prior was not erased — it was suppressed by a more recent prediction. This is the prediction/updating trade-off made concrete: updating is expensive, partial, and context-dependent.

### Ch. 7 — Memory

**The life history argument.** Memory is a fitness-relevant adaptation, not an archival system. The organizing question is not "why did we forget that?" but "what allows the organism to be maximally efficacious in its current environment?" Natural selection shaped memory toward ecological efficacy: fast access to what is relevant now, shedding what is no longer relevant, compressing what is useful but doesn't need to be episodic.

**Ecological relevance, not just encoding strength.** The standard account emphasizes encoding — strongly encoded things are remembered, weakly encoded things are forgotten. This is true but incomplete. A morning routine from junior high may have been adequately encoded at the time, but it has zero action relevance now. The social and physical ecology it was calibrated to no longer exists. Forgetting it is not a failure; it is the model staying current. What persists is what still connects to the present organism's world: transferable skills, conceptual structures, attachment patterns, threat calibrations, unresolved states.

**Priming as the fast-access mechanism.** The things most likely to be needed in the current ecology should be most rapidly accessible. Priming is how current context keeps relevant material activated. It is the memory system's equivalent of the prediction/compression solution: rather than searching from scratch, the relevant past is already partially active. Priming is context-sensitive, which is why the same person retrieves different material in different states, contexts, and social situations.

**Episodic→semantic compression as the normal pathway.** Episodes accumulate and compress into generative concepts — schemas, skills, semantic knowledge — that predict without requiring retrieval of individual instances. The episodic scaffolding then dissolves because only the compressed product is needed. This is not memory failure; it is compression working correctly. The morning routine episodes from junior high consolidated into "how mornings work" and then the episodic traces faded. This is also the arc of good teaching: students begin with episodic traces ("I read this example") and instruction drives compression into a concept that travels.

### Ch. 12 — Emotion, Stress & Coping

**The cognitive light cone as the mechanism for human-specific chronic stress.** Zebras mobilize stress physiology around immediate physical threat. Humans can activate the same ancient systems around imagined futures, remembered failures, symbolic threats, social judgment, financial uncertainty, moral conflict, and future selves. The human cognitive light cone — the range of events, meanings, and consequences that can matter to the organism — is vastly larger than any other animal's. Simulation capacity that evolved for planning becomes a vulnerability when it keeps the threat-mobilization system running in the absence of any physical threat. Chronic stress is what happens when the simulation machinery runs on threatening futures that never resolve.

**Stress as mobilization around perceived demand.** The mechanism: the organism predicts that demands may exceed available resources or control. That prediction triggers physiological mobilization — energy release, attention narrowing, immune and reproductive deprioritization. Adaptive for acute, resolvable threats. Costly when the mismatch persists, repeats, or cannot be acted on. Allostatic load is the cumulative cost of mobilization that never fully settles.

### Ch. 13 — Disorders & Therapy

**Trauma as stuck episodic consolidation.** Traumatic memories resist the normal episodic→semantic pathway. They remain primed, vivid, and intrusive — not because they were encoded more strongly in the ordinary sense, but because the nervous system treats the threat as unresolved. Consolidation into a managed semantic structure requires something like "this situation is understood and handled." Without that signal, the specific episode remains maximally accessible for rapid activation. The organism is doing exactly what it evolved to do — keeping a high-stakes unresolved situation primed — but the calibration is stuck because the ecology changed while the model did not.

**The encoding failure is upstream of the consolidation failure.** Extreme stress — high cortisol, amygdala hyperactivation — disrupts hippocampal encoding during the traumatic event itself. The hippocampus is what contextualizes memory in time and place, stamping events as "this happened, there, then, and is now over." Under severe threat, that contextual encoding is impaired. The memory never gets marked as past. This is why traumatic intrusions feel present-tense rather than like remembering — not confusion, but an absent past-tense marker. The event was never encoded as completed.

**Fear generalization widens the threat net.** After trauma, the nervous system doesn't keep only the original cues flagged — it broadens the similarity radius. Stimuli that partially resemble the original threat get treated as dangerous. Immediately adaptive (better to over-detect than miss a real threat) but maladaptive when the threat net stays wide in a changed ecology. The organism is detecting threats calibrated to a world that no longer applies.

**The dysregulation paradox.** The triggered threat response is the worst state for integration. Recontextualization requires prefrontal function — narrative reasoning, temporal reasoning, updating. But the threat response suppresses the PFC and floods the system with cortisol and adrenaline. The moment the memory activates, the person is in the state least capable of processing it. This is why talking about trauma without concurrent regulation often doesn't help — cognitive recontextualization is being attempted while the system that enables it is offline.

**Interpersonal trauma as a special case.** When the original threat involved another person — violation, betrayal, abuse — the threat model incorporates social signals: facial expressions, tones of voice, proximity, power differentials. This is particularly costly because close relationships, which should provide the safety needed for regulation and updating, share structure with the original threat. The very context most needed for recovery partially resembles the context that caused the damage.

**Why reconsolidation-based treatments work.** Memories become labile (updateable) when retrieved, then restabilize. Treatments like EMDR and exposure therapy open that reconsolidation window — activating the memory enough to access it, then pairing it with something that signals safety or contradicts the original threat response, before restabilization occurs. This is different from extinction, which adds new learning alongside the original trace. Reconsolidation approaches attempt to update the original trace itself. The distinction matters: extinction can be renewed and context-dependent; a successfully reconsolidated memory is revised at the source.

## What not to claim

- Do not present active inference / free energy minimization as consensus neuroscience. It is a productive theoretical framework, not a settled account.
- Do not say the brain *is* a prediction machine as if that settles the debate. Say the brain *uses* prediction as a major organizing principle.
- Do not collapse prediction error, dopamine, emotion, and stress into a single mechanism. Name the chain and its links separately.
- Do not imply the Tesla analogy is mechanistically accurate — only that it illustrates the bottleneck problem.
- Do not say "full sensory processing is metabolically impossible" — the bottleneck is attention, bandwidth, time, and action demands, not metabolism alone.
- Do not generalize the dopamine RPE mechanism to other emotional systems or to stress physiology.
- Do not invoke qualia as a teaching concept — the phenomenological point (each person's model has a first-person character) can be made without entering philosophy of mind disputes.
- Do not say "everything psychological is biological" without adding that biological explanation does not exhaust the psychological.
