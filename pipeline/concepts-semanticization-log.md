## Starting intuition

Conceptual knowledge may be more foundational than a standard cognition chapter suggests.

The emerging idea:

> Concepts are semanticized experience. They are compressed products of repeated episodes that become portable prediction tools.

This seems to connect many parts of psychology: Piaget, schemas, prototypes, exemplars, semantic memory, stereotypes, Dunning-Kruger, expertise, therapy, neural nets, AI, and teaching itself.

A concept is not just a vocabulary word. It is a structure that lets the organism infer more than it directly observes.

## Core formulation

Repeated experience leaves episodic traces. Across time, some of those traces compress into semantic structures: concepts, schemas, scripts, skills, categories, expectations, and causal models.

The original episodes do not all need to remain accessible. The useful product is the compressed structure.

Examples:

- A child does not need to remember every apple they have seen to understand “apple.”
- A student does not need to remember every class example to understand “reinforcement.”
- A psychologist does not need to remember every patient or study to understand “validity,” “stress,” “attachment,” or “stereotype.”
- A teacher’s textbook may itself be the semanticized product of years of teaching episodes: student confusions, classroom examples, failed explanations, successful metaphors, and revised concepts.

Working line:

> Semanticization is the mind turning lived episodes into portable prediction tools.

## Piaget connection

Piaget’s schema notion maps well onto this, but it should not be reduced to prototypes.

A schema is broader than a prototype. It is a working model for interpreting and acting in the world. It may include perceptual expectations, motor routines, causal beliefs, social scripts, emotional predictions, and language.

Assimilation = using the existing schema to interpret a new case.  
Accommodation = revising the schema when the new case does not fit.

In the spine language:

- assimilation = prediction using the current compressed model
- accommodation = model revision after meaningful prediction error

The “doggie/deer” example matters because it exposes the whole mechanism. The child has a coarse schema or prototype-like category. It works well enough to generalize but poorly enough to overgeneralize. Feedback forces differentiation.

Possible sequence:

1. Episode: child encounters one dog.
2. Early schema: furry, four-legged, moves, maybe friendly, “doggie.”
3. Prototype-like compression: a rough typical animal category emerges.
4. Overextension: deer, cat, or horse may be treated as doggie.
5. Accommodation: dog, deer, cat, horse split into differentiated concepts.
6. Mature semantic map: dog becomes connected to pet, mammal, breed, barking, leash, danger/friendliness, domestication, wolf ancestry, family memories, and cultural meanings.

Important guardrail:

Do not say “prototypes become concepts” as a fixed developmental law. Better:

> Early schemas are often prototype-heavy. Mature concepts are differentiated semantic structures that can include prototypes, exemplars, rules, causal beliefs, scripts, and language.

## Prototypes and exemplars

Prototype theory and exemplar theory describe different ways concepts can be represented and used.

Prototype:

- compressed “average” or typical case
- useful for speed
- explains typicality effects
- risky for edge cases

Exemplar:

- stored specific examples
- useful for exceptions and variability
- important for expertise
- helps explain why atypical cases can still be categorized

Mature concepts likely use both. The prototype gives fast orientation. Exemplars preserve detail and exceptions. Expertise makes the semantic neighborhood denser and more differentiated.

Working idea:

> Novices often have thin prototypes. Experts have dense semantic spaces.

## Semantic maps and Huth et al.

Huth et al. helps because it gives a modern biological image for conceptual knowledge.

Meaning does not appear to be stored like a dictionary, a single node, or one cortical “spot” per concept. Their natural-speech fMRI work suggests semantic information is distributed across cortex in structured patterns. Broad semantic domains include social, emotional, mental, visual, tactile, numeric, temporal, locational, abstract, professional, communal, and violent content.

Teaching interpretation:

> Concepts are distributed patterns in semantic space.

Guardrails:

- Do not say the brain literally stores concepts as word embeddings.
- Do not say there is one place for each concept.
- Do not overgeneralize from seven subjects.
- Do not ignore culture and experience; shared maps may reflect shared biology, shared language, shared education, shared culture, or all of these.

Best usable version:

> Semantic maps show that meaning is distributed, structured, and partially shared across people. They do not eliminate individual history, culture, embodiment, or emotion.

## Connection to teaching

This may be one of the deepest teaching models in the project.

Students arrive with fragments:

- a definition
- a phrase
- a personal example
- a misconception
- an image from class
- a story from a friend
- a half-remembered demonstration

Good instruction creates conditions for compression. The fragments become a generative concept.

A concept is ready when the student can use it to predict something they have not been directly told.

Memorization without concepts is brittle because facts have nowhere to live. Concepts give details somewhere to live.

This suggests a teaching arc:

1. Start with a misconception or familiar example.
2. Give several contrasting examples.
3. Force classification or prediction.
4. Let the student overgeneralize.
5. Create safe prediction error.
6. Help them differentiate the concept.
7. Test transfer to a new case.

## Stereotypes

Stereotypes can be explained as social concepts built from biased examples.

This is powerful because it avoids treating stereotypes as cognitively exotic. They are ordinary categorization machinery applied to people under bad sampling, cultural repetition, emotional salience, and power.

A stereotype is a prototype-like social category that feels like knowledge because categories always feel like knowledge from the inside.

Possible line:

> A stereotype is a social concept built from biased, salient, emotionally weighted, or culturally supplied examples.

This connects to:

- social psychology
- prejudice
- availability
- confirmation bias
- attribution
- development
- media exposure
- AI bias

## Dunning-Kruger

Dunning-Kruger can be framed as poor conceptual calibration.

Novices do not just lack facts. They lack the differentiated concept needed to judge quality.

A weak writer may not recognize weak writing because they lack the concept of strong writing. A weak reasoner may not detect weak reasoning because the evaluative concept is underdeveloped.

Possible line:

> You need a sufficiently developed concept to evaluate your own performance within that conceptual domain.

This connects to metacognition, expertise, intelligence, education, and AI literacy.

## Expertise

Expertise is conceptual differentiation.

Experts do not merely know more. They organize the domain differently.

They see:

- finer distinctions
- better basic-level categories
- deeper causal structure
- more relevant exceptions
- more diagnostic features
- less superficial similarity

Example: novice sees “bird.” Expert sees species, sex, age, ecological niche, behavior, call, seasonal pattern, and exception cases.

Working line:

> Expertise changes the grain size of perception because it changes the structure of the concept.

## Therapy and maladaptive schemas

Therapy can be framed as schema revision.

A person may carry concepts of self, threat, safety, attachment, responsibility, control, authority, intimacy, or future that were calibrated to a past ecology.

These concepts may have once been adaptive. Later they become maladaptive because the ecology changes but the model persists.

Therapy often creates conditions for accommodation:

- old prediction activates
- new experience violates it
- safety is sufficient to prevent defensive shutdown
- the model updates

This connects therapy to the broader spine: prediction, emotion, memory, trauma, and semanticization.

## AI connection

AI is useful because it makes semantic compression visible.

Language models learn high-dimensional statistical structure from many examples. They generalize from patterns rather than storing a simple dictionary. Embeddings are machine analogues of semantic neighborhoods.

Useful analogy:

- examples train representations
- representations support generalization
- biased input produces biased concepts
- similar cases cluster
- boundaries can be fuzzy
- prompts can activate regions of semantic space

Limits:

- AI does not have human embodiment
- AI does not have childhood
- AI does not have survival stakes
- AI does not have affective meaning
- AI does not have social accountability in the human sense
- AI semantic structure is not identical to human conceptual understanding

Best formulation:

> AI helps students see that concepts can be learned from examples and represented as relations in a space. Human concepts are also relational and learned from experience, but they are embodied, emotional, developmental, social, and tied to action.

## Chapter 9 implication

Chapter 9 should not be treated as just “concepts and categories.”

It may be one of the load-bearing chapters for the whole textbook.

Possible chapter argument:

> The mind does not store the world as definitions. It builds concepts from experience, uses them to make fast inferences, and revises them when experience forces differentiation.

Two-week memory target:

> Concepts are compressed experience. They let us predict more than we directly observe, but they also inherit the limits and biases of the examples that formed them.

## Possible Learning Lab

Title: Semantic Map Lab — Build a Concept from Examples

Students choose a concept:

- chair
- bird
- intelligence
- stress
- disorder
- fairness
- addiction
- reinforcement
- stereotype

Steps:

1. List examples.
2. Mark typical and atypical cases.
3. Create two axes that organize the examples.
4. Place examples in semantic space.
5. Compare with a peer.
6. Revise the axes.
7. Ask AI for a different map only after effort.
8. Reflect: what did the AI notice, miss, or overgeneralize?

Learning target:

Students experience that concepts are not definitions. They are structured spaces built from examples, goals, experience, and cultural context.

This lab could be reused across chapters.
