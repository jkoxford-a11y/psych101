Chapter 11 · Social & Personality

Personality

Common Belief

Most of us describe people using trait language without thinking twice about it: she's introverted, he's conscientious, they're agreeable. The assumption embedded in that language is that traits are stable, cross-situational forces — that an introverted person will behave introvertedly in classrooms, at parties, at job interviews, and in traffic.

Research says this is partly right and significantly wrong, and the tension between those two conclusions has driven personality psychology for more than half a century.

In 1968, Walter Mischel reviewed the literature on whether measured personality traits predicted real behavior. His finding was sobering: the correlation between a trait score and a single behavioral observation was typically around .30. This is the personality coefficient: modest, and accounting for less than 10% of the variance in any single behavioral observation. Mischel argued that situational context was driving far more behavioral variance than trait theory acknowledged.

But before concluding that traits barely matter, hold on. A correlation of .30 is not trivial — it is similar in magnitude to the effect sizes produced by some of social psychology's most famous experimental manipulations (Funder & Ozer, 1983). And for consequential outcomes accumulated over many situations — your semester grade, your job tenure, your long-term relationship quality — traits turn out to predict quite a lot (Funder & Ozer, 2019). The right conclusion is not "traits are meaningless" but "traits predict patterns across time and situations better than single acts on a given Tuesday."

A better way to think about traits is not "this person always behaves this way," but "this person tends to behave this way, especially when behavior is averaged across many situations, and unless the situation strongly pushes otherwise."

Section 1: Psychoanalytic Approaches to Personality

Sigmund Freud's theory of personality dominated psychology for the first half of the twentieth century, and its influence on popular culture — the idea of the unconscious, of repression, of defense mechanisms — is still pervasive even where the underlying science has not held up. Understanding what Freud proposed, and what modern research has retained versus discarded, is essential intellectual housekeeping for any psychology student.

The Structural Model

Freud divided the mind into three interacting structures, each operating according to a different logic (Freud, 1923).

The id is entirely unconscious and operates on the pleasure principle — it seeks immediate gratification of biological drives (hunger, sex, aggression) with no consideration for reality or consequences.

The ego develops in early childhood as the infant learns that the world does not immediately comply with the id's demands. It operates on the reality principle — it mediates between the id's desires and the constraints of the external world, seeking gratification through realistic, socially acceptable means. The ego is mostly conscious, though some of its activity is not.

The superego develops later, roughly during the preschool years, as the child internalizes parental and societal standards of right and wrong. It generates guilt when the ego violates its standards, and pride when it meets them.

Freud also distinguished levels of awareness: the conscious (what you are attending to now), the preconscious (information retrievable but not currently attended to), and the unconscious (drives, memories, and conflicts that influence behavior but are actively kept from awareness). Freud described the unconscious as "a chaos, a cauldron full of seething excitations" — a source of energy that the ego must continually manage (Freud, 1933/1964).

Defense Mechanisms

Because the unconscious contains threatening material, the ego deploys defense mechanisms to manage that threat (A. Freud, 1936). These are largely unconscious strategies for distorting or denying reality to protect the self from anxiety.

Common defense mechanisms include:

  • Repression — involuntary motivated forgetting of threatening material (the foundational mechanism in Freud's system)
  • Denial — refusing to acknowledge a threatening reality
  • Projection — attributing one's own unacceptable feelings to others ("I'm not angry — she's the one who's hostile")
  • Reaction formation — expressing the opposite of what one unconsciously feels
  • Rationalization — constructing plausible-sounding justifications for behavior actually driven by other motives
  • Displacement — redirecting an unacceptable impulse toward a safer target
  • Sublimation — channeling an unacceptable impulse into a socially productive activity (Freud considered this the healthiest defense)

The concept of defense mechanisms has fared better in modern psychology than most of Freud's theory. Social cognition research documents that people regularly engage in motivated reasoning, self-serving attributions, and unconscious distortions of information that serve self-protective functions (Baumeister, Dale, & Sommer, 1998). The specific hydraulic energy model Freud proposed is not supported; the behavioral phenomena are real.

One important caution: the concept of repression as a defense mechanism does not imply that suppressed memories can be accurately recovered decades later with therapeutic assistance. The research on "recovered memories" and the mechanisms underlying them is distinct from, and should not be conflated with, defense mechanism theory.

Psychosexual Stages

Freud proposed that personality development proceeds through a sequence of psychosexual stages, each centered on a different erogenous zone (Freud, 1905). In the oral stage (birth to ~18 months), pleasure centers on feeding. In the anal stage (~18 months to 3 years), the focus shifts to control and toilet training. In the phallic stage (~3 to 6 years), Freud proposed that children develop unconscious sexual attraction toward the opposite-sex parent and rivalry with the same-sex parent — the Oedipus complex — resolving it through identification with the same-sex parent. Later writers, including Jung, used the term "Electra complex" for the proposed female counterpart; Freud himself preferred to retain the term Oedipus complex for both sexes, and his account of female development in this stage was considerably more complicated and contested even within psychoanalytic theory. The latency stage (~6 to puberty) involves relative suppression of sexual impulses, and the genital stage (puberty onward) involves mature relationships.

Evaluation: The specific stage sequence, claimed timing, and the Oedipal theory have received very little empirical support and are not considered scientifically established by modern developmental psychology. What Freud identified correctly — that early experience shapes later development, that some psychological processes operate outside awareness, and that motivation is not always what it appears to be — subsequent research has supported using better methods, while discarding the specific hydraulic machinery.

Section 2: Humanistic Approaches to Personality

The humanistic movement emerged partly as a reaction against psychoanalysis and behaviorism — both of which, humanistic psychologists argued, gave a distorted picture of human nature. Carl Rogers proposed instead that people are fundamentally growth-oriented, and that personality is best understood in terms of the self and its striving toward fulfillment.

Rogers and the Self-Concept

Rogers placed the self-concept at the center of personality — the organized collection of perceptions, beliefs, and values that define who we think we are (Rogers, 1961). The self-concept is evaluative and motivating. We behave in ways consistent with our self-concept, and we feel distress when our experience conflicts with it (a phenomenon Rogers called incongruence).

The self-concept is shaped by the conditions of worth imposed by significant others. When parents communicate that love is contingent on specific behaviors — "I'm proud of you when you perform well," "I love you if you don't embarrass me" — children learn to filter their experience through those conditions, suppressing feelings and perceptions that threaten acceptance.

Worked example: A student who has internalized "I am only lovable when I perform well" may chronically ignore exhaustion, hide academic failure, and interpret ordinary feedback as evidence of personal worthlessness. This is not a rational response to the objective situation — it is a self-concept organized around conditions of worth, which distorts every incoming experience through that lens. Rogers would say this student has low incongruence tolerance and a conditional self-concept, not simply "low self-esteem."

The antidote, in Rogers's view, is unconditional positive regard — acceptance not contingent on performance or compliance. When people receive this — from a therapist, a partner, a parent — they can drop defensive distortions and engage more honestly with their own experience. Rogers called the endpoint of this process the fully functioning person: someone open to experience, living in the present, trusting their own judgment, and in the process of continual growth.

Note

Do Not Confuse: Self-Concept and Self-Esteem Self-concept is descriptive — your answer to "who am I?" It includes beliefs about your traits, roles, abilities, and values. Self-esteem is evaluative — your answer to "how good am I?" You can have an accurate self-concept and low self-esteem, or an inflated self-concept and high self-esteem. Rogers's theory is primarily about self-concept accuracy (are you perceiving your own experience honestly?) rather than self-esteem valence (how positively do you evaluate yourself?).

Evaluation: Rogers's insight that the quality of being accepted shapes self-concept development is consistent with subsequent attachment and clinical research. On the therapy-outcome side, empirical work finds that therapeutic alliance, positive regard, and empathy all show reliable relationships with treatment outcome. Positive regard specifically shows a small-to-moderate association (g = .28) across 64 studies (Farber, Suzuki, & Lynch, 2018); therapist empathy predicts outcome at a similar magnitude (mean weighted r = .28 across 82 samples; Elliott, Bohart, Watson, & Murphy, 2018). These findings support the relational dimension of Rogers's theory, not the full theoretical architecture — relationship variables are important ingredients in therapy, but they are not the only active ingredients, and they do not validate every detail of Rogers's humanistic framework.

Section 3: Trait Approaches to Personality

Trait theory takes a more descriptive and empirical approach: rather than proposing a grand theory of development and unconscious dynamics, it asks what the basic structural units of personality are and whether they can be reliably measured.

Gordon Allport, one of the founders of modern trait psychology, argued that traits are real neuropsychic structures that cause consistent patterns of behavior across situations (Allport, 1937). He distinguished cardinal traits (a single dominant theme organizing an entire life — rare), central traits (the five to ten characteristics that define a person most distinctively), and secondary traits (situation-specific preferences). Most subsequent research has focused on identifying the basic dimensions of personality that are reliable, valid, and generalizable.

The Big Five (OCEAN)

The most empirically robust answer to "what are the basic dimensions of personality" is the Big Five — five broad trait dimensions that emerge consistently from factor-analytic studies of personality-descriptive language and behavior (Goldberg, 1990; Costa & McCrae, 1992):

Trait High pole Low pole Common misconception Everyday example
Openness Curious, creative, imaginative Conventional, routine-preferring, concrete "Open" just means agreeable or accepting of others' views Seeks out unfamiliar cuisines, art, ideas; drawn to novelty
Conscientiousness Organized, reliable, self-disciplined Impulsive, disorganized, unreliable Just means "hardworking" Maintains a calendar; arrives on time; follows through without reminders
Extraversion Sociable, assertive, energized by others Reserved, solitary, energized by solitude Introversion = shyness; Extraversion = being outgoing Feels energized after social events rather than drained; seeks out company
Agreeableness Cooperative, trusting, empathic Competitive, suspicious, critical Disagreeable people are necessarily rude or hostile Prioritizes harmony; gives benefit of the doubt; avoids conflict
Neuroticism High negative affect, anxious, irritable Emotionally stable, calm, resilient "Neurotic" in the casual-insult sense Experiences worry, mood shifts, and emotional reactivity more frequently and intensely
Note

Do Not Confuse: Trait Dimension vs. Personality Type The Big Five does not sort people into five kinds of people. Each trait is a dimension — a continuum, not a box. You are not "an extravert" or "an introvert" as a fixed category; you fall somewhere on a continuous scale, and your position on that scale still leaves room for situational variation in any given moment. Many popular frameworks (including the widely used MBTI) assign people to binary categories; the Big Five explicitly rejects that approach. This distinction matters because thinking in types leads to the false belief that all "introverts" behave the same, and that a trait score alone predicts a specific situation.

Key properties of the Big Five:

Reliability. The five factors replicate across different questionnaire measures, different rater perspectives (self-report vs. peer-report), and different samples. Test-retest reliability is good, particularly over intervals of less than a year.

Cross-cultural generalizability. The five factors emerge, with some variation, across studies conducted in many different countries and language groups — suggesting they are not an artifact of English-language personality description. The replication is not perfect, and some researchers argue additional or different factors are needed in certain cultural contexts (McCrae & Costa, 1997). Some lexical studies in non-English-speaking cultures recover a sixth factor, Honesty-Humility, giving rise to the HEXACO model (Ashton & Lee, 2007) — a reminder that personality structures are empirical models, not universal constants.

Heritability. Twin studies show Big Five traits are moderately heritable — estimates generally range from 40% to 60% across factors, with shared family environment accounting for surprisingly little variance (Jang, Livesley, & Vernon, 1996). This does not mean traits are fixed at birth; it means that genetic differences between people contribute meaningfully to personality differences between people, in interaction with environment.

Predictive validity. Big Five traits predict real-world outcomes. Conscientiousness is among the most consistent predictors of academic and occupational performance. Neuroticism predicts mental health vulnerability. Extraversion predicts social relationship quality and leadership emergence.

Section 4: The Person-Situation Debate

Mischel's Challenge

Walter Mischel's 1968 book Personality and Assessment presented a challenge that reoriented personality psychology. After reviewing research on whether personality traits predicted behavior, Mischel found that the correlation between a trait measure and a single observation of relevant behavior was typically around .30 — the personality coefficient. He argued that situational factors were driving far more behavioral variance than trait theory acknowledged.

This conclusion aligned with the broader situationist movement in social psychology — the same tradition that produced Milgram's obedience findings and Asch's conformity studies, all demonstrating that ordinary people behave in surprising ways under situational pressure.

Before concluding from Mischel that traits are unimportant, two corrections matter. First, Funder and Ozer (1983) showed that the famous social psychology experiments Mischel was implicitly contrasting with trait research — Milgram's obedience study, Darley and Latané's bystander effect study — when converted to correlation coefficients, yield effect sizes in roughly the same range as trait-behavior correlations. A personality coefficient of .30 is not obviously smaller than a "situationist" effect of similar magnitude. Second, and more fundamentally, r = .30 should not be dismissed as trivial. For consequential outcomes — predicting who will graduate, who will stay employed, who will stay married — small correlations accumulated over time and repeated observations can be practically important (Funder & Ozer, 2019).

Mischel's point was not that personality is meaningless. It was that traits predict single acts with limited accuracy, and that situational factors are underestimated in everyday explanation.

Classic Study: The Good Samaritan Experiment

Darley and Batson (1973) conducted one of the most striking demonstrations of situational power over expected behavior. The study was conducted at Princeton Theological Seminary — a setting where you might expect religious values and personality to be strong behavioral predictors.

Participants (seminary students) were asked to prepare a short talk and walk across campus to deliver it. Half were preparing a talk on the parable of the Good Samaritan; the other half on an unrelated topic. Within this, some were told they had plenty of time; others were told they were already late.

On the way across campus, participants encountered a confederate slumped in a doorway, apparently in need of help.

The results were stark. Being in a hurry was the dominant predictor of helping: roughly 10% of participants in the high-hurry condition stopped to help, compared to roughly 63% in the low-hurry condition. What the participant was preparing to speak about — including whether they were literally thinking about the Good Samaritan parable — made essentially no difference. Prior measures of religious orientation did not predict helping in this context.

The lesson is not that character is irrelevant. It is that a strong situational constraint (being late) overrides expected dispositional tendencies in any given situation. Designing situations to make helping easy — removing time pressure, making need salient, reducing diffusion of responsibility — may produce more helping than selecting people with "good character" and expecting traits to override situational obstacles.

A concrete aggregation example

Consider conscientiousness and class attendance. A conscientious student will on average attend class more reliably than a less conscientious one. But whether they show up on any given Tuesday depends heavily on whether they're sick, whether there's a family crisis, whether their car starts, whether there's a blizzard. The trait predicts the semester pattern; the situation explains Tuesday. This is why Mischel's "correlation of .30" is not a verdict on the importance of personality in general — it is a statement about the noise in predicting single behavioral acts.

The Modern Interactionist Resolution

Most personality psychologists now hold an interactionist position: behavior is a joint function of person characteristics and situational factors, and the two interact in complex ways (Mischel & Shoda, 1995).

Several qualifications on the situationist challenge emerge from subsequent research:

Aggregation over time and situations. Trait measures correlate more strongly with averaged behavior across many situations than with behavior in any single observation. Average over the semester, not just Tuesday.

Weak vs. strong situations. In strong situations — clear norms, obvious behavioral expectations, significant consequences for noncompliance — traits matter less. In weak situations — ambiguous, unstructured, without clear norms — traits matter more. Darley and Batson created a strong situation; in an unstructured social gathering, extraversion predicts behavior well. Figure 11.2 illustrates this interaction.

Behavioral signatures. Mischel and Shoda (1995) proposed that personality is not unconditional cross-situational consistency but if-then patterns: "if in a situation of type A, then behavior X; if in type B, then behavior Y." These conditional behavioral signatures are consistent across time and distinctive across individuals — they just look like inconsistency if you expect unconditional uniformity.

Note

AI Connection: Do AI Models Have Personality? Personality psychologists define traits as stable, cross-situational behavioral tendencies. By this definition, it is worth asking whether AI systems have something analogous — users frequently report that different AI models seem to have characteristic styles: one more formal, another more likely to agree, another more prone to hedging. Researchers have begun to probe this. When large language models are assigned Big Five personas and asked to complete personality inventories, their responses show coherent profiles — word usage patterns and narrative choices in stories reflect the assigned traits (Jiang et al., 2024). This looks like trait-like consistency. The parallel: A model assigned a high-Extraversion persona scores higher on sociability items and produces more outward-focused writing than a model assigned a high-Introversion persona. There is a kind of cross-situational coherence within an assigned persona. The simplification: But this coherence is more situationally fragile than human trait consistency. LLM "personality" profiles can be sensitive to how questionnaire items are worded, what system prompt is in place, and whether a persona has been explicitly assigned — the "trait" tracks the prompt more than it tracks something internal. Human traits show cross-situational consistency without being told which trait to express. The breakdown: The deeper problem is what is missing. Human personality traits involve a self-concept — a psychological structure the person identifies with, and that motivates behavioral consistency even when consistency is costly. An honest person tells the truth partly because dishonesty conflicts with who they are. An AI system has no self-concept. Its trait-like tendencies are regularities in parameter space, not identity-invested commitments. Change the prompt, and the "personality" changes — with no experience of self-betrayal, because there is no self to betray. That is not what personality traits do.

Section 5: Personality Assessment

Measuring personality raises the same reliability and validity questions introduced in Chapter 2. Personality assessment instruments fall broadly into two categories: objective measures and projective measures.

Category Example Strength Main validity concern
Objective self-report NEO-PI-R (Big Five) Standardized, reliable, scalable, validated against real outcomes Self-insight limits; social desirability effects
Clinical inventory MMPI-2 / MMPI-3 Validity scales, empirical keying, established clinical norms Not designed for casual personality profiling
Projective Rorschach (R-PAS), TAT May surface themes not accessible to self-report Reliability and validity vary sharply by variable and scoring system
Online personality quiz Social media quizzes Engaging Rarely meets psychometric standards; typically produces types, not dimensions

Objective measures present standardized stimuli with structured response formats and score responses according to objective criteria. The Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI), originally developed by Hathaway and McKinley (1943) and substantially revised (MMPI-2; MMPI-3), is among the better-supported clinical personality assessment instruments in common use. It includes hundreds of true-false statements and produces scores on clinical scales (depression, schizophrenia, antisocial personality, and others) plus validity scales designed to detect response patterns suggesting malingering, underreporting, or inconsistent responding. The MMPI's empirical keying approach — scales developed by identifying items that distinguished clinical groups from controls, not by theoretical reasoning — is a methodological strength.

The NEO-PI-R (Costa & McCrae, 1992) is the dominant objective measure in research and non-clinical assessment contexts, measuring the Big Five factors and their sub-facets with strong psychometric properties.

Projective measures present ambiguous stimuli and ask for open-ended responses — on the assumption that interpretations will "project" personality characteristics. The two most familiar are:

The Rorschach inkblot test (Rorschach, 1921) presents ten symmetrical inkblots and asks respondents to describe what they see. The Rorschach's scientific status has been contested for decades. A large meta-analytic review found that some individual Rorschach variables — particularly those assessing externally observable characteristics like cognitive processing and thought disorder — show meaningful validity (mean r = .27 for externally assessed criteria across 53 meta-analyses; Mihura, Meyer, Dumitrascu, & Bombel, 2013). Other variables, particularly those assessing personality characteristics via introspective criteria, show much weaker or inconsistent validity. The overall picture is that Rorschach validity is not uniformly terrible but is not uniformly supported either — it depends critically on which variable, which scoring system, and which application. Wood et al. (2003) reached a more skeptical conclusion about broader clinical applications. Students should resist both blanket dismissal and blanket endorsement.

The Thematic Apperception Test (TAT) (Murray, 1943) asks respondents to tell stories about ambiguous pictures of people in various situations. It has shown reasonable validity for assessing specific motivational themes — particularly achievement motivation in McClelland's work — but lower validity for the broad clinical diagnostic applications it has historically been used for.

Note

Do Not Confuse: Reliability and Validity in Personality Assessment A personality test can give consistent scores (good reliability) and still fail to measure what it claims (poor validity). Online personality quizzes often fail both: they classify people into types with low reliability (your "type" might change if you take it again next week) and low validity (the types don't predict real-world outcomes). Serious measures — MMPI, NEO-PI-R — demonstrate both through extensive research programs, though even established measures have applications where the evidence is stronger or weaker.

Chapter Summary

Personality psychology has been shaped by three theoretical traditions and a central empirical challenge.

The psychoanalytic tradition, originating with Freud, proposed that behavior is driven by unconscious conflicts among the id (pleasure principle), ego (reality principle), and superego (internalized moral standards), and that defense mechanisms protect the ego from anxiety by distorting threatening material. Specific claims — especially about psychosexual stages — have weak empirical support; the broader insight that motivated, self-protective cognition operates outside awareness is more durable.

The humanistic tradition, centered on Rogers, argued that personality is organized around the self-concept and that psychological distress arises when conditions of worth distort authentic self-experience. The research-supported finding from this tradition is that relationship variables in therapy — alliance, positive regard, empathy — reliably predict outcomes, though they are not the only active ingredients.

Trait approaches use factor analysis to identify the basic dimensions of personality. The Big Five — Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism — are the most empirically supported framework: reliable, moderately heritable, replicating across many cultures, and predicting real-world outcomes. The Big Five describes dimensions, not types — a distinction that matters for how you think about your own and others' personality.

The person-situation debate, launched by Mischel (1968), established that traits predict single behavioral observations with limited accuracy (~r = .30), and that this figure is not obviously smaller than classic situationist effects. The modern interactionist resolution holds that traits and situations interact: traits predict behavior better when aggregated over many situations, in weak situations, and when framed as if-then behavioral signatures.

Personality assessment divides into objective instruments (MMPI-3, NEO-PI-R) with well-established psychometric properties, and projective instruments (Rorschach, TAT) with more variable reliability and validity that depends heavily on the specific variable, scoring system, and application.

Key Terms

Review Questions

1. Freud's id operates on the pleasure principle, while the ego operates on the reality principle. What does this distinction mean in practice?

Show answer & rationale

The id seeks immediate gratification of drives with no consideration for consequences — it cannot delay satisfaction or tolerate frustration. The ego manages the id's demands by finding realistic, socially acceptable pathways to satisfaction, delaying gratification when necessary. In practice: the id produces the impulse (want food now), the ego implements it through realistic means (wait until lunch, pay for the meal).

2. A student insists they are not angry at a professor who failed them and then becomes unusually warm and complimentary toward that professor. What defense mechanism might this illustrate? Name a different mechanism that could produce different behavior in the same situation.

Show answer & rationale

This most likely illustrates reaction formation — expressing the opposite of an unacceptable feeling (hostility → exaggerated warmth). Displacement could produce different behavior: the student remains neutral toward the professor but becomes irritable with roommates or friends, redirecting hostility toward a safer target. Rationalization might produce yet another pattern: the student convinces themselves the failing grade was deserved and educational, constructing a plausible justification rather than acknowledging the threat to self-esteem.

3. Rogers argued that conditional positive regard in childhood can distort self-concept development. Explain the mechanism he proposed.

Show answer & rationale

When caregivers communicate that love and approval depend on specific behaviors, children learn to monitor and suppress aspects of their experience that threaten that approval. Over time, the self-concept becomes organized around conditions of worth rather than authentic experience. The result is incongruence: a gap between actual experience and the self-concept, which Rogers saw as a source of ongoing psychological distress — not because the world is objectively dangerous, but because every incoming experience must pass through a filter built to protect relational security.

4. The Big Five is described as having good "predictive validity." What does this mean, and give one specific example?

Show answer & rationale

Predictive validity means that scores on a measure correlate with real-world outcomes beyond the test itself. For the Big Five: Conscientiousness is among the most consistent predictors of academic and occupational performance. People who score high on conscientiousness earn higher grades and receive better supervisor performance ratings — even controlling for intelligence. This is predictive validity: the trait score predicts something meaningfully different about how people live, not just how they describe themselves.

5. A classmate hears that personality traits predict behavior with a correlation of only about .30 and concludes: "So traits basically don't matter." Give two specific reasons why this conclusion is too strong.

Show answer & rationale

First, Funder and Ozer (1983) showed that the situationist experiments Mischel was contrasting with trait research — Milgram obedience, Asch conformity, Darley and Latané bystander effect — when converted to correlation coefficients, yield effect sizes in roughly the same range. A correlation of .30 for traits is not obviously smaller than "classic" situationist effects. Second, and more fundamentally, .30 predicts single behavioral acts. For predicting patterns over time and across situations — semester GPAs, job tenure, relationship stability — traits accumulate into practically meaningful predictions (Funder & Ozer, 2019). The right conclusion is not that traits don't matter, but that they predict patterns better than individual acts.

6. What did Darley and Batson's Good Samaritan study find, and what does it suggest about how we should think about "good people"?

Show answer & rationale

Seminary students were more likely to stop and help a person in distress when they were not in a hurry (~63% of low-hurry participants) than when they were late (~10% of high-hurry participants). The content of what they were preparing to speak about — including whether they were thinking about the Good Samaritan parable — made essentially no difference. This does not mean character is irrelevant, but that a strong situational press (being late) can override expected dispositional tendencies in any given instance. Designing situations to make helping easy may be more effective than selecting "good people" and expecting character to override situational obstacles.

7. A clinician uses the Rorschach to conclude that a patient "shows disordered thinking." A colleague questions whether this conclusion is valid. What two concepts would the colleague invoke, and what would a well-grounded response look like?

Show answer & rationale

The colleague would invoke reliability (does the Rorschach produce consistent scores for this patient if administered again or by a different scorer?) and validity (does this particular Rorschach variable actually predict disordered thinking as assessed by independent external criteria?). A well-grounded response would note that Rorschach validity varies sharply by variable, scoring system, and application. Mihura et al.'s (2013) meta-analysis found moderate validity for thought-disorder-related variables assessed against external criteria (mean r = .27); this is meaningfully different from broader personality or clinical claims that lack comparable support. The clinician's conclusion is best supported if they are using a validated scoring system (R-PAS) for a specific thought-disorder variable, not making a broad personality inference.
