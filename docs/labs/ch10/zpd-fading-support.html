Learning Lab - Chapter 10

Scaffold or Substitute? Testing What Remains After Help Fades

A polished answer can hide an important difference. Did the help change only the answer in front of you, or did it leave you more capable when the help disappeared? This lab makes that distinction visible across four short reasoning problems.

Allow about 10-15 minutes. This is a conceptual demonstration and learning activity, not an assessment of your intelligence, learning capacity, or preferred way of learning.

Accessibility and Privacy

This lab runs entirely in your browser. It stores only your prediction, phase progress, responses, hint use, and completion state in sessionStorage for this browser session. It stores no names, emails, IDs, analytics, or tracking data, and nothing is sent to a server.

Phase 2B - Complete-answer support

Try, Then Receive the Completed Answer

Engineers are tracking whether fictional nori machines emit a blue signal.

CaseCeramic gearPulse switchBlue signal
1YesNoYes
2NoYesYes

A new machine has both a ceramic gear and a pulse switch. Will it emit a blue signal?

Complete rule and worked answer

The machine emits a blue signal when exactly one of the two features is present—not when both are present and not when neither is present.

The new machine has both features. Both is not exactly one, so the worked classification is No.

      Phase 3 of 6

      What the Sequence Demonstrates

      A completed product is not the same as a changed learner.

      The zone of proximal development is the range in which a task is beyond what a learner can currently do alone but achievable with effective guidance. Scaffolding provides temporary, calibrated support within that range. Good scaffolding fades. The strongest evidence of learning is not the quality of the assisted answer but what the learner can reconstruct and apply after the support is removed.

      Evidence boundary: this is not a psychological assessment

      This brief activity does not measure your intelligence or prove that one form of help always works better. You completed different tasks in a fixed sequence, not a controlled experiment. The activity demonstrates the logic of scaffolding and transfer.

      Transfer the Idea to AI

      Scenario

      A student asks an AI, “Write my comparison of Piaget and Vygotsky.” The AI produces an excellent paragraph.

      Redesign the interaction so the AI functions as a scaffold rather than a substitute. Complete every part.

      Feedback criteria

      A strong design includes student effort before assistance, calibrated hints, withholding of the finished product, fading support, and a new unsupported transfer task. These criteria evaluate the design of the interaction; they do not provide a paragraph for the student to copy.

        Instructor Note

        Use the lab individually, then have pairs compare what the completed answer did with what the graduated hints required them to do. The key discussion moment is the unsupported museum task: transfer is evidence relevant to learning, not infallible proof that learning occurred. Ask students to redesign one real AI interaction by naming what the system should withhold and how its support should fade.
