Classical Conditioning Simulator

This simulator uses a simplified teaching model to show one possible pattern of acquisition, extinction, and spontaneous recovery. The curve is generated by fixed rules in the code. It is not experimental data, a biological model, or evidence that one specific storage mechanism must be correct.

Accessibility and Privacy

This lab runs entirely in your browser. It stores your prediction, trial progress, explanation, and transfer response in sessionStorage for this browser session only. It does not collect names, emails, IDs, analytics, or personally identifying information.

Conditioning Terms

UCS

Unconditioned stimulus: the puff of air that naturally produces a blink.

UCR

Unconditioned response: the blink elicited by the air puff.

CS

Conditioned stimulus: the tone that starts out neutral and becomes predictive.

CR

Conditioned response: the learned blink to the tone alone.

Text Event Log

    Explain

    What the teaching model just illustrated

    During acquisition, the tone became a predictor of the air puff and conditioned responding increased. During extinction, the tone occurred without the air puff and responding declined. The simulator then inserted a partial rebound after a simulated delay to illustrate spontaneous recovery. That rebound is built into the model; it is not evidence generated by the activity. In real experiments, the return of responding after time or a context change supports the conclusion that extinction adds new, context-sensitive learning rather than reliably erasing the earlier CS–UCS learning. The exact biological and memory mechanisms remain more complex than this curve can show.

    Retrieve and Transfer

    Instructor Note

    Use this lab as a prediction-and-debrief activity. Have students commit predictions, run acquisition and extinction in pairs, then pause before the modeled recovery step. The key discussion is epistemic: the curve can illustrate a theory-consistent pattern, but students should not mistake a programmed outcome for experimental evidence.
