Classical Conditioning Simulator

This simulator uses a simplified instructional model to show how a conditioned response can grow during acquisition, weaken during extinction, and partially return after a delay. It is not a full biological model or a real experiment.

Accessibility and Privacy

This lab runs entirely in your browser. It stores your prediction, trial progress, explanation, and transfer response in sessionStorage for this browser session only. It does not collect names, emails, IDs, analytics, or personally identifying information.

Conditioning Terms

US

Unconditioned stimulus: the puff of air that naturally produces a blink.

UR

Unconditioned response: the natural blink after the air puff.

CS

Conditioned stimulus: the tone that starts out neutral.

CR

Conditioned response: the learned blink to the tone alone.

    Explain

    What the simulator just showed you

    Extinction did not erase the original CS–CR association. It created a new, competing inhibitory connection — after repeated CS-alone trials, the tone now predicts "nothing coming." After a simulated delay, that inhibitory learning weakened faster than the original excitatory association, so the original CS–CR link partially resurfaced. This is the new-learning account of extinction: the original association is suppressed, not deleted, which is why spontaneous recovery is possible and why context matters — extinction learned in one environment often does not fully transfer to a new one.

    Retrieve and Transfer

    Instructor Note

    Use this lab as a short prediction-and-debrief activity. Have students commit predictions, run acquisition and extinction in pairs, then pause on spontaneous recovery to discuss why extinction reduces responding without erasing the original association.
