Learning Lab - Chapter 11

The Feed Is Not the World

A social media feed feels like a window onto what people think. It isn't — it's a sample, and the sample can change based on what you click. In this lab, you will rate your own view on a low-stakes campus issue, scroll a short simulated feed, and see how the feed responds to your own engagement before rating your view again.

Accessibility and Privacy

This lab runs entirely in your browser. It stores your prediction, ratings, feed progress, explanation, and transfer response in sessionStorage for this browser session only. It does not collect names, emails, IDs, analytics, or personally identifying information, and nothing is sent to a server.

Core Idea

Perceived consensus

How common you believe an opinion is — a judgment your brain forms from what it has been shown, not from an actual poll of everyone.

Informational vs. normative influence

Informational influence updates what you believe is true. Normative influence changes what you say or do without changing what you privately believe.

Sample, not population

A feed selects and repeats certain views. Repetition and visibility are not the same thing as evidence quality or majority opinion.

    Instructor Note

    Use this after covering conformity (Asch) and before or alongside stereotypes and social identity. The key discussion moment is the reveal panel: most students expect the feed to have been assigned to them; instead it adapted to their own clicks after a neutral first round. Debrief around "the feed is a sample, not the population," and be explicit that this simulation exaggerates, for teaching clarity, an effect that real platform research finds is genuine but usually much smaller — the 2020 Facebook/Instagram Election Study found algorithm changes shifted what people saw without producing a clear shift in measured political attitudes over three months, while a 2024 preregistered X/Twitter field experiment found a real but modest (about 2 points on a 100-point scale) causal shift in affective polarization from reranking exposure. The mechanism is real; the size of the effect in this lab is not a claim about how much any real platform moves anyone's beliefs.
