Learning Lab - Chapter 11

Change the Situation, Change the Probability

Situations are not fog surrounding a person. They contain identifiable variables. In this lab, you will predict published group results, change one structural feature at a time, and watch the probability of behavior move.

Allow about 15 minutes. You are interpreting past research—not reenacting it and not being assessed.

Accessibility and Privacy

This lab runs entirely in your browser. It stores only your predictions, progress, explanation, classifications, and transfer response in sessionStorage for this browser session. It stores no names, emails, IDs, analytics, or tracking data, and nothing is sent to a server.

Phase 1 of 6

Predict and Commit

Predict the percentage of participant responses or participants meeting the stated outcome in each condition. These are predictions about groups—not questions about what you would do.

Asch: percentage of critical-trial judgments matching the incorrect majority
Milgram: percentage of participants reaching the 450-volt endpoint
Darley–Latané: percentage reporting the emergency before the staged fit ended

Complete every percentage and write a brief explanation. Your predictions will freeze after commitment.

Phase 2A - Asch

Change Unanimity

In the baseline condition, a participant publicly judged line lengths after a unanimous majority gave the same plainly incorrect answer. In the comparison condition, one person answered correctly before the participant, breaking unanimity.

Incorrect majority matches across critical trials

The unanimous-majority condition produced 32 percent incorrect-majority matches. With one correct ally, the rate was 5.5 percent.

Unanimous majority32%
One correct ally5.5%

Text result: Responses matched the incorrect majority on 32% of critical trials under unanimous pressure and 5.5% with one correct ally.

What changed

Unanimity was the manipulated situational variable. One ally changed the social structure of dissent: the participant no longer stood alone. The study does not show that everyone conforms or that participants could not see the correct line—68% of critical judgments remained correct even under unanimous pressure.

Phase 2B - Milgram

Change Relationships and Proximity

Participants believed an experiment required increasingly severe shocks after a learner's errors. Compare the standard condition with two structural changes: the participant had to press the learner's hand onto a shock plate, or the experimenter left and communicated remotely.

Participants reaching the 450-volt endpoint

The standard condition produced 65 percent continuation to 450 volts. Touch proximity produced 30 percent, and remote experimenter instructions produced 20.5 percent.

Standard condition65%
Touch proximity30%
Experimenter remote20.5%

Text result: 65% reached 450 V in the published baseline, compared with 30% in touch proximity and 20.5% when the experimenter gave instructions remotely.

What changed

Continuation changed when the authority relationship and physical proximity changed. The results do not show that participants were evil or lacked moral conflict, and they should not be reduced to compliance with one explicit order. The precise mechanism remains contested; identification with the scientific project and appeals to its importance may have mattered.

Identify the study's structure

Phase 2C - Darley and Latané

Change Apparent Responsibility

During an intercom discussion, a participant heard another supposed participant experience a seizure. Some believed they alone heard it. Others believed four additional listeners were also present, while nobody could tell what the others were doing.

Participants reporting before the staged fit ended

When participants believed they alone could hear the emergency, 85 percent reported it before the fit ended. When they believed four other listeners were present, 31 percent did.

Only available helper85%
Four others present31%

Text result: 85% reported the emergency before the staged fit ended when they believed they alone could help, compared with 31% when they believed four other listeners were present.

What changed

The number of apparent bystanders changed felt personal responsibility. Diffusion of responsibility is not evidence that people simply do not care: nonresponders often showed distress. It is one possible interruption in a larger chain that also includes noticing, interpreting, knowing what to do, and acting.

Identify the study's structure

Phase 3 of 6

What the Comparisons Demonstrate

The situation has parts

Across the three cases, researchers changed unanimity, proximity within an authority relationship, or the number of apparent bystanders. Changing one structural feature changed the probability of behavior without changing the kinds of people in the study. A probability shift is not a behavioral guarantee.

Transfer the Idea

Scenario

Several students notice a possible safety problem in a laboratory. Everyone else appears calm, no individual has been assigned responsibility, and a respected supervisor says the issue can wait. Change one structural feature of this situation to make speaking up or acting more likely.

Phase 6 of 6

Completion Summary

What to carry forward

Situational variables alter constraints, relationships, information, and responsibility. Changing them can shift the probability of behavior without making any outcome inevitable.

This activity compares your predictions with published group results. It does not diagnose how you personally would behave.

Instructor Note

Have students predict individually, then compare errors in pairs before discussing mechanisms. The key moment is not which famous percentage they remembered. It is whether they can name the structural variable, the measured behavior, and a defensible boundary on the inference. For transfer, compare interventions that break unanimity, assign one named person responsibility, or change the authority relationship.

Sources and Measure Notes

Asch values are the percentage of critical-trial judgments that followed the incorrect majority, not the percentage of people who ever conformed. Milgram values are the percentage who reached the 450-volt endpoint in each condition. Darley–Latané values are the percentage who reported the emergency before the staged fit ended.
