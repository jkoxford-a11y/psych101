Learning Lab - Chapter 4

Weber's Law: Difference Depends on Proportion

Question: Does an identical amount of change remain equally noticeable when the starting stimulus grows? This browser activity uses line length—not fake lifted weights. Plan on 8–12 minutes.

Accessibility and privacy

Responses are stored only in sessionStorage for this browser tab; no identifying data, analytics, or network transmission is used. The exact line lengths and percentages are provided as text after each response, so the logic remains available without relying on vision alone.

1. Orient and predict

Set A adds the same 12 pixels to a short and a long line. Which difference should be easier to notice?
Set B changes short and long lines by the same 20%. What should happen?

2. Compare the lines

For each pair, decide whether the second line is longer. The exact values stay hidden until you answer.

    3. Observe and explain

    What the comparisons reveal

    With the absolute change fixed at 12 pixels, it was 20% of the 60-pixel baseline but only 6.7% of the 180-pixel baseline. With proportion fixed near 20%, the long line needed a much larger absolute change. Weber's Law describes this proportional pattern.

    A handful of screen comparisons cannot estimate your Weber fraction. Display scaling, viewing distance, attention, and the tiny sample all matter.

    4. Retrieve

    5. Summary and transfer

    Worked distinction

    Absolute threshold: the faintest light detected 50% of the time. Difference threshold: the smallest change between two lights detected 50% of the time. Weber's Law: that difference threshold is often a roughly constant proportion of the starting intensity, not a fixed number of units.

    Instructor Note

    Ask students why “same number of pixels” initially feels like the fair comparison. Debrief when they see that holding proportion constant requires different absolute changes.
