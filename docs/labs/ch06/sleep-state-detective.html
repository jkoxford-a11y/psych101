Learning Lab - Chapter 6

Sleep-State Detective

A sleeper moves, shouts, or reports a vivid dream. Those observations are not enough by themselves to identify the sleep state. In this 10–20 minute lab, you will combine EEG, eye movement, muscle activity, time of night, and behavior.

Accessibility, Privacy, and Scope

This lab runs entirely in your browser. It stores your prediction, classifications, explanation, and transfer response in sessionStorage for this browser session only. It collects no names, email addresses, student IDs, analytics, or other identifiers, and it sends nothing to a server. All cases and recordings are simplified fictional teaching examples; they cannot diagnose a sleep disorder. Every schematic trace is described in text.

    3. What the Pattern Means

    Movement Is Not a Sleep Stage

    Polysomnography combines EEG, eye movement, and muscle activity. No single outward behavior is sufficient. Ordinary sleepwalking and night terrors emerge from deep NREM sleep, usually early in the night. Ordinary REM combines wake-like EEG activity and rapid eye movements with REM atonia.

    Actual dream enactment requires a different pattern: REM physiology plus a failure of normal atonia. Cataplexy reverses the boundary in another direction—REM-like muscle atonia intrudes while the person is awake and conscious.

    6. Instructor Note

    The key comparison is the sleepwalking case versus the REM sleep behavior disorder case. Ask students to name the outward similarity first, then identify the physiological evidence that reverses the classification. This makes the difference between describing behavior and identifying the underlying state visible.
