Learning Lab - Chapter 6

When Will You Feel Sleepy?

Sleepiness is not one signal. In this 10–20 minute lab, you will separate homeostatic sleep pressure from circadian timing, then test what caffeine, a nap, travel, and light actually change.

Accessibility, Privacy, and Scope

This lab runs entirely in your browser. It stores your prediction, scenario choices, explanation, and transfer response in sessionStorage for this browser session only. It collects no names, email addresses, student IDs, analytics, or other identifiers, and it sends nothing to a server. The curves are simplified teaching models, not individualized sleep forecasts or medical advice; every visual result is also stated in text.

    3. What the Pattern Means

    Two Signals, Different Levers

    Process S is homeostatic sleep pressure: it rises with time awake and falls during sleep. Process C is circadian timing: it changes the biological tendency toward alertness or sleep across the day. The two usually cooperate, but jet lag, shift work, naps, and caffeine can pull them apart.

    Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors and therefore masks part of the experienced pressure without clearing it. A nap discharges some Process S. Light shifts circadian timing rather than replacing sleep. The curves in this lab show conceptual relationships, not exact values for a particular person.

    6. Instructor Note

    Have pairs commit each prediction before revealing the model. The key debrief is the caffeine scenario: feeling more alert is not evidence that the accumulated need for sleep has disappeared. A second useful comparison is late caffeine versus a late nap—one masks pressure, while the other actually reduces some Process S.
