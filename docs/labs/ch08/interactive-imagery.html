Learning Lab - Chapter 8

Can You Make Two Words Stick Together?

In three rounds, you will study unrelated word pairs and then use the first word to recall its partner. The strategy changes one step at a time: first your usual approach, then an interaction, then a bizarre interaction.

This is a progressive teaching demonstration, not a controlled experiment or diagnostic assessment. The rounds occur in a fixed order and use different word lists, so your scores show what happened during this activity—not proof that one instruction caused the difference.

Accessibility and Privacy

This lab runs entirely in your browser. It stores your prediction, brief scene descriptions, task progress, explanation, and transfer response in sessionStorage for this browser session only. It collects no names, identifiers, or analytics and sends no data to a server. You do not need vivid mental pictures: a verbal or spatial description of one integrated event also works.

What the activity was designed to show

Interactive imagery places both items into one event. That relational binding stores more than two isolated words: it stores who did what to what. Later, the cue can reactivate the event and provide a route to its partner. Constructing the event also adds elaboration and retrieval cues.

A bizarre interaction can add distinctiveness because it violates ordinary knowledge. But weirdness is not the essential step. An ordinary interaction may already bind the pair effectively, and bizarre imagery does not reliably improve every list or every person.

Do Not Confuse: Bizarreness with Measured Prediction Error

A bizarre scene violates expectations, so it may recruit some of the same attention-to-mismatch processes discussed under prediction error. This lab did not present an unexpected outcome or measure prediction-error signals, however. Its most direct explanations are relational binding, elaboration, imagery, and distinctiveness.

What your scores cannot establish

The rounds occurred in a fixed instructional sequence and used different pairs. Practice, attention, pair difficulty, and spontaneous strategy use can all affect the pattern. Treat your scores as evidence to reason about, not as a controlled causal test.

Retrieve and Transfer

Instructor Note

Keep the fixed progression. It is the teaching mechanism: students first expose their default strategy, then add interaction, then add bizarreness. Debrief the class pattern without aggregating it as research data. Ask whether the largest change came from binding the pair or from making the already-bound scene strange.

Evidence Base

The design draws on paired-associate learning and interactive-imagery research. Its interpretation separates the robust benefit of constructing a relation between two items from the more conditional bizarreness effect.
