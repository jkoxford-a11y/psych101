Learning Lab - Chapter 8

What Makes a Word Stick?

You are about to make three kinds of judgments about words: what they look like, what they sound like, and what they mean. After a brief interruption, you will test which kind of processing produced the strongest memory.

This is a short instructional demonstration, not a diagnostic test of your memory and not a controlled experiment on you. With only a few words per condition, an individual result can differ from the usual group-level pattern by chance.

Accessibility and Privacy

The Question

Appearance

Attend to a surface feature, such as whether the target word appears in uppercase letters.

Sound

Attend to the word's phonological form by deciding whether it rhymes with another word.

Meaning

Attend to what the word means by deciding whether it fits a sentence.

      What the activity was designed to show

      All 30 targets appeared for one judgment. What changed was the mental operation you performed on each word. Appearance questions directed attention to a visual feature. Sound questions required phonological processing. Meaning questions connected the target to a semantic context, usually creating a richer and more broadly retrievable memory trace. Across groups, meaning-based judgments typically produce the strongest later memory.

      Do Not Confuse: Deep Processing with a Particular Study Activity

      Rereading is not automatically shallow, and semantic processing is not guaranteed to win on every possible test. A student can reread deeply by explaining, comparing, and generating examples. Memory also benefits when the operations used during encoding match the later retrieval task. The broader claim is that meaningful, elaborative processing usually produces memories that remain useful across more situations.

      Retrieve and Transfer

      Instructor Note

      Use the prediction before students read the levels-of-processing section, or use the lab afterward as a study-strategy audit. Have students compare the class pattern rather than treating any one student's score as decisive. The key debrief is not merely that “meaning won,” but that exposure time was held roughly constant while the cognitive operation changed.

      Evidence Base

      The design adapts the orienting-task logic of Craik and Tulving's classic depth-of-processing studies. The interpretation is deliberately qualified by later work on transfer-appropriate processing: durable memory depends on what the learner does with information and on how that information will later be retrieved.
