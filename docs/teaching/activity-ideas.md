## Prologue — How to Actually Learn This Stuff

### First-Week Retrieval Audit
Status: Draftable
Type: in-class demo / opener
Estimated time: 10 minutes
Core concepts: testing effect, metacognition, fluency illusion, familiarity vs. recall
Learning goal: Students experience the gap between feeling like they know something and being able to retrieve it — the core problem the prologue is designed to fix.
Activity sketch:
1. Display a list of 12 common psychology terms (no definitions — terms only): e.g., cognitive dissonance, classical conditioning, confirmation bias, working memory, etc.
2. Students rate confidence: "How well do you know this?" (1 = never heard of it, 5 = could explain it right now).
3. Students close their notes/devices and write a one-sentence definition for each term they rated 4 or 5.
4. Compare predicted confidence vs. actual recall accuracy.
5. Debrief: the gap IS the fluency illusion. Recognition feels like knowing; recall reveals what you actually have.
Student deliverable: confidence rating sheet + attempted definitions (self-scored).
Materials needed: term list (slide or handout); no device needed after step 1.
Possible output: handout in docs/assignments/ or a slide routine; also works as a verbal opener with a poll.
Notes:
- Best run the very first day or first week before any content is introduced.
- Works as a concrete setup for the prologue message: "Familiarity is not the same as learning."
- Keep the tone non-threatening — the point is that everyone overestimates, not that students are bad.

### AI Fluency Demonstration
Status: Draftable
Type: in-class demo / AI literacy exercise
Estimated time: 15 minutes
Core concepts: fluency illusion, desirable difficulty, retrieval practice, metacognition
Learning goal: Students observe that AI explanations feel clear and accessible — and that this ease is precisely what makes them weak as learning tools when not paired with retrieval effort.
Activity sketch:
1. Instructor displays an AI-generated explanation of a concept (e.g., confirmation bias) — well-written, clear, confident prose.
2. Students read it and rate: "How well do you understand this now?" (1–5).
3. Students close the text and immediately write down what they remember and can explain.
4. Students compare their rated confidence to what they actually recalled.
5. Debrief: fluent input ≠ durable encoding. AI prose is highly fluent. That's a risk, not a feature.
Student deliverable: confidence rating + recall attempt.
Materials needed: pre-written AI explanation (displayed on screen); no AI access required for students.
Possible output: slide routine; could become a standalone handout.
Notes:
- Instructor should prepare the AI text in advance — do NOT use live generation, as latency disrupts pacing.
- The concept used as the example doesn't matter much; what matters is that students feel the confidence-recall gap.
- Connects directly to prologue Section 8 (Working With AI) and the AI scaffolding vs. replacement framework.

### Study Habit Behavior Chain
Status: Idea
Type: short individual reflection / take-home
Estimated time: 5–10 minutes (in class or homework)
Core concepts: metacognition, self-regulated learning, avoidance behavior
Learning goal: Students map one real procrastination or avoidance pattern as a behavior chain to identify where the chain breaks and what an effective interruption point would be.
Activity sketch:
1. Students identify one recurring study avoidance behavior (e.g., "I open my notes, feel overwhelmed, open my phone").
2. Students map the sequence: trigger → thought → feeling → behavior → short-term outcome → long-term cost.
3. Students identify one specific intervention point: where in the chain could a different behavior fit?
Student deliverable: one completed behavior chain diagram + one sentence naming the intervention point.
Materials needed: simple worksheet template.
Possible output: PDF handout in docs/assignments/ or a journal prompt.
Notes:
- Frame as observation, not self-criticism. Students do not need to share personal content.
- Pairs with the Study Contract (docs/assignments/prologue-study-contract.docx — to be built during final activities pass).

### Exam Wrapper — First Quiz Version
Status: Draftable
Type: post-assessment reflection
Estimated time: 10 minutes (immediately after first quiz)
Core concepts: metacognition, testing effect, encoding failure vs. retrieval failure, spacing
Learning goal: Students distinguish among the three failure modes (didn't learn it, learned it but forgot it, knew it but couldn't retrieve under pressure) so they can choose corrective strategies rather than just "study more."
Activity sketch:
1. Students receive their first quiz/exam results.
2. For each missed item, students classify the failure: (a) I never encoded this; (b) I learned it but didn't space my review; (c) I knew it but couldn't retrieve under test conditions.
3. Students identify one concrete behavioral change per failure type.
Student deliverable: completed exam wrapper (3-column table: missed item / failure type / corrective strategy).
Materials needed: simple 3-column worksheet; quiz result.
Possible output: PDF handout in docs/assignments/.
Notes:
- Assign the same day as the first quiz so the metacognitive moment is fresh.
- Connects to prologue Section 4 (Retrieval) and Section 7 (Metacognition).
- Do not reveal answers before students complete the wrapper — the attempt at diagnosis is the point.

## Chapter 8 — Thinking, Language & Intelligence

### Using AI Without Outsourcing Your Thinking
Status: Built
Type: in-class activity / AI literacy exercise
Estimated time: 50 minutes
Core concepts: System 1, System 2, availability heuristic, representativeness heuristic, confirmation bias, anchoring, fluency heuristic, source checking, disconfirmation
Learning goal: Students practice evaluating AI output without letting fluent, confident prose substitute for their own reasoning.
Activity sketch:
1. Students use or inspect AI-generated text.
2. Students identify possible cognitive biases affecting their trust.
3. Students check specific factual claims instead of trusting general fluency.
4. Students ask for disconfirming evidence or alternative interpretations.
5. Students reflect on how their confidence changed after checking.
Student deliverable: One bias-audit revision, two checked factual claims, and one short reflection on trust/calibration.
Materials needed: AI access if available; no-device fallback text; chapter concepts; optional rubric.
Possible output: Already built as `assignments/ch08-ai-bias-activity.html`.
Notes:
- Strong activity because students perform the chapter's central skill rather than just memorizing bias names.
- Keep the no-device fallback.
- Add or preserve an instructor note that provided AI-style text should not be assumed accurate.
- Consider adding a simple credit/no-credit rubric.

### K-Word Availability Demo
Status: Draftable
Type: opener / poll / short in-class demo
Estimated time: 5–8 minutes
Core concepts: availability heuristic, ease of recall, metacognition
Learning goal: Students experience how ease of recall can be mistaken for actual frequency.
Activity sketch:
1. Students predict: are there more English words that begin with k, or more words with k as the third letter?
2. Students write down their answer and one sentence explaining their reasoning.
3. Students generate examples of each for 30 seconds.
4. Class compares which category felt easier.
5. Instructor reveals that ease of generation is not the same as actual frequency and introduces the availability heuristic.
Student deliverable: Written prediction plus one-sentence reasoning.
Materials needed: slide or poll; optional worksheet.
Possible output: slide opener, textbook Stop and Predict box, or embedded poll.
Notes:
- Works best if students commit to a prediction before the concept is explained.
- This should remain low-stakes; the point is noticing the reasoning process, not guessing correctly.

### Linda Problem Nested-Set Mini-Exercise
Status: Draftable
Type: in-class reasoning check / figure-linked activity
Estimated time: 8–10 minutes
Core concepts: representativeness heuristic, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, probability
Learning goal: Students distinguish "sounds representative" from "is more probable."
Activity sketch:
1. Students answer the Linda problem individually.
2. Students briefly justify their answer.
3. Instructor shows a nested-set diagram: bank tellers as the larger set, feminist bank tellers as a subset.
4. Students revise their answer using the diagram.
5. Instructor connects the error to representativeness and the conjunction fallacy.
Student deliverable: Initial answer, revised answer, one-sentence explanation.
Materials needed: Linda prompt; nested-set figure.
Possible output: figure, slide activity, textbook retrieval prompt.
Notes:
- Be careful not to overclaim about frequency framing. Frequency/nested-set formats often reduce the fallacy.

### Wason Selection Task Rule-Testing Demo
Status: Draftable
Type: poll / short interactive demo / slide routine
Estimated time: 10 minutes
Core concepts: confirmation bias, falsification, rule testing
Learning goal: Students learn that testing a rule requires looking for evidence that could prove it wrong.
Activity sketch:
1. Show four cards: E, K, 4, 7.
2. Rule: If a card has a vowel on one side, it must have an even number on the other side.
3. Students choose which cards must be flipped.
4. Instructor reveals that E and 7 are the diagnostic cards.
5. Students explain why 4 is tempting but not diagnostic.
Student deliverable: Card choice plus one-sentence explanation.
Materials needed: slide or interactive card demo.
Possible output: static figure first; interactive HTML demo later.
Notes:
- Connect explicitly to confirmation bias and to checking AI/source claims.

## Chapter 11 — Personality

### Situational Press on AI "Personality"
Status: Draftable
Type: in-class demo / AI literacy exercise
Estimated time: 20–30 minutes
Core concepts: person-situation debate, trait consistency, situational press, Big Five
Learning goal: Students observe that a chatbot's apparent "personality" is highly prompt-sensitive, paralleling Mischel's finding that situational context drives behavioral variance. This demonstrates both a limit of AI and a principle of personality psychology.
Activity sketch:
1. Instructor loads the same base chatbot model three times with three different system prompts — e.g., a formal corporate tone, an enthusiastic peer tutor, and a reserved cautious advisor.
2. Students ask each "version" the same question (e.g., "What do you think I should do to improve my study habits?").
3. Students record: How did the tone, warmth, and assertiveness differ across versions? Which Big Five traits seemed elevated or suppressed?
4. Class discussion: Is the chatbot high or low in Extraversion? The answer depends on which system prompt is active — trait-like qualities shift dramatically. Compare this to human trait consistency: a highly extraverted person maintains higher sociability across most situations, even if a strong situation modifies their behavior in the moment.
5. Instructor asks: What would have to be true for the chatbot to have a "real" personality? What is missing (self-concept, developmental history, integrated identity over time)?
Student deliverable: Written trait ratings (3 adjectives per version) plus one-sentence reflection on whether these constitute "personality."
Materials needed: chatbot access (Claude, GPT-4, or similar); three pre-written system prompts; optional rating worksheet.
Possible output: assignment page in docs/assignments/; could also be a slide routine without chatbot access using pre-recorded response examples.
Notes:
- Keep assignment in assignment layer (docs/assignments/), not inline in chapter body.
- Connects directly to the chapter's AI Connection callout on Jiang et al. (2024) and self-concept.
- Works as a parallel to the Darley & Batson "strong situation" effect: just as seminary students helping at ~10% under time pressure are unrecognizable from their trait profile, the chatbot under a formal system prompt is unrecognizable from its "enthusiastic" version.
- Emphasize the "what's missing" discussion — self-concept, stable identity over time, and cross-situational consistency without explicit instruction are the key contrasts.

## Chapter 12 — Emotion, Stress & Coping

### Body-Budget Audit
Status: Draftable
Type: take-home / week-long tracking exercise
Estimated time: ~5 min/day for one week; 20-min debrief session
Core concepts: allostasis, body budget, core affect, valence, arousal
Learning goal: Students apply the body-budget and core-affect framework to their own lives, observing how sleep, nutrition, movement, and social contact covary with daily mood ratings.
Activity sketch:
1. Students track daily: sleep (hours), meals (regular/skipped), movement (yes/no), significant social contact (yes/no), workload demands (low/medium/high).
2. Students also rate daily valence (pleasant/unpleasant, 1–7) and arousal (activated/deactivated, 1–7) at end of day.
3. At the end of the week, students look for patterns: do depleted-budget days (poor sleep + skipped meals) cluster with negative valence ratings?
4. Debrief as a class: discuss the body-budget framework as a predictive model.
Student deliverable: tracking log + one-paragraph reflection on observed patterns.
Materials needed: simple tracking worksheet (one page).
Possible output: PDF worksheet in docs/assignments/.
Notes:
- The learning target is applying the framework, not self-help or wellness coaching. Frame as empirical observation.
- Students should not be pressured to share personal data; reflection can be private.

### Emotional Granularity Exercise
Status: Draftable
Type: in-class activity / short writing exercise
Estimated time: 15–20 minutes
Core concepts: emotional granularity, affect labeling, constructed emotion
Learning goal: Students practice distinguishing among negative emotional states with precision, connecting granularity to the body-budget / constructionist account.
Activity sketch:
1. Present students with a list of ~20 negative emotion words (stressed, anxious, frustrated, overwhelmed, disappointed, resentful, discouraged, irritated, worried, apprehensive, dejected, ashamed, envious, lonely, bored, helpless, embarrassed, guilty, dread, regret).
2. Students identify which words they have used in the last week and briefly describe the situation attached to each.
3. For each labeled state, students identify: (a) what body signal comes with it; (b) what it was about (the situation); (c) what response it seemed to call for.
4. Discuss as a class: how many students collapsed multiple states into "stressed" or "bad"? What information is lost by doing that?
Student deliverable: completed emotion-mapping worksheet.
Materials needed: emotion word list handout.
Possible output: PDF handout in docs/assignments/.
Notes:
- Pairs well with the affect-labeling Classic Study Walkthrough in Section 2.
- Connect back to the granularity → coping research (Kashdan et al., 2015).

### Coping Strategy Matching
Status: Draftable
Type: in-class small-group activity
Estimated time: 20 minutes
Core concepts: problem-focused coping, emotion-focused coping, primary appraisal, secondary appraisal, controllability
Learning goal: Students apply Lazarus & Folkman's framework by categorizing stressors and selecting appropriate coping strategies.
Activity sketch:
1. Present 6 stress scenarios varying in controllability: (a) exam in 3 days; (b) family member with terminal illness; (c) conflict with a roommate; (d) financial aid error that will take weeks to resolve; (e) failed audition for a team or group; (f) overwhelming workload with competing deadlines.
2. Small groups classify each as primarily controllable, uncontrollable, or mixed.
3. Groups choose whether to lead with problem-focused or emotion-focused coping and justify their choice.
4. Class compares answers; instructor uses disagreements to show that controllability assessment is itself part of the coping process.
Student deliverable: scenario classification worksheet.
Materials needed: scenario handout.
Possible output: PDF worksheet in docs/assignments/.
Notes:
- Scenarios (c) and (f) are intentionally ambiguous — the point is that appraisal varies.
- Connect to the coping match table in the chapter.

### AI Affect-Labeling Check
Status: Draftable
Type: individual exercise / AI literacy + emotional granularity
Estimated time: 15–20 minutes
Core concepts: emotional granularity, affect labeling, AI and emotion
Learning goal: Students use AI to generate multiple possible emotion labels for a stressor, then evaluate which label best fits their body state, context, and implied coping response — practicing granularity without outsourcing self-reflection.
Activity sketch:
1. Students describe a recent stressor briefly (no identifying details required).
2. Students prompt an AI: "Give me three possible emotion labels for this situation. For each label, explain what coping response it would imply. Do not tell me which is correct."
3. Students evaluate each AI-generated label against: (a) what their body actually felt like; (b) what the situation was specifically about; (c) which implied coping response seems most relevant.
4. Students write one sentence: "The label that best fits is ___ because ___."
5. Brief reflection: did the AI miss anything that required inside knowledge (body state, history, stakes)?
Student deliverable: stressor description + AI output + label evaluation + one-sentence conclusion.
Materials needed: AI access (Claude, GPT-4, or similar); no-device fallback = instructor provides a pre-run example.
Possible output: assignment page in docs/assignments/.
Notes:
- Keep in assignment layer only — do NOT put chatbot prompt inline in the chapter body.
- The goal is not to let AI diagnose the emotion — it's to use AI-generated vocabulary as a starting point for student self-reflection.
- Discuss afterward: what would the AI need to do this well that it does not have? (Body state, interoception, allostatic context.)

### Diagnostic Criteria Case Vignettes
Status: Draftable
Type: in-class small-group activity
Estimated time: 20–30 minutes
Core concepts: 3D criteria (distress, dysfunction, deviance), DSM-5-TR, diathesis-stress model, biopsychosocial model
Learning goal: Students apply the 3D criteria to ambiguous vignettes and practice distinguishing disorder from normal-range distress.
Activity sketch:
- Present 4–5 brief case vignettes that vary in how clearly they meet the 3D criteria.
- Small groups classify each: meets criteria / borderline / does not meet criteria, with justification.
- Debrief: highlight cases where reasonable people disagree and why.
Student deliverable: classification worksheet with written justifications.
Materials needed: vignette handout.
Possible output: PDF worksheet in docs/assignments/.
Notes:
- Vignettes should be fictional and constructed to avoid reinforcing stigma.
- Connect to Rosenhan (1973) and the Temerlin (1968) labeling-effects discussion.

### Therapy Matching Exercise
Status: Draftable
Type: individual or small-group activity
Estimated time: 20 minutes
Core concepts: CBT, DBT, psychodynamic therapy, humanistic/client-centered therapy, pharmacotherapy, Dodo Bird Verdict, therapeutic alliance
Learning goal: Students match evidence-based treatment approaches to presenting problems, then evaluate why the match is imperfect.
Activity sketch:
- Present 5–6 presenting problems (e.g., panic disorder, MDD, BPD, schizophrenia, PTSD, grief).
- Students match each to a primary evidence-based treatment and briefly justify.
- Debrief: discuss the Dodo Bird Verdict (all bona fide therapies show similar outcomes overall) and what therapeutic alliance means across approaches.
Student deliverable: matching worksheet with justifications.
Materials needed: treatment-type reference card (could be the chapter's treatment comparison table).
Possible output: PDF worksheet in docs/assignments/.
Notes:
- Do not ask students to say which therapy is "best" — the Dodo Bird framing is the point.
- Keep pharmacotherapy options in the mix; students often default to "medication" without justification.
