## Figure 13.1 — Bounded maintenance lens

- **Filename:** `ch13_maintenance_lens_bounded.svg`
- **Caption:** A response can make sense in the short term yet help maintain a problem over time. This lens is especially useful for avoidance, compulsions, trauma-related threat learning, and behavioral withdrawal; it is not a universal architecture of psychological disorder.
- **Alt text:** A bounded maintenance lens showing a short-term protective response, immediate relief, and a feedback path that can preserve a problem over time, alongside a boundary note that the lens applies to some symptoms rather than all disorders.
- **Creation method:** Original programmatic SVG created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, directed and reviewed by Jon Oxford, 2026. No external copyrighted image assets used.
- **License / attribution:** CC BY 4.0. “Original figure created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, 2026.”
- **Public-sharing status:** Use now.
- **Caveat:** The loop is a teaching model for selected maintenance processes, not a general causal architecture of psychopathology.

## Figure 13.2 — Diagnostic evidence questions

- **Filename:** `ch13_diagnostic_evidence_questions.svg`
- **Caption:** The 3Ds are preliminary questions that can identify concern. A diagnosis requires substantially more: disorder-specific criteria, duration, context, exclusions, differential diagnosis, and trained clinical judgment.
- **Alt text:** Diagnostic concern begins with distress, impairment, or unusual experience, then proceeds through duration, development and culture, medical or substance exclusions, differential diagnosis, and clinical judgment; the 3Ds are labeled as preliminary questions rather than required intersecting criteria.
- **Creation method:** Original programmatic SVG created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, directed and reviewed by Jon Oxford, 2026. No external copyrighted image assets used.
- **License / attribution:** CC BY 4.0. “Original figure created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, 2026.”
- **Public-sharing status:** Use now.
- **Caveat:** The diagram does not reproduce a DSM decision algorithm. It shows the types of evidence omitted by a three-item introductory heuristic.

## Figure 13.3 — Diagnosis is not a cause

- **Filename:** `fig_diagnosis_is_not_a_cause_flowchart.png`
- **Caption:** A diagnosis organizes a clinically significant pattern. It does not, by itself, explain why that pattern occurred or determine which treatment will help. Those require further evidence.
- **Alt text:** A flowchart from symptoms through DSM diagnosis to further causal and treatment questions, with a red X blocking a mistaken arrow that loops directly from diagnosis back to “cause explained.”
- **Creation method:** Existing original explanatory figure retained from the Chapter 13 asset set. Repository provenance identifies it as an original figure; no external asset is apparent.
- **License / attribution:** Project original; attribution retained as “Original figure for Oxford Psychology 101.” Treat as CC BY 4.0 for project publication.
- **Public-sharing status:** Use now.
- **Caveat:** Diagnosis can inform prognosis and treatment evidence, but it does not logically entail one etiology or one intervention.

## Figure 13.4 — Vulnerability, context, and changing probability

- **Filename:** `ch13_vulnerability_context_probabilistic.svg`
- **Caption:** Diathesis-stress reasoning asks how vulnerabilities, stressors, and protective resources interact over time. It changes probabilities; it does not calculate a person’s fate from a fixed vulnerability-plus-stress score.
- **Alt text:** Two people face overlapping but not identical vulnerabilities, stressors, and supports; curved arrows show interaction over time, and outcomes are represented as changing probabilities rather than bars crossing a fixed threshold.
- **Creation method:** Original programmatic SVG created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, directed and reviewed by Jon Oxford, 2026. No external copyrighted image assets used.
- **License / attribution:** CC BY 4.0. “Original figure created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, 2026.”
- **Public-sharing status:** Use now.
- **Caveat:** The framework organizes probabilistic interaction. It does not specify a measurable common unit or literal threshold.

## Figure 13.5 — Avoidance and corrective learning

- **Filename:** `ch13_anxiety_avoidance_learning_loop.svg`
- **Caption:** Avoidance and compulsions can be maintained by immediate relief. Exposure-based treatment creates conditions for corrective or inhibitory learning; it does not promise that the original fear memory is erased.
- **Alt text:** A cycle from threat cue to anxiety to avoidance or compulsion to immediate relief and then reduced opportunity for corrective learning; an exposure branch stays with the cue long enough for new safety learning while noting that fear may not disappear completely.
- **Creation method:** Original programmatic SVG created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, directed and reviewed by Jon Oxford, 2026. No external copyrighted image assets used.
- **License / attribution:** CC BY 4.0. “Original figure created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, 2026.”
- **Public-sharing status:** Use now.
- **Caveat:** This is a common maintenance loop, not a complete account of the origin or treatment of every anxiety presentation.

## Figure 13.6 — Depression maintenance loop

- **Filename:** `fig_depression_maintenance_loop.png`
- **Caption:** Behavioral withdrawal is one possible maintenance pathway in depression. Behavioral activation interrupts the loop by scheduling useful or rewarding action before waiting for motivation to return.
- **Alt text:** A circular loop connecting low mood, withdrawal and inactivity, fewer rewarding experiences, and lower motivation, with an arrow exiting the loop from withdrawal toward behavioral activation.
- **Creation method:** Existing original explanatory figure retained from the Chapter 13 asset set. Repository provenance identifies it as an original figure; no external asset is apparent.
- **License / attribution:** Project original; attribution retained as “Original figure for Oxford Psychology 101.” Treat as CC BY 4.0 for project publication.
- **Public-sharing status:** Use now.
- **Caveat:** Behavioral withdrawal is one maintenance pathway, not a universal cause or complete model of depression.

## Figure 13.7 — Positive and negative symptoms

- **Filename:** `fig_schizophrenia_positive_negative_symptoms.png`
- **Caption:** Positive symptoms add experiences such as hallucinations or delusions; negative symptoms reduce capacities such as motivation or expression. Depression, medication effects, and other causes must be considered when evaluating apparent negative symptoms.
- **Alt text:** Two-column comparison of schizophrenia symptoms, with positive symptoms as experiences added and negative symptoms as capacities reduced.
- **Creation method:** Existing original explanatory figure retained from the Chapter 13 asset set. Repository provenance identifies it as an original figure; no external asset is apparent.
- **License / attribution:** Project original; attribution retained as “Original figure for Oxford Psychology 101.” Treat as CC BY 4.0 for project publication.
- **Public-sharing status:** Use now.
- **Caveat:** The positive/negative distinction is descriptive. Apparent negative symptoms require differential assessment.

## Figure 13.8 — Treatment selection

- **Filename:** `ch13_treatment_selection_evidence_context.svg`
- **Caption:** Treatment is selected by matching evidence and clinical context to the person and problem. Relationship processes matter within psychotherapy, but medication and ECT are not extensions of a psychotherapy common-factors hub.
- **Alt text:** Treatment selection begins with the specific problem and evidence, then considers urgency, risk, prior response, adverse effects, client preferences, access, and monitoring; psychotherapy, medication, combined care, and somatic treatment appear as options rather than spokes from one universal mechanism.
- **Creation method:** Original programmatic SVG created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, directed and reviewed by Jon Oxford, 2026. No external copyrighted image assets used.
- **License / attribution:** CC BY 4.0. “Original figure created for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT, 2026.”
- **Public-sharing status:** Use now.
- **Caveat:** The figure is a decision framework, not a treatment algorithm. Condition-specific guidance and qualified clinical judgment remain necessary.
