## Caption

Light detected by specialized retinal cells travels through the retinohypothalamic tract to the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), the brain’s master circadian clock. The SCN helps time pineal melatonin release, which rises in darkness and falls with light. The key point is that light entrains the clock; it does not create the rhythm from scratch, and melatonin is a timing signal rather than a simple sleep switch.

## Alt text

Biology infographic showing the circadian light pathway. Light enters the eye and reaches the retina, then signals travel through the retinohypothalamic tract to the SCN in the brain. The SCN sends timing signals toward the pineal gland, where melatonin is released into the bloodstream during darkness. A small graph shows melatonin levels rising at night and falling during the day.

## Accuracy and simplification notes

- This is a teaching diagram, not a full anatomical map.
- The retinohypothalamic tract to the SCN is simplified as a single direct pathway.
- The SCN-to-pineal route is simplified as a sympathetic pathway; intermediate relays are not shown.
- Melatonin is framed as a circadian timing signal, not as a simple on/off sleep switch.
- The melatonin curve is schematic rather than empirical data from a specific study.
