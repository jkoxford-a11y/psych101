# Psych 101 Figure Style Guide

**Purpose:** Use this guide when creating, selecting, revising, or approving figures for the Psych 101 textbook, slide decks, and public-facing course materials.

The goal is not to make figures decorative. The goal is to make figures students can reason from.

---

## 1. Core figure philosophy

A good Psych 101 figure should do one of four things:

1. **Explain a mechanism**  
   Example: how an action potential travels, how myelin changes conduction, how synaptic excitation and inhibition affect firing.

2. **Compare easily confused concepts**  
   Example: neurotransmitter vs. hormone, sympathetic vs. parasympathetic, sensation vs. perception, correlation vs. causation.

3. **Show a system-level relationship**  
   Example: dopamine and serotonin projection systems, autonomic balance, stress-response pathways.

4. **Correct a misconception**  
   Example: dopamine is not simply pleasure; inhibition is not bad; the brain is not a set of isolated Lego blocks.

Avoid figures that merely decorate a page.

---

## 2. Default visual style

Preferred style:

- Semi-realistic biology / medical-illustration look.
- Clean white or very light background.
- 16:9-friendly composition when possible.
- Large, readable labels.
- Few labels, not every possible anatomical detail.
- Smooth, polished, textbook-quality rendering.
- Clean arrows showing direction, sequence, or influence.
- Consistent use of color to represent systems or contrasts.
- No visible figure numbers embedded in the image.
- No watermarks, decorative logos, or source branding inside the image.
- No dark neon “YouTube science” style unless there is a specific reason.

The strongest recent Chapter 3 examples used this style:
- Nervous-system centralization across body plans.
- Neurotransmitter vs. hormone as wired/focal vs. bloodstream broadcast.
- Dopamine/serotonin as projection systems.
- Myelin and saltatory conduction.
- Excitation/inhibition and circuit selectivity.
- Autonomic balance as push-pull.
- Minimal-label lateral brain lobes.

---

## 3. One hard idea per figure

Each figure should have a single instructional job.

Good:
- “Myelin makes signaling faster because the action potential regenerates at nodes of Ranvier.”
- “Neurotransmitters and hormones differ by delivery route, not chemical identity.”
- “Dopamine effects depend on projection pathway and target circuit.”

Too much:
- A single figure showing every neurotransmitter, every receptor, every brain region, every behavioral effect, and every disorder.

If a figure needs more than three or four major labels, consider splitting it into two figures or moving detail into the caption.

---

## 4. Use the right layout for the concept

### Side-by-side comparison
Use when students need to distinguish two concepts.

Examples:
- Neurotransmitter vs. hormone.
- Excitation vs. inhibition.
- Sympathetic vs. parasympathetic.
- Random sampling vs. random assignment.

### Sequential panels
Use when the concept unfolds over time.

Examples:
- Action potential threshold → depolarization → repolarization.
- Dopamine prediction error: unexpected reward → cue learning → expected reward → omitted reward.
- Classical conditioning acquisition → extinction → spontaneous recovery.

### Pathway / projection diagram
Use when source and target matter.

Examples:
- Dopamine pathways from VTA/substantia nigra.
- Serotonin projections from raphe nuclei.
- Stress response: sympathetic route plus endocrine route.

### Push-pull / balance diagram
Use when two systems continuously regulate a state.

Examples:
- Sympathetic and parasympathetic autonomic balance.
- Excitatory and inhibitory input integration.
- Sleep pressure and circadian timing.

### Comparative body-plan diagram
Use when the point is organismal or evolutionary logic.

Examples:
- Diffuse nerve net → radial coordination → bilaterian centralization → vertebrate CNS.
- Sensory tuning across species.
- Developmental or life-history tradeoffs.

Do not make these diagrams look like a simple ladder unless the prose explicitly explains that the comparison is not a ladder of “more evolved” organisms.

---

## 5. Labeling rules

Use labels sparingly.

Preferred label style:
- Short noun labels.
- Short action labels.
- One-line function labels when needed.

Examples:
- “VTA”
- “Nucleus accumbens”
- “Faster conduction”
- “Closer to threshold”
- “Bloodstream broadcast”
- “Focal synapse”

Avoid:
- Paragraphs inside figures.
- Multiple full sentences in small text.
- Over-explaining in callout boxes.
- Labels that repeat the caption.
- Embedded figure numbers such as “Fig. 3.4.”

The figure should teach visually; the caption can carry nuance.

---

## 6. Captions

Every figure needs a caption that explains the conceptual point, not just what is pictured.

Weak caption:
> Diagram of the autonomic nervous system.

Better caption:
> The sympathetic and parasympathetic branches operate as a coordinated push-pull system, not an on/off switch. Sympathetic activity mobilizes the body for action; parasympathetic activity supports recovery and regulation.

Caption pattern:
1. State what the figure shows.
2. State the core concept.
3. Add one accuracy caveat if needed.

---

## 7. Alt text

Every figure needs accessibility-first alt text.

Alt text should:
- Identify the type of figure.
- Describe the important visual structure.
- Include the core relationship being shown.
- Avoid unnecessary artistic detail.

Example:
> Two-panel infographic comparing neurotransmitters and hormones. The neurotransmitter panel shows an electrical signal traveling down an axon, neurotransmitter release at an axon terminal, and receptors on a specific postsynaptic target cell. The hormone panel shows an endocrine gland releasing hormones into the bloodstream, where receptor-bearing cells respond and cells without receptors do not.

For complex figures, write 2–4 sentences rather than trying to compress everything into one sentence.

---

## 8. Attribution and public-sharing workflow

For each figure, record:

- Suggested filename.
- Caption.
- Alt text.
- Source or creation method.
- License.
- Attribution text.
- Public-sharing status.
- Whether the figure is:
  - **use now**
  - **slide only**
  - **candidate/reference**
  - **do not use**

Default attribution for generated originals:
> Original figure generated for Oxford Psychology 101 with ChatGPT image generation, 2026. No external copyrighted image assets intentionally used. Safe for public sharing pending final biological accuracy review.

For adapted open-license figures:
> Adapted from [Creator/Source], [Title], [License]. Changes: [cropped/recolored/relabelled/etc.].

For public-domain or CC0 figures:
> [Creator], [Title], Wikimedia Commons, public domain / CC0. Attribution not legally required but retained for source tracking.

Never use unknown-source screenshots as final public textbook figures.

---

## 9. Filename pattern

Use descriptive filenames, not figure numbers.

Preferred pattern:
`ch##_short_concept_slug.png`

Examples:
- `ch03_nervous_system_centralization_hybrid.png`
- `ch03_chemical_messengers_wired_focal_vs_broadcast.png`
- `ch03_neuromodulator_projection_systems_dopamine_serotonin.png`
- `ch03_myelin_saltatory_conduction_speed_efficiency.png`
- `ch03_excitation_inhibition_circuit_selectivity.png`
- `ch03_autonomic_balance_push_pull.png`
- `ch03_lateral_brain_lobes_minimal_labels.png`

Do not embed figure numbers in image filenames unless there is a specific build-system requirement. Chapter order may change.

## 10. Accuracy rules for Chapter 3 biology figures

### Nervous-system evolution / body plans
Do:
- Show increasing centralization as a body-plan and behavior problem.
- Use comparative examples carefully.

Do not:
- Imply sea stars are ancestors of planarians.
- Imply evolution is a ladder from “primitive” to “advanced.”
- Overclaim that brains evolved for one simple reason.

Preferred wording:
> The point is not a ladder of more evolved animals, but a comparison of how body plans create different information-processing problems.

### Neurotransmitter vs. hormone
Do:
- Emphasize delivery route, timescale, and target specificity.
- Say neurons use wired axons and focal synapses.
- Say hormones use bloodstream broadcast and act where receptors are present.

Do not:
- Say neurotransmitters are simply “local” without clarification.
- Ignore that axons can be long.
- Imply all body cells respond to a hormone.

Preferred wording:
> Neurotransmitters and hormones are both chemical messengers. The main difference is delivery route: neurons use wired axons and focal synapses; hormones use the bloodstream and act broadly where receptors are present.

### Dopamine and serotonin
Do:
- Treat them as neuromodulatory projection systems.
- Show source nuclei and target circuits.
- Emphasize pathway and receptor context.
- Present dopamine as prediction, motivation, and reward-prediction error.

Do not:
- Call dopamine the pleasure chemical.
- Call serotonin the happiness chemical.
- Make serotonin a mood-only system.
- Turn the section into a neurotransmitter catalog.

Preferred wording:
> The important principle is not “dopamine does X” and “serotonin does Y.” Pathway and receptor context determine psychological effect.

### Excitation and inhibition
Do:
- Show excitation as increased likelihood of firing.
- Show inhibition as decreased likelihood of firing or stabilization away from threshold.
- Show circuit-level inhibition as selectivity, timing, and noise reduction.

Do not:
- Treat excitation as good and inhibition as bad.
- Treat inhibition as simply stopping the brain.
- Overload students with ion-channel details unless the figure’s purpose requires it.

Preferred wording:
> Inhibition is not a failure of signaling. It sharpens timing, suppresses noise, and helps circuits select one response over competing alternatives.

### Myelin and saltatory conduction
Do:
- Show myelin segments and nodes of Ranvier.
- Show action-potential regeneration at nodes.
- Explain faster and more energy-efficient signaling.

Do not:
- Imply the signal literally jumps through empty space.
- Use absolute speed values without “approximate” language.
- Ignore that conduction velocity varies by axon diameter, myelination, and species.

Preferred wording:
> Current spreads under the myelin to the next node, where the action potential is regenerated.

### Autonomic nervous system
Do:
- Show sympathetic and parasympathetic as a coordinated push-pull balance.
- Emphasize both branches are continuously active.
- Include fight, flight, freeze, and tend-and-befriend only as behavioral strategies under stress.

Do not:
- Present sympathetic and parasympathetic as a simple on/off switch.
- Present tend-and-befriend as a third autonomic branch.
- Reduce parasympathetic activity to “calm” only.

Preferred wording:
> The autonomic nervous system prepares the body, but behavior depends on the problem: confront, escape, freeze, recover, or connect.

### Brain-region locator figures
Do:
- Use broad functional labels.
- Pair region labels with network caveats.

Do not:
- Suggest each lobe “contains” a behavior.
- Overuse “center for” language.
- Make generic brain art that does not support a chapter concept.

Preferred wording:
> Each lobe contributes to larger networks; behavior is not produced by one region acting alone.

## 11. Video guidance

Use short videos only when motion adds real understanding.

Best Chapter 3 video candidates:
- Action potential propagation.
- Myelin and saltatory conduction.
- Excitation/inhibition input integration.
- Dopamine reward-prediction error.

Default specs:
- 12–20 seconds.
- 720p if file size matters.
- 12–15 fps usually sufficient.
- MP4 or WebM.
- No voiceover unless necessary.
- Large labels.
- Include a poster PNG.
- Target file size under 5 MB; preferably 1–3 MB.

Avoid video for static locator diagrams unless animation clarifies the concept.

## 12. Image-generation prompt template

Use this structure for new generated figures:

```text
Create a [16:9 / square / wide] semi-realistic educational biology infographic for first-year General Psychology students.

Core teaching idea:
[One sentence explaining the concept.]

Style:
- Clean white background.
- Semi-realistic textbook medical illustration.
- Large readable labels.
- Minimal text.
- No embedded figure number.
- No watermark.
- Avoid dark neon or decorative stock-image style.

Layout:
[Describe two-panel, three-panel, pathway diagram, balance diagram, etc.]

Required labels:
[List exact labels.]

Accuracy constraints:
[List what the image must not imply.]

Final bottom key idea:
[Optional one-sentence key idea.]

Output:
PNG suitable for textbook and slides.
```

## 13. Figure review checklist

Before using a generated or external figure:

- Does it teach one clear concept?
- Is the figure visually consistent with the book?
- Are all labels spelled correctly?
- Are all anatomical labels approximately correct?
- Is any claim too absolute?
- Does it accidentally reinforce a misconception?
- Is text readable at slide distance?
- Does the image work without color alone?
- Is there alt text?
- Is attribution recorded?
- Is public-sharing status clear?
- Is the figure worth page space compared with prose, table, or lab activity?

If any answer is no, mark the figure as candidate/reference instead of use-now.

---

## 14. Use-now vs. candidate standards

### Use now
A figure can go into the chapter now if:
- It directly supports a core learning objective.
- It is biologically accurate enough for Psych 101.
- It is visually polished and readable.
- It has caption, alt text, attribution, and source status.
- It does not create unnecessary cognitive load.

### Slide only
Use for lecture if:
- It is visually useful but too dense for the book.
- It supports instructor explanation but would overload independent readers.
- It is a good enrichment image.

### Candidate/reference
Keep in a candidate folder if:
- It is legally usable but not stylistically consistent.
- It is useful as a source for a redraw.
- It is too technical for intro students.
- It requires more citation or accuracy review.

### Do not use
Do not use if:
- License/source is unclear.
- Labels are wrong or garbled.
- It implies a misconception.
- It is decorative rather than instructional.
- It cannot be made accessible.

---

## 15. Standing preference

When in doubt, prefer an original explanatory diagram over an external image. External images are valuable when they show real anatomy, historical cases, or primary research visuals that would be difficult or inappropriate to redraw. For most course concepts, original diagrams are cleaner, safer, and more teachable.
