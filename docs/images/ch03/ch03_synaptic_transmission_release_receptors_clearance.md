## Teaching purpose

Show the basic cycle of communication at a chemical synapse without requiring students to mentally animate the entire mechanism from prose:

1. An action potential reaches the presynaptic terminal and neurotransmitter is released.
2. Neurotransmitter crosses the synaptic cleft and binds to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron.
3. Receptor activation changes ion flow, making the postsynaptic neuron more or less likely to fire.
4. The signal is cleared either through reuptake into the presynaptic terminal or, when neurotransmitter remains in the cleft, through enzymatic degradation.

The figure intentionally omits voltage-gated calcium channels and detailed vesicle-fusion machinery. Those mechanisms are real, but they are not necessary for the instructional job of this Psych 101 figure.

## Caption

**Synaptic transmission is a short-lived chemical conversation.** An arriving action potential triggers neurotransmitter release from the presynaptic terminal. Neurotransmitter molecules cross the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors, changing ion flow and making the postsynaptic neuron more or less likely to fire. One way the signal ends is reuptake into the presynaptic terminal. Neurotransmitter that remains in the cleft may instead be broken down by enzymes.

## Alt text

Two-panel educational infographic showing how a chemical synaptic signal is sent and cleared. In the left panel, an action potential reaches a blue presynaptic terminal, neurotransmitter molecules are released into the synaptic cleft, and the molecules bind to receptors on a green postsynaptic neuron. Receptor opening changes sodium or potassium ion movement, altering the postsynaptic neuron’s likelihood of firing. In the right panel, one route of clearance is reuptake through transporter proteins in the presynaptic membrane. The other route shows neurotransmitter molecules that remain in the cleft being broken into inactive pieces by enzymes.

## Accuracy notes

- Reuptake transporters must appear in the **presynaptic membrane**, facing the synaptic cleft.
- Enzymatic degradation must occur **within the synaptic cleft**, acting on neurotransmitter that remains there.
- Receptor effects should be described as changing ion flow and the **likelihood** of firing, not as automatically causing an action potential.
- Sodium influx and potassium movement are simplified examples of ion-channel effects; receptor and ion effects vary across synapses.
- The figure should not imply that reuptake and enzymatic degradation are the only clearance mechanisms at every synapse.
- The figure should not include calcium-channel detail; that addition created unnecessary cognitive load during revision.
- The SSRI mechanism is intentionally omitted from the figure. It can remain in the prose or caption so the figure retains one primary instructional job.
