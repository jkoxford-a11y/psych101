## Figure 3.1 — Neuron anatomy and signal direction
**Caption:** A neuron receives input through dendrites, integrates it in the soma, and sends output down the axon toward another cell.
**Alt text:** Diagram of a neuron showing dendrites receiving input, a soma containing the nucleus, a long myelinated axon with nodes of Ranvier, and axon terminals forming synapses with the next cell.
**Files:** `fig3_1_neuron_anatomy_signal_direction.png`, `fig3_1_neuron_anatomy_signal_direction.svg`

## Figure 3.2 — Action potential: threshold, spike, and refractory period
**Caption:** An action potential occurs only when input crosses threshold. Stronger input does not make a bigger spike; it changes the likelihood or frequency of firing.
**Alt text:** Line graph of membrane voltage over time showing a failed subthreshold response and a full action potential after threshold is crossed, followed by a refractory period.
**Files:** `fig3_2_action_potential_threshold.png`, `fig3_2_action_potential_threshold.svg`

## Figure 3.3 — Common misconception: the action potential
**Caption:** A stronger stimulus does not produce a bigger action potential — it produces more of them. Firing rate, not spike size, encodes stimulus intensity.
**Alt text:** Myth/reality card contrasting "a stronger stimulus produces a bigger action potential" with the actual mechanism, plus a gun-trigger analogy.
**Files:** `fig3_3_misconception_action_potential_lecture.jpg` (rendered from lecture slide; no source .svg)

## Figure 3.4 — Neurotransmitter vs. hormone
**Caption:** Neurotransmitters usually act locally and quickly across synapses; hormones travel through the bloodstream and act more broadly over a longer timescale.
**Alt text:** Two-panel diagram comparing a neurotransmitter crossing a synapse to a hormone traveling through the bloodstream to distant target cells.
**Files:** `fig3_4_neurotransmitter_vs_hormone.png`, `fig3_4_neurotransmitter_vs_hormone.svg`

## Figure 3.5 — Agonists and antagonists: the lock and key
**Caption:** An agonist mimics a neurotransmitter and activates its receptor; an antagonist fits the receptor but blocks it. Antagonist does not mean harmful — naloxone is a lifesaving opioid antagonist.
**Alt text:** Lock-and-key diagram of agonist vs. antagonist action, plus a table of drugs (nicotine, morphine/heroin, naloxone, SSRIs), their target neurotransmitter, and effect.
**Files:** `fig3_5_agonist_antagonist_lecture.jpg` (rendered from lecture slide; no source .svg)

## Figure 3.6 — Stress response: fast wiring and slow hormones
**Caption:** The stress response has a fast neural component and a slower hormonal component. Together they prepare the body for action.
**Alt text:** Flowchart showing a threat activating both the sympathetic nervous system for rapid bodily changes and the adrenal cortisol pathway for slower, longer-lasting effects.
**Files:** `fig3_6_stress_response_fast_slow.png`, `fig3_6_stress_response_fast_slow.svg`

## Figure 3.7 — The autonomic nervous system as two opposing accelerators
**Caption:** Sympathetic and parasympathetic are not an on/off switch — both are always active, and what you feel reflects the balance between them.
**Alt text:** Side-by-side cards showing sympathetic ("fight-or-flight") and parasympathetic ("rest-and-digest") activity in the moment, with applied examples (panic attack, deep sleep, PTSD).
**Files:** `fig3_7_ans_balance_lecture.jpg` (rendered from lecture slide; no source .svg)

## Figure 3.8 — Brain research methods: what each method is good for
**Caption:** Brain imaging methods answer different questions. No method gives perfect timing, location, chemistry, and anatomy at once.
**Alt text:** Comparison table showing the strengths and limitations of EEG, fMRI, PET, and structural MRI.

## Figure 3.9 — Split-brain logic: real lateralization, not personality types
**Caption:** Split-brain studies show real hemispheric specialization under unusual surgical conditions, not left-brain/right-brain personality types.
