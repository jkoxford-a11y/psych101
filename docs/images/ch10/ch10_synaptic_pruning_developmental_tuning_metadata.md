## Instructional purpose

Explain synaptic pruning as **developmental tuning**, not damage or simple loss. The figure should help students understand that early development creates many possible synaptic connections, and repeated experience stabilizes some pathways while less-used connections quiet down. The mature network becomes faster, cheaper, and more specialized for the child’s history of stimulation.

## Caption

Early development creates more synaptic possibilities than the brain will keep. Repeated experience stabilizes frequently activated pathways and lets less-used connections quiet down or fade. The result is not damage; it is developmental tuning — a network that becomes faster, cheaper, and more specialized for the child’s history of stimulation. Different brain systems tune on different developmental clocks, creating sensitive windows when experience has especially strong effects.

## Alt text

Two-panel educational infographic about synaptic pruning. The left panel shows an early-development neural network with many possible synaptic connections, labeled high plasticity, low specialization, and overbuilt synaptic connections. A center arrow labeled repeated experience plus time points to a later-development neural network where selected pathways are thicker and clearer, while other connections are faint. Right-side labels say frequently activated pathways strengthen, less-used connections quiet down, and signaling becomes faster and cheaper. A bottom inset shows staggered sensitive windows for sensory systems, language sounds, and executive control.

## Accuracy caveats

- Pruning should not be read as brain damage.
- The figure should not imply that whole neurons, abilities, or brain regions are removed.
- "Optimized" means tuned to repeated experience; it does not always mean ideal. A system can be tuned to harsh, impoverished, or highly specific environments in ways that later become mismatched.
- The sensitive-window bars are schematic teaching aids, not precise empirical timing data.
- Real developmental timing varies by brain system, individual history, and environmental context.
