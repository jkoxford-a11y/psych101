## Core teaching point to preserve

The figure should not merely say:

> early overproduction → pruning → fewer connections

The stronger Chapter 10 teaching point is:

> Development starts with excess possible synaptic connections. Repeated experience stabilizes and strengthens the circuits that match the child’s history of stimulation, while less-used connections quiet down or fade. The result is not damage or simple loss. It is a network that becomes faster, cheaper, more specialized, and better tuned to the world the child repeatedly encounters.

This supports the broader chapter argument that development is not just maturation on a timer. Experience tunes plastic systems.

## Essential ideas

The final figure should preserve all three ideas, but not give them equal visual weight:

1. **Overproduction / saturation**
   - Early development creates many possible synaptic connections.
   - The network is highly plastic but not yet highly specialized.

2. **Experience-dependent tuning**
   - Repeated activation stabilizes and strengthens some pathways.
   - Less-used connections become quieter or fade.
   - This is the mechanism behind the phrase "use it or lose it," but the figure should avoid implying that unused abilities vanish completely.

3. **Sensitive windows**
   - Different brain systems are especially plastic at different developmental times.
   - This should appear only as a small inset or caption note unless it can be included without clutter.

## Bottom takeaway

Preferred text:

`Experience does not just remove connections. It tunes the brain toward the world it repeatedly encounters.`

Alternate shorter text if needed:

`Experience tunes the brain toward the world it repeatedly encounters.`

## Accuracy caveats

- Do not imply pruning is brain damage.
- Do not imply whole neurons or brain regions are removed.
- Do not make the later network look empty.
- Do not imply unused abilities vanish completely.
- Do not overstate optimization as always ideal. "Optimized" means tuned to repeated experience; a system can be tuned to harsh or impoverished conditions in ways that later become mismatched.
- Do not use fake data or precise timing unless the figure is explicitly based on a cited source.

## Caption draft

Early development creates more synaptic possibilities than the brain will keep. Repeated experience stabilizes frequently activated pathways and lets less-used connections quiet down or fade. The result is not damage; it is developmental tuning — a network that becomes faster, cheaper, and more specialized for the child’s history of stimulation. Different brain systems tune on different developmental clocks, creating sensitive windows when experience has especially strong effects.
