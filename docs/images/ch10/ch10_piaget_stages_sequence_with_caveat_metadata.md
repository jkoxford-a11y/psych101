## Core teaching idea

Piaget's four stages are a useful map of how children's thinking changes qualitatively across development, but they should not be treated as a rigid calendar. Later evidence supports the broad insight that children reason differently at different ages while complicating the timing and sharpness of the stage boundaries.

## On-image content

The figure shows four broad stages:

1. **Sensorimotor** — birth to 2 years; understanding the world through senses and actions; object permanence emerges.
2. **Preoperational** — 2 to 7 years; language and symbols; egocentric thinking; conservation errors.
3. **Concrete operational** — 7 to 11 years; logical thinking about concrete objects/events; conservation, seriation, classification.
4. **Formal operational** — 11 years and up; abstract, logical, hypothetical reasoning.

The bottom caveat notes that stages are a useful map, not a rigid calendar, and that children's abilities develop gradually with overlap between stages.

## Caption

Piaget proposed that children move through four broad stages of cognitive development, each marked by a different way of reasoning about the world. The sequence is still useful because it reminds us that children are not simply less-informed adults; at different ages, they often think according to different rules. The caveat is just as important: modern research shows that abilities emerge more gradually, with more overlap, and sometimes earlier than Piaget's original tasks suggested.
