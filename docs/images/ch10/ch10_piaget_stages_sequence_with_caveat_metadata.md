## Core teaching purpose

This figure gives students a compact map of Piaget's four cognitive stages while reducing the common misconception that stages are rigid stair steps. It preserves the canonical sequence and milestones but emphasizes approximate ages, fuzzy boundaries, and the modern update that early cognition is richer than Piaget assumed.

## Caption

Piaget's four-stage sequence remains a useful map of major changes in reasoning, especially the shift from sensorimotor action to symbolic thought, concrete logic, and abstract hypothetical reasoning. The boundaries should not be treated as hard walls: modern research shows that some abilities appear earlier than Piaget's tasks suggested, and early cognition begins with more structure than his original account assumed.

## Alt text

A horizontal four-card diagram summarizes Piaget's sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, and formal operational stages. Each card gives an approximate age range and a key milestone: object permanence, symbolic thought with conservation errors, concrete logical reasoning with conservation and reversibility, and abstract hypothetical reasoning. A bottom note says the sequence is useful, but stage ages are approximate, boundaries are fuzzy, and early cognition starts with more structure than Piaget assumed.
