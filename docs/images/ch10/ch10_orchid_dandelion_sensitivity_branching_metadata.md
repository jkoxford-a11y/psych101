## Instructional purpose

Correct the common misconception that an "orchid child" is simply fragile. The figure reframes orchid/dandelion as a differential-susceptibility story: the same heightened sensitivity to context can become risk in a poor-fit environment or strength in a supportive one.

## Caption

The orchid/dandelion metaphor is a simplified way to describe differential susceptibility. Some children are more sensitive to environmental context: that sensitivity can increase risk in chaotic or poorly matched environments, but it can also support especially strong growth in warm, predictable, supportive environments. The important point is not that children fall into rigid types. The point is that sensitivity amplifies context.

## Alt text

Branching educational infographic showing high sensitivity to context at the top center. One arrow leads left to a poor-fit or chaotic context with unpredictable stress, inconsistent support, and few safe routines, ending in increased risk. A second arrow leads right to a good-fit or supportive context with predictable routines, warm adult support, and room to recover and explore, ending in increased growth. A bottom takeaway states that sensitivity is not weakness; it amplifies the environment.

## Accuracy caveats

- The orchid/dandelion distinction is a teaching metaphor, not a rigid child-type classification.
- Real children and real environments are multidimensional; the figure simplifies context into a poor-fit/supportive contrast.
- The figure should not imply that caregiving alone determines developmental outcome. Temperament, broader ecology, culture, stress exposure, and support systems all matter.
