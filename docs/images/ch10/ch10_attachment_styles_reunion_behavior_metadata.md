## Core teaching idea

Ainsworth’s attachment classifications are distinguished most clearly by observable behavior during reunion. Caregiver sensitivity is meaningfully associated with attachment security, but the relationship is probabilistic rather than deterministic. Attachment classifications describe patterns within a caregiver-child relationship, not permanent traits of the child.

## On-image content

The figure compares four attachment classifications using observable reunion behavior:

- Secure: seeks contact or reassurance, is soothed, and returns to exploration.
- Anxious-ambivalent/resistant: seeks and resists contact and remains difficult to soothe.
- Anxious-avoidant: minimizes visible attachment behavior and avoids or ignores the caregiver.
- Disorganized: shows contradictory, disoriented, frozen, or frightened behavior.

The footer identifies these as patterns in a relationship rather than fixed child traits and presents caregiving sensitivity as a probabilistic correlate alongside temperament, stress, culture, and context.

## Caption

Attachment classifications in the Strange Situation are read most clearly during reunion. Secure infants seek comfort, are soothed, and return to exploration; anxious-ambivalent/resistant infants seek and resist contact and remain difficult to soothe; anxious-avoidant infants minimize visible attachment behavior or avoid the caregiver; disorganized infants show contradictory, disoriented, frozen, or frightened behavior. More sensitive and responsive caregiving is associated, on average, with secure attachment, while less consistent or rejecting care is associated more often with insecure patterns. These associations are probabilistic rather than deterministic, and temperament, stress, culture, and caregiving context also matter.
