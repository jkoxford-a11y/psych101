## Core teaching idea

Cross-sectional and longitudinal designs answer developmental questions in different ways. Cross-sectional studies compare different age groups at one moment, which is fast but vulnerable to cohort effects. Longitudinal studies follow the same people across time, which tracks real within-person change but is slower and vulnerable to attrition.

## Caption

Cross-sectional studies compare people of different ages at a single point in time. That makes them efficient, but age is tangled with cohort: older and younger participants also grew up in different historical circumstances. Longitudinal studies follow the same people across multiple years, so they better capture real within-person change, but they are slow, costly, and vulnerable to attrition.

## Accuracy notes

- Cross-sectional must show different people of different ages tested at one time.
- Longitudinal must show the same people followed across multiple testing waves.
- Cohort effects belong to cross-sectional designs.
- Attrition belongs to longitudinal designs.
- Do not frame one design as universally better; each solves one problem while creating another.
