## Core teaching idea

The zone of proximal development is the learning region between what a learner can already do independently and what is too difficult even with help. Scaffolding is effective when support is matched to that zone and gradually withdrawn as the learner gains competence.

## On-image content

The figure shows three learning zones arranged by increasing task difficulty:

1. **Can do alone** — tasks the learner can already complete independently.
2. **Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)** — tasks the learner cannot complete alone but can complete with guidance and support.
3. **Too hard** — tasks the learner cannot complete even with help.

A side panel shows scaffolding moving from strong support to moderate support, light support, and finally independence. The bottom message emphasizes that learning is strongest when a task is just beyond current ability but achievable with the right support.

## Caption

Vygotsky's zone of proximal development is the instructional sweet spot: the task is too difficult for the learner to complete alone, but achievable with guidance from a more capable partner. Scaffolding supplies that support, then fades as competence grows. The goal is not dependence on the helper; the goal is for the learner to become more capable after the support is removed.

## Accuracy notes

- The ZPD is not a fixed trait of the child; it shifts as the learner grows and as task demands change.
- Scaffolding should be calibrated to the learner's current level and withdrawn as competence increases.
- The figure should not imply that more help is always better; excessive help can skip the learner's own cognitive work.
- This figure pairs especially well with the chapter's AI-learning caveat: effective AI assistance should scaffold effort rather than replace it.
